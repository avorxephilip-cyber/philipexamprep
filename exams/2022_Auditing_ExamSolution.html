2022 Auditing Exam Solutions

Question 1

A. Written Representations

Explain three purposes of, and two procedures for, obtaining written representations.
(5 marks)

Solution to Q1A

Purposes:
  • To obtain management's acknowledgement of its responsibility for the fair presentation of financial statements.
  • To document management’s responses to inquiries where other evidence is limited or subjective.
  • To reduce the risk of misunderstandings between the auditor and management regarding management's assertions.
Procedures:
  • Inquire: Seek information from management regarding specific matters like subsequent events or related parties.
  • Letter of Representation: Obtain a formal, signed letter (Management Representation Letter) before the audit report is signed.
B. Goodname Plc — Acceptance of Appointment

Your firm has been approached by the board of directors of Goodname Plc who would like to engage your firm as the company's auditors for the next financial year, ending on 31 December 2014. The directors feel that their current auditors do not give them a good service and they will be asked to resign.

Required:
What ethical and statutory matters would you consider and what investigations would you undertake before your firm could accept appointment and be appointed as auditors of Goodname Plc?
(10 marks)

Solution to Q1B

  • Statutory Matters: Ensure the predecessor auditor has been properly removed or has resigned in accordance with the Companies Code.
  • Professional Clearance: Communicate with the retiring auditor to ask if there are professional reasons not to accept appointment.
  • Management Integrity: Investigate the reputation of the directors to ensure the firm is not facilitating illegal acts.
  • Competence: Assess whether the firm has the necessary skills, resources, and independence to perform the audit effectively.
C. Goodname Plc — Audit Committee

Goodname Plc is seeking to get listed on the stock market and is aware that subsequent to the stock exchange listing, it will need to establish an audit committee and has asked for some advice in relation to this.

Explain five benefits to Goodname Plc for establishing an audit committee.
(5 marks)

Solution to Q1C

  • Public Confidence: Enhances the credibility and objectivity of the company's financial reports.
  • Internal Audit Support: Provides a forum for the internal audit department to report findings independently.
  • External Auditor Independence: Provides a buffer between management and the external auditor, resolving disputes.
  • Assists Directors: Helps non-executive directors fulfill their financial reporting oversight responsibilities.
  • Communication: Facilitates better working relationships between management, internal auditors, and external auditors.

Question 2

External Auditor Reliance on Internal Audit Work

Your firm is the newly appointed external auditor to a large company that sells, maintains and leases office equipment and furniture to its customers. You have been asked to co-operate with internal audit to keep audit costs down. The company wants you to rely on some of the work already performed by internal audit.

The internal auditors provide the following services to the company:
- A cyclical audit of the operation of internal controls in the company's major functions (operations, finance, customer support and information services);
- A review of the structure of internal controls in each major function every four years;
- An annual review of the effectiveness of measures put in place by management to minimise the major risks facing the company.

During the current year, the company has gone through a major internal restructuring in its information services function and the internal auditors have been closely involved in the preparation of plans for restructuring and in the related post-implementation review.

Required:
A. Explain the extent to which your firm will seek to rely on the work of internal auditors in each of the areas noted above. (6 marks)
B. Describe the information your firm will seek from the internal auditors in order for you to determine the extent of your reliance. (6 marks)
C. Describe the circumstances in which it would not be possible to rely on the work of the internal auditors. (4 marks)
D. Explain why it will be necessary for your firm to perform its own work on certain audit areas in addition to relying on the work performed by internal audit. (4 marks)

Solution to Q2

Based on the provided audit and internal review materials, here are the answers regarding external auditor reliance on internal audit work.

A. Explain the extent to which your firm will seek to rely on the work of internal auditors in each of the areas noted:
  • Cyclical audit of internal controls: Your firm can seek significant reliance here to reduce the extent of substantive testing of transactions and balances, provided the internal audit (IA) function is effective. The external auditor will pay particular attention to these activities as they bear directly on the scope and depth of external work.
  • Review of internal control structure (every four years): Reliance may be limited because external auditors require a current assessment for each annual audit. While the IA reviews provide background, the external auditor must still ascertain and evaluate the system for the specific year under audit.
  • Annual review of risk management effectiveness: Your firm can rely on this to assist in its own risk assessment process. IA’s role in evaluating and controlling risk provides valuable evidence on the overall control environment.
  • Information services restructuring and post-implementation review: Reliance here will be extremely limited or non-existent. Because the internal auditors were "closely involved in the preparation of plans," auditing the post-implementation review would constitute a self-review threat. They would essentially be checking their own design work, which impairs objectivity.
B. Describe the information your firm will seek from the internal auditors in order for you to determine the extent of your reliance:
  • Organizational Status: Details on to whom the internal auditors report (ideally the highest level of management or Audit Committee) and whether they are free from operating responsibilities.
  • Scope of Function: The nature and extent of the assignments they perform and whether management acts on their recommendations.
  • Technical Competence: Information on the staff's technical training, proficiency, experience, and professional qualifications.
  • Due Professional Care: Evidence that their work is properly planned, supervised, reviewed, and documented, including the existence of audit manuals and working papers.
  • Liaison and Reports: Access to relevant internal reports and their tentative plan for the period to coordinate timing and coverage.
C. Describe the circumstances in which it would not be possible to rely on the work of the internal auditors:
  • Lack of Objectivity: If the internal auditors lack independence from the areas they audit or if their reporting lines are compromised (e.g., reporting only to the Finance Director).
  • Lack of Technical Competence: If the internal audit staff do not possess adequate technical training or proficiency.
  • Lack of Due Professional Care: If the work is poorly planned, lacks supervision, or is not appropriately documented.
  • Self-Review Threats: When the internal auditors have been involved in designing or implementing the systems they are now reviewing.
  • Significant Risks: In areas of high risk of material misstatement where the external auditor's judgment must be primary.
D. Explain why it will be necessary for your firm to perform its own work on certain audit areas in addition to relying on the work performed by internal audit:
  • Sole Responsibility: The external auditor has sole responsibility for the audit opinion expressed; this responsibility is not reduced by any use made of internal audit work.
  • Independent Judgment: All judgments relating to the audit of financial statements—such as materiality levels and risk assessments—must be those of the external auditor.
  • Different Objectives: Internal audit serves management's requirements, while the external auditor’s primary concern is whether financial statements are free of material misstatement for shareholders.
  • Evidence Reliability: To confirm the adequacy of IA work, the external auditor must perform its own procedures, such as re-examining items already tested by IA to ensure the results are consistent.
  • Statutory Requirements: External auditors must comply with International Standards on Auditing (ISAs), which require them to obtain sufficient appropriate evidence directly.

Question 3

Praise Plc — Audit Evidence Procedures (ISA 500)

You are the auditor of Praise Plc, a limited liability company which manufactures and sells small cuddly toys by mail order. The company is managed by Mr Adomako and two assistants. Mr Adomako authorises important transactions such as wages and large orders; one assistant maintains the payables ledger and orders inventory and pays suppliers; the other assistant receives customer orders and despatches cuddly toys. Due to other business commitments, Mr Adomako only visits the office once a week.

At any time, about 100 different types of cuddly toys are available for sale. All sales are made cash with order — there are no receivables. Customers pay using credit cards and occasionally by sending cash. Turnover is approximately GHS100 million.

You are planning the audit of Praise Plc and are considering using some of the procedures for gathering audit evidence recommended by ISA 500 as follows:
- Analytical Procedures
- Inquiry
- Inspection
- Observation
- Recalculation

Required:
A. For each of the above procedures:
- Explain its use in gathering audit evidence. (5 marks)
- Describe one example for the audit of Praise Plc. (5 marks)

B. Discuss the suitability of each procedure for Praise Plc, explaining the limitations of each. (10 marks)

Solution to Q3

A. Audit Evidence Procedures: Use and Examples
Procedure Explanation of Use in Gathering Evidence Example for Praise Plc Audit
Analytical Procedures Evaluation of financial information made by a study of plausible relationships among both financial and non-financial data, including the investigation of significant fluctuations that deviate from predicted amounts. Comparing the gross profit margin of cuddly toy sales this year with the prior year to identify any unusual variances in the mail-order operations.
Inquiry Seeking information from knowledgeable persons, financial or non-financial, inside or outside the entity. Range from formal written to informal oral inquiries. Interviewing the assistant in charge of despatches to understand how they verify that a customer has paid before shipping the toy.
Inspection Examining records or documents (internal or external) in paper or electronic form, or physically examining tangible assets. Examining credit card settlement reports from the bank to verify that sales recorded in the system match actual cash received.
Observation Looking at a process or procedure being performed by others, such as observing the performance of control activities. Watching the assistant process a mail order to see if they check the credit card authorization before initiating the despatch.
Recalculation Checking the mathematical accuracy of documents or records. Can be performed manually or through IT. Re-adding the totals of the daily sales reports for a sample of days to ensure they were correctly posted to the ledger.
B. Suitability and Limitations for Praise Plc

1. Analytical Procedures

  • Suitability: Highly suitable because sales are "cash with order," making relationships between turnover and bank receipts highly predictable.
  • Limitations: Reliance depends on the reliability of the underlying data. If the bookkeeping is poorly maintained by the assistants, the ratios may be misleading.

2. Inquiry

  • Suitability: Essential for understanding the informal control environment of a small entity managed by only three people.
  • Limitations: Inquiry alone is insufficient to provide evidence of the operating effectiveness of controls. Information from management is persuasive rather than conclusive and requires corroboration.

3. Inspection

  • Suitability: Highly suitable for verifying the 100 types of cuddly toys in inventory and verifying the trail of credit card payments.
  • Limitations: Inspecting records provides evidence of varying degrees of reliability. Physical inspection of inventory confirms existence but does not necessarily provide evidence of valuation or ownership (rights/obligations).

4. Observation

  • Suitability: Useful for checking if the two assistants are actually performing their separate roles as described.
  • Limitations: It is limited to the specific point in time the auditor is present. Furthermore, the act of being observed may cause the assistants to perform their duties more diligently than they do normally.

5. Recalculation

  • Suitability: Excellent for verifying that turnover (GHS100 million) is correctly aggregated from many small mail-order transactions.
  • Limitations: While it confirms mathematical accuracy, it does not confirm the validity or existence of the original transactions. If an assistant creates a fake sale entry, recalculating it will still result in an "accurate" but incorrect total.

Question 4

A. Adom Plc — Sales System Deficiencies (Seed Division)

Adom Plc supplies a wide range of garden and agricultural products to trade and domestic customers. The company has 10 divisions, with each division specialising in the sale of specific products, for example, seeds, garden furniture, agricultural fertilizers, etc. The company has an internal audit department which provides audit reports to the audit committee on each division on a rotational basis.

Products in the seed division are offered for sale to domestic customers via an internet site. Customers review the product list on the internet and place orders for packets of seeds using specific product codes, along with their credit card details, onto Adom Plc's secure server. Order quantities are normally between one and three packets for each type of seed. Order details are transferred manually onto the company's internal inventory control and sales system, and a two-part packing list is printed in the seed warehouse.

Each order and packing list is given a random alphabetical code based on the name of the employee inputting the order, the date, and the products being ordered.

In the seed warehouse, the packages of seeds for each order are taken from specific bins and dispatched to the customer with one copy of the packing list. The second copy of the packing list is sent to the accounts department where the inventory and sales computer is updated to show that the order has been dispatched. The customer's credit card is then charged by the inventory control and sales computer. Bad debts are currently 3% of total sales.

Finally, the computer system checks that for each charge made to a customer's credit card account, the order details are on file to prove that the charge was made correctly. The order file is marked as completed confirming that the order has been dispatched and payment obtained.

Required:
In respect of sales in the seeds division of Adom Plc, prepare a report to be sent to the audit committee of Adom Plc which:
- Identifies and explains four deficiencies in that sales system;
- Explains the possible effect of each deficiency; and
- Provides a recommendation to alleviate each deficiency.
(12 marks)

Solution to Q4A

Report identifying system deficiencies, their effects, and recommended improvements for the seed division of Adom Plc.

To: Audit Committee of Adom Plc
From: Internal Audit Department
Date: [Current Date]
Subject: Internal Control Report – Sales System Deficiencies (Seed Division)

Introduction
At the conclusion of the internal control assessment of the seed division, we have identified several deficiencies in the sales system. This report details these weaknesses, explains their possible effects on the company’s financial standing and operations, and provides recommendations for improvement.

Report on Sales System Deficiencies

Identified Deficiency Possible Effect Recommendation
Manual Transfer of Order Details: Customer order details are transferred manually from the internet site to the internal inventory control and sales system. Manual data entry is highly susceptible to human error, such as mistyped product codes or quantities. This could lead to incorrect shipments or inventory records. The internet order site should be integrated directly with the internal inventory and sales system to automate data transfer and eliminate manual entry errors.
Random Alphabetical Coding: Orders and packing lists are assigned random alphabetical codes based on employee names and dates rather than sequential numerical codes. The lack of sequential numbering makes it impossible to ensure the completeness of orders. It is difficult to identify if an order has been lost or skipped in the process. All packing lists and orders should be pre-numbered sequentially by the system. This allows for audits of numerical sequences to ensure all orders are processed.
Delayed Credit Card Charging: The customer’s credit card is only charged after the packing list is sent from the warehouse to the accounts department following dispatch. Since goods are shipped before payment is confirmed, there is a high risk of non-payment if a card is declined. This likely contributes to the current 3% bad debt rate. The system should authorize and charge credit cards at the point of order placement on the secure server. Orders should only be sent to the warehouse once payment is confirmed.
Inadequate Segregation of Duties: The accounts department updates the inventory computer for dispatches and also initiates the credit card charges through the same system. A lack of segregation between recording and custodial/financial functions increases the risk of fraud. An employee could potentially suppress a sale record after dispatch. Separate the functions of inventory record updates and the processing of financial transactions. Different personnel or system modules should handle these distinct tasks.

Conclusion
Implementing these recommendations will significantly reduce the risk of material misstatement in the financial statements and improve the overall efficiency of the seed division's operations. We would be pleased to discuss these points with management at your convenience.

Signed,
Audit Team

B. Mensah & Co / Mensah Financial Services Plc — Ethical Threats

You are the audit manager of Mensah & Co, a firm of chartered accountants, and you are planning the audit of Mensah Financial Services Plc (Mensah), which specialises in the provision of loans and financial advice to individuals and companies. Mensah & Co has audited Mensah for many years. The directors are planning to list Mensah on a stock exchange within the next few months and have asked if the engagement partner can attend the meetings with potential investors. In addition, as the finance director of Mensah is likely to be quite busy with the listing, he has asked if Mensah & Co can produce the financial statements for the current year.

During the year, the assistant finance director of Mensah left and joined Mensah & Co as a partner. It has been suggested that due to his familiarity with Mensah, he should be appointed to provide an independent partner review for the audit. Once Mensah obtains its stock exchange listing, it will require several assignments to be undertaken, for example, obtaining advice about corporate governance best practice. Mensah & Co is very keen to be appointed to these engagements. However, Mensah has implied that in order to gain this work, Mensah & Co needs to complete the external audit quickly and with minimal questions/issues.

The finance director has informed you that once the stock exchange listing has been completed, he would like the engagement team to attend a weekend away at a luxury hotel with his team, as a thank you for all their hard work. In addition, he has offered a senior member of the engagement team a short-term loan at a significantly reduced interest rate.

Required:
- Explain four ethical threats which may affect the independence of Mensah & Co's audit of Mensah; and
- For each threat, explain how it might be mitigated.
(8 marks)

Solution to Q4B

  • Self-Review Threat: The request to produce financial statements that Mensah & Co will later audit.
    Mitigation: The firm should refuse to prepare financial statements for a public interest entity client. If allowed, separate teams must be used.
  • Familiarity Threat: A former assistant finance director of the client is now a partner at the audit firm.
    Mitigation: This partner must not be involved in the audit of Mensah for at least two years after leaving the company.
  • Intimidation Threat: The client implied that future high-value assignments depend on a "quick audit with minimal issues".
    Mitigation: Clearly communicate that professional standards cannot be compromised. Consider declining the audit if pressure persists.
  • Self-Interest Threat (Gifts): The offer of a luxury weekend away and a low-interest loan to an engagement team member.
    Mitigation: The firm must prohibit acceptance of gifts or loans from audit clients unless they are clearly insignificant in value.
