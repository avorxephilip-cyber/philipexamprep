2023B EXAMINATION — SECTION B

Case Summary: Pandora was founded in 1999 by Tim Westergren, a musician and film composer. The company raised $1 million, went through financial difficulties (nearly broke for 2.5 years), pitched to over 300 venture capitalists before one said yes, and shifted its business model to Internet radio streaming. Pandora went public in 2011 on the NYSE and now has over 250 million registered users.

Part I: What Type of Entrepreneur is Tim Westergren? [1 mark]
Tim Westergren is a Novice Entrepreneur — an individual who is in his first major business venture. However, based on his persistence and risk-taking, he also demonstrates characteristics of an Opportunist Entrepreneur — one who takes advantage of market opportunities (the gap in personalized Internet radio) and transforms them into a business venture.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 1 — Types of Entrepreneurs, pp.4–5)
Part II: Mention Three Entrepreneurial Attributes that Helped Tim Westergren Succeed [3 marks]
Based on the case study and entrepreneurial personality traits from the textbook:
Need for Achievement (n Ach): Westergren demonstrated a strong desire to solve problems on his own and achieve goals through his own efforts. Despite Pandora going essentially broke for 2.5 years, he persisted — a hallmark of high n Ach entrepreneurs.
Internal Locus of Control: Westergren believed the future was his to control through his own effort. Rather than accepting failure as fate, he continued pitching to over 300 venture capitalists, demonstrating the belief that his own actions could change the outcome.
Risk-Taking Propensity: Westergren willingly accepted financial risk — the company went through periods of near-insolvency — yet continued to push the venture forward. Risk-taking propensity is closely related to the need for achievement and is a core entrepreneurial trait.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 3 — The Entrepreneurial Process (Psychological Approach), pp.14–15)
Part III: Describe One Technique Westergren Adopted in Generating Ideas to Solve the Anger Expressed by Parents [2 marks]
The technique Westergren adopted is Brainstorming. He had open sessions with employees and also experts to discuss the business ideas to solve the anger expressed by parents. Brainstorming is the method of bringing people together and asking them to suggest alternatives to a problem or to generate new product ideas — it is exactly this approach (collective discussion with employees and experts) that Westergren used to respond to the wide age range complaint.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 2 — Finding a Sound Idea for a Business, pp.36–37)
Part IV: Outline Four Rules to Be Observed in Applying Brainstorming [8 marks]
The technique of brainstorming includes a strict series of rules. The four basic rules are:
No Criticism Allowed: No criticism is allowed by anyone in the group — no negative comments. The purpose is to encourage free expression of ideas without fear of judgment. When participants feel safe to suggest even unconventional ideas, the creative process flourishes.
State Any Ideas Regardless of How Extreme: Participants should state any ideas regardless of how extreme or outlandish they may seem. Wild ideas often spark creative connections and can lead to genuinely innovative solutions when refined.
Ideas Belong to the Group: Participants should approach each idea as belonging to the group and build upon the ideas of others. This collaborative ownership fosters a supportive atmosphere and encourages participants to develop and improve each other's contributions.
Generate, Do Not Evaluate Ideas: The goal of the brainstorming session is to generate as many ideas as possible, not to evaluate them in the moment. Evaluation comes after the session. The brainstorming session should be fun, not work-oriented, with no expert dominating or inhibiting the discussion.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 2 — Finding a Sound Idea for a Business, pp.36–37)
Part V: Evaluate Three Reasons Why Westergren Developed a Prototype to Show Potential Clients [6 marks]
Westergren built a prototype and had a product to show to potential customers. Three reasons why developing a prototype was important:
To Attract Investment and Financing: A prototype provides tangible evidence that the business concept is technically feasible. For Pandora, having a working prototype gave venture capitalists something concrete to evaluate, improving the credibility of the business proposal. According to the feasibility criteria, it is certainly impressive to financial backers when a venture can demonstrate a ready-to-go or near-complete product.
To Test and Validate the Business Concept: A prototype allows the entrepreneur to identify and address technical or design flaws before full launch. Independent testing of the prototype helps determine whether the product performs as expected under normal operating conditions — a key element of technical feasibility assessment.
To Build Credibility and Customer Confidence: Showing a working prototype to potential customers demonstrates seriousness and capability. It reduces skepticism and helps potential clients visualize the product's value. For Pandora, this was critical in convincing investors and early adopters that the Internet radio model was viable and scalable.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 3 — New Business Assessment, pp.47–48)
2023B — QUESTION 2
Part I: Differentiate Between Bank Overdraft and Bank Loan (Two Points Each) [4 marks]
Bank Overdraft: (i) An overdraft allows the borrower to overdraw their account up to a specified limit reviewed normally on an annual basis — giving flexibility to alter financing requirements from day to day. (ii) Interest is calculated on the daily outstanding balance, meaning no interest is paid on any unutilized portion; the facility is repayable on demand.
Bank Loan: (i) A bank loan is a fixed amount advanced for a specific period with a pre-agreed repayment schedule (principal plus interest at agreed intervals) — it is not repayable on demand like an overdraft. (ii) Interest is charged on the full loan amount from the date of disbursement regardless of how much of the funds have been utilized, making it suitable for specific capital expenditures rather than day-to-day working capital.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 6, Session 3 — Short Term Business Finance, pp.177–178)
Part II: Discuss Five Long-Term Sources of Finance Available to Small and Medium Enterprises [10 marks]
(See full answers already covered in 2020A Q2c — shares, debentures, leasing, mortgage, and venture capital. Summary below:)
Shares (Equity and Preference): The most important source for raising long-term capital. Equity shareholders bear risk and enjoy residual dividends; preference shareholders have preferential claims on dividends and repayment of capital.
Debentures: Instruments acknowledging long-term loans. Debenture holders receive fixed interest before shareholders — even if the company makes a loss. Usually secured against specific assets.
Asset Leasing: A lease agreement between a lessor and lessee allowing use of assets (plant, machinery, vehicles) without large up-front capital. Operating leases and finance leases are the two forms.
Mortgage: A long-term secured loan, usually on property or fixed assets. If the firm fails to repay, the lender can repossess and sell the asset to recover the money.
Venture Capital: Private equity capital typically provided by outside investors to new and growing businesses in exchange for shares. Venture capitalists may also provide managerial and technical expertise, as Pandora experienced.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 6, Session 2 — Long Term Business Finance, pp.171–174)
Part III: State and Explain Three Factors for Assessing Creditworthiness of Owner-Managers [6 marks]
(Three of the five factors from 2023 Q4B above apply here. Summary:)
Character: The reputation, honesty and reliability of the owner-manager. A business owner who has consistently honoured obligations will be viewed favourably by lenders.
Capacity: The business sense, ability to pay, level of experience, and business history of the owner-manager. This assesses whether the manager has the skill to generate revenue sufficient to service and repay the debt.
Guarantees: The lender may require the owner-manager to pledge assets to guarantee the loan. Assets such as land, buildings, or equipment serve as collateral, providing security for the lender in case of default.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 6, Session 3 — Short Term Business Finance, p.177)
2023B — QUESTION 3: Business Information
Part I: Differentiate Between 'Proprietary' and 'Subscription' Secondary Sources of Business Information [4 marks]
Proprietary Information: Proprietary information is owned by a particular company or institution but may be available in exchange for a fee. The owner controls who can access it and under what conditions, making it more exclusive than public information. Examples include market research reports sold by private companies.
Subscription Information: Subscription information is a halfway house between public and proprietary — it is owned but is routinely made available and updated for a limited number of qualified subscribers. Unlike proprietary information, which may require individual purchase arrangements, subscription services provide ongoing, regularly updated access to those who pay periodic fees. Examples include trade journals, industry databases, and news wire services.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 3 — Business Informational Resources, p.115)
Part II: Discuss Three Advantages of Business Information [6 marks]
Information Reduces Risk:
Information has a cost — either a direct cost (such as commissioned market research) or a cost in time and effort. However, as with any investment, gathering information repays by reducing the risk of uninformed decisions. Entrepreneurs who base decisions on good information are less likely to make costly mistakes.
Records Enable Sound Management:
Records are a specialized form of information — produced consciously or as by-products of business activities and retained because of their value. Sound records management ensures that the integrity of records is preserved for as long as they are required, whether for their business value, as part of corporate memory, or to meet legal, fiscal, or accountability requirements.
Enables Effective Communication:
Business information enables firms to overcome some of the barriers to ineffective communication — including physical, perceptual, emotional, cultural, language, and interpersonal barriers. When information is disseminated to all members of the organisation, it ensures that decisions are made in the best interest of the business.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 3 — Business Informational Resources, p.116)
Part III: Discuss Five Types of Business Information Available to the Small Business Owner [10 marks]
(See related answer in 2020A Q3b for four types. The five types below include internal types as well:)
Market Information: Information on market size, market growth, consumer spending power, consumer needs and behaviour, and market share. Used for new product development, market planning, advertising decisions, and export planning.
Competitor Information: Information on where competitors are, what they are doing, and their performance. Accurate competitor information is difficult to collect but essential for strategic positioning and maintaining competitive advantage.
Supplier Information: Focuses on factors such as cost, reliability, quality, and speed of delivery. Helps businesses plan production effectively and assess whether necessary raw materials can be sourced to meet lead times.
Production Information: An internal type of information focused on production efficiency, throughput, cost, wastage, quality, and machine efficiency. It tells a firm whether production can deliver the volume and quality that marketing and sales have promised to customers.
Internal Financial Information: Describes what is usually called the 'bottom line' — includes balance sheet information on profit and loss, assets and liabilities, and a wide range of financial ratios. This information provides the basic picture of the company's financial health and profitability.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 3 — Business Informational Resources, pp.113–115)
