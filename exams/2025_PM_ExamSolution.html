2025 EXAMINATION — SECTION B

Question 1

The government of Ghana as part of its effort to boost health care in the country has embarked upon Agenda 111. The project is aimed at building hospitals in each district of Ghana. As a project consultant, you have been appointed to analyse the risk associated with the project and advise the government on the following:
Part a. i.. Examine five internal and external risks that might affect the project [(10 marks)]
Five Internal Risks:
  • Impossible Schedule Commitments: Unrealistic timelines for completing hospitals across all districts simultaneously — set without adequate feasibility analysis — will inevitably lead to construction delays.
  • Organization's Resources Overcommitted: Managing hundreds of hospital construction sites simultaneously may stretch the government's procurement capacity, financial resources, and human resources beyond manageable limits.
  • Conflicting Project Priorities: Multiple competing government priorities and budget allocations may starve individual hospital projects of timely funding, causing stagnation.
  • No Functional Input in the Planning Phase: If district health authorities and communities are excluded from planning, critical hospital operational requirements may be missed, requiring expensive redesigns during execution.
  • Unrealistic Planning and Scheduling: Overly optimistic construction timelines that do not account for material lead times, site-specific conditions, and seasonal weather factors lead to inherent delays.
Five External Risks:
  • Natural Disasters: Floods, earthquakes, or excess rainfall can simultaneously disrupt construction sites across multiple regions.
  • Inflation and Currency Rate Fluctuations: Rising material costs (steel, cement, glass) and currency depreciation can cause significant budget overruns that require supplementary funding and negotiation delays.
  • Unforeseen Regulatory Requirements: New environmental, health, or safety regulations introduced after project commencement can require costly design revisions and construction stoppages.
  • Social/Community Opposition: Local communities may resist hospital siting decisions through protests, litigation, or non-cooperation, causing delays to land acquisition and construction.
  • Political Risk: Changes in government policy, budget reallocations, or a change in administration could result in project starvation — reduced funding or outright cancellation of individual sites.
(Source: Unit 6, Session 1 — Sources of Project Risk (Internal and External), pp.151–152)
Part a. ii.. Evaluate two techniques that can be used to identify project risks. [(6 marks)]
  • Brainstorming: A preferred risk identification method due to its flexibility and capability to generate a wide and diverse range of risks when appropriately structured. The technique requires: no criticism of any idea; free expression regardless of how extreme; treating all ideas as belonging to the group and building on them; and generating rather than evaluating ideas. Evaluation: Highly effective for diverse project teams but requires skilled facilitation to maintain productivity and focus.
  • Expert Judgement: Involves seeking input from individuals with specialized knowledge or experience in the specific type of project or risk domain. This leverages past experience and deep technical knowledge to identify risks invisible to less experienced team members. Evaluation: Highly credible and targeted — but limited by the expert's scope of knowledge, can be expensive, and may introduce personal bias if not checked against other techniques.
(Source: Unit 6, Session 2 — Risk Identification Methods, pp.153–154)
Part b.. The project agenda 111 has provided you with the following data on risk analysis:
Risk Type Probability (P) Severity (S)
Financial Risk 0.1 0.6
Political Risk 0.6 0.4
Technology Risk 0.3 0.8
Part i.. Calculate the threshold of these risks [(2 marks)]
Using the formula: Risk (R) = Probability (P) × Severity (S)

  • Financial Risk: R = 0.1 × 0.6 = 0.06
  • Political Risk: R = 0.6 × 0.4 = 0.24
  • Technology Risk: R = 0.3 × 0.8 = 0.24
(Source: Unit 6, Session 3 — Risk Scoring Matrix: R = P × S, p.157)
Part ii.. Based on the answer in b(i) classify the risk into high, low and medium [(2 marks)]
  • Financial Risk (R = 0.06): LOW — low probability and moderate severity
  • Political Risk (R = 0.24): MEDIUM — high probability but moderate severity
  • Technology Risk (R = 0.24): MEDIUM — moderate probability but high severity
(Source: Unit 6, Session 3 — Risk Scoring Matrix and Classification, p.157)

Question 2

The government of Ghana has promised to construct a STEM school in your community. As a project consultant, have been asked to identify the stakeholders for the project and draw a plan for their engagement. Required:
Part a. i.. Using the power interest grid, identify two stakeholders for each of the quadrants and justify their selection. [(6 marks)]
High Power / High Interest — Manage Closely: (1) Ministry of Education — has the authority to approve and fund the project AND a high direct interest in educational outcomes and policy compliance. (2) District Assembly — has political power over land allocation and construction permits AND a direct interest in the community development impact of the school.
High Power / Low Interest — Keep Satisfied: (1) Ghana Revenue Authority — has regulatory authority over tax compliance matters but limited day-to-day interest in the school's design or programme. (2) Environmental Protection Agency — has authority to approve environmental impact assessments but low interest in ongoing construction activities.
Low Power / High Interest — Keep Informed: (1) Parents and Community Members — have a strong interest in the school's design, curriculum, and quality but no formal decision-making authority. (2) Future Teachers/Staff — highly interested in facility quality and working conditions but no authority over project decisions.
Low Power / Low Interest — Monitor: (1) General Media — may report on the project but have little sustained interest or power unless a major issue arises. (2) Neighbouring Businesses — low formal interest and no power unless construction activities directly disrupt their operations.
(Source: Unit 5, Session 5 — Power/Interest Grid, pp.139–141)
Part a. ii.. Explain how each of the stakeholders identified in each quadrant is to be managed. Your explanation should be supported with relevant examples. [(6 marks)]
Manage Closely (High Power/High Interest): Engage frequently through regular meetings, progress reports, and direct involvement in key decisions. Example: Brief the Ministry of Education and District Assembly monthly on project milestones, budget utilization, and any emerging risks requiring decisions.
Keep Satisfied (High Power/Low Interest): Keep informed of major milestones and compliance matters without overwhelming them with operational detail. Example: Provide GRA with quarterly tax compliance reports and EPA with environmental monitoring updates without including them in weekly construction meetings.
Keep Informed (Low Power/High Interest): Provide regular, accessible updates through community forums, newsletters, or social media. Collect their feedback and reflect community preferences in design decisions. Example: Hold monthly community town halls to update parents on construction progress and gather input on school facilities and safety measures.
Monitor (Low Power/Low Interest): Monitor periodically but invest minimal management resources. Update the strategy if their power or interest levels change. Example: Review media coverage weekly to catch any negative reporting early; reach out to neighbouring businesses only if construction creates access or noise issues.
(Source: Unit 5, Session 5 — Plan Stakeholder Engagement, pp.140–141)
Part b.. Examine four outputs that would be achieved if the project managers of Agenda 111 perform integrated change control successfully. [(8 marks)]
  • Approved Change Requests: All change requests are formally processed and those approved are documented and implemented through the Direct and Manage Project Work process. This gives legitimacy to all modifications to the Agenda 111 hospital designs or specifications.
  • Change Log: Documents all changes — approved and rejected — that occur during the project, including their impact on time, cost, and risk. Communicated to appropriate stakeholders. For Agenda 111, this creates an audit trail of all design modifications across all 111 hospital sites.
  • Project Management Plan Updates: Approved changes require updates to subsidiary plans and baselines (scope, schedule, cost). These updates reflect the new authorized direction and ensure the PMP remains current and actionable.
  • Project Documents Updates: All project documents subject to formal change control — risk registers, requirements documentation, stakeholder registers — are updated to reflect the impact of approved changes, maintaining accuracy across all project records.
(Source: Unit 5, Session 3 — Perform Integrated Change Control, pp.130–131)

Part a.. Examine four importance of project scope management [(6 marks)]
(Any four from the six listed in the textbook — Shared Understanding, Verification of Work, Prevents Scope Bloat, Defines Exclusions, Establishes Control Mechanisms, Enables Accurate Estimation. See full answers under 2019 Question 1c.)
Part b.. Examine four outputs achieved when a project manager validates the scope of the project. [(4 marks)]
  • Accepted Deliverables: Deliverables that meet the defined acceptance criteria are formally signed off by the customer or sponsor, confirming they are accepted as complete.
  • Change Requests: Deliverables not meeting acceptance criteria may trigger change requests for defect repair or additional rework to bring them to the required standard.
  • Work Performance Information: Information on scope completion progress — which deliverables have been accepted and which rejected — is documented and communicated to stakeholders.
  • Project Documents Updates: Requirements documentation and the requirements traceability matrix are updated to reflect what has been validated and what remains outstanding.
(Source: Unit 3, Session 3 — Validate Scope, p.57 area)
Part c.. Explain five guidelines that plan schedule management provides in project management [(10 marks)]
  • Scheduling Methodology: Establishes the scheduling model to be used throughout the project — e.g., Critical Path Method (CPM), Precedence Diagramming Method — ensuring all team members use a consistent approach.
  • Level of Accuracy: Defines the acceptable range for activity duration estimates, enabling consistent and realistic scheduling across all project phases.
  • Units of Measure: Defines measurement units for resources (e.g., staff hours, staff days) and time (weeks, months) for consistent reporting and comparison.
  • Control Thresholds: Sets variance thresholds for monitoring schedule performance — e.g., ±5% from baseline — that trigger corrective action when exceeded, providing an early warning system.
  • Rules for Performance Measurement: Establishes how Earned Value Management (EVM) data will be reported and recorded, ensuring consistent, comparable performance measurement throughout the project lifecycle.
(Source: Unit 3, Session 5 — Project Time Management Activities, p.71 area)

Question 4

Part a.. Using relevant examples, explain what is project delay [(2 marks)]
Project delay is defined as the inability of a project team to complete all activities stipulated under a particular project within the estimated duration. Project delays manifest as a breach of the time constraint of a project, which could affect the cost, scope, and quality either positively or negatively. A project delay can be a deliberate action or an indeliberate action.

Example: A contractor failing to complete an Agenda 111 hospital within the three-year timeline due to material supply disruptions — an indeliberate delay beyond immediate control.
(Source: Unit 1, Session 6 — Project Delays, p.23)
Part b.. Examine the types of project delays using relevant examples [(8 marks)]
  • Critical vs Non-Critical Delays: A critical delay extends the overall project duration. Example: Delay in hospital foundation work.
  • Excusable vs Non-Excusable Delays: Excusable delays are outside the team's control (e.g., floods). Non-excusable are due to internal failures.
  • Concurrent Delays: Multiple delay events affecting the project simultaneously.
  • Compensable vs Non-Compensable Delays: Compensable delays entitle the team to time and cost compensation (e.g., client-requested design changes).
(For full detailed definitions with examples, see 2021 Question 1a.)
Part c.. Analyse five causes of project delays. Your answer must reflect relevant examples. [(10 marks)]
  • Design Errors: Errors requiring rework or demolition.
  • Scope Change: Major changes mid-execution impacting all baselines.
  • Inappropriate Procurement: Vague contracts leading to disputes.
  • Complexity: Large projects vulnerable to inflation and price spikes.
  • Subcontractor Delays: Specialized partners failing to deliver on time.
(For full detailed analysis with examples, see 2021 Question 1b.)
