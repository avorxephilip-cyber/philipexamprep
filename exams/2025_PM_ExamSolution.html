2025 EXAMINATION — SECTION B

Question 1

The government of Ghana as part of its effort to boost health care in the country has embarked upon Agenda 111. The project is aimed at building hospitals in each district of Ghana. As a project consultant, you have been appointed to analyse the risk associated with the project and advise the government on the following:
Part a. i.. Examine five internal and external risks that might affect the project [(10 marks)]
Five Internal Risks:
  • Impossible Schedule Commitments: Unrealistic timelines set without feasibility analysis lead to inevitable construction delays.
  • Organization's Resources Overcommitted: Managing 111 sites simultaneously stretches procurement and financial capacity.
  • Conflicting Project Priorities: Competing government priorities may starve individual projects of timely funding.
  • No Functional Input in Planning: Excluding district health authorities leads to missing operational requirements.
  • Unrealistic Planning: Over-optimism regarding material lead times and weather factors.
Five External Risks:
  • Natural Disasters: Floods or excess rainfall disrupting construction across regions.
  • Inflation and Currency Fluctuations: Rising material costs (steel, cement) causing significant budget overruns.
  • Unforeseen Regulatory Requirements: New environmental or safety regulations introduced mid-project.
  • Social/Community Opposition: Resistance to siting decisions through protests or litigation.
  • Political Risk: Budget reallocations or changes in administration resulting in project starvation.
📖 Source: Unit 6, Session 1 — Sources of Project Risk, pp.151–152
Part a. ii.. Evaluate two techniques that can be used to identify project risks. [(6 marks)]
  • Brainstorming: Highly flexible method to generate a wide range of risks. Evaluation: Effective for diverse teams but requires skilled facilitation to avoid evaluation during generation.
  • Expert Judgement: Leveraging individuals with specialized knowledge. Evaluation: Highly credible and targeted but limited by the expert's scope and potential personal bias.
📖 Source: Unit 6, Session 2 — Risk Identification Methods, pp.153–154

Question 2

Part a. i.. Using the power interest grid, identify two stakeholders for each quadrant. [(6 marks)]
  • High Power/High Interest (Manage Closely): Ministry of Education (authority/funding) and District Assembly (permits/community interest).
  • High Power/Low Interest (Keep Satisfied): GRA (tax regulatory authority) and EPA (environmental regulatory authority).
  • Low Power/High Interest (Keep Informed): Parents/Community (direct beneficiaries) and Future Staff (highly interested in working conditions).
  • Low Power/Low Interest (Monitor): General Media and Neighbouring Businesses.
Part a. ii.. Explain how each of the stakeholders identified is to be managed. [(6 marks)]
  • Manage Closely: Frequent engagement via regular meetings and direct involvement in decisions. Example: Monthly briefs to the Ministry.
  • Keep Satisfied: Informed of major milestones without operational detail. Example: Quarterly tax compliance reports for GRA.
  • Keep Informed: Regular updates through community forums. Example: Monthly town halls for parents.
  • Monitor: Periodic monitoring with minimal resources. Example: Weekly media coverage reviews.
Part b.. Examine four outputs of integrated change control. [(8 marks)]
  • Approved Change Requests: Formally processed and documented modifications to designs or specifications.
  • Change Log: An audit trail of all approved and rejected changes across all sites.
  • PMP Updates: Updates to baselines (scope, schedule, cost) reflecting new authorized directions.
  • Project Document Updates: Adjustments to risk registers, requirements, and stakeholder lists.

Question 3

Part a.. Examine four importance of project scope management [(6 marks)]
  • Shared Understanding: Stakeholders and teams agree on how goals will be met.
  • Verification of Work: Sets procedures for formal acceptance of deliverables.
  • Prevents Scope Bloat: Controls unruly requirements lists and unauthorized additions.
  • Defines Exclusions: Clearly states what is NOT part of the project to prevent execution errors.
📖 Source: Unit 3, Session 1 — Importance of Project Scope Management, pp.55–56
Part b.. Examine four outputs achieved when a project manager validates the scope. [(4 marks)]
  • Accepted Deliverables: Formally signed-off deliverables by customer/sponsor.
  • Change Requests: Rework requests for deliverables not meeting criteria.
  • Work Performance Information: Documentation of which deliverables were accepted or rejected.
  • Project Document Updates: Updating the requirements traceability matrix.
Part c.. Explain five guidelines that plan schedule management provides. [(10 marks)]
  • Scheduling Methodology: Establishes the model (e.g., CPM) to be used consistently.
  • Level of Accuracy: Defines the acceptable range for activity duration estimates.
  • Units of Measure: Defines measurement units for resources (hours, days) and time.
  • Control Thresholds: Sets variance thresholds (e.g., ±5%) that trigger corrective action.
  • Performance Rules: Establishes how Earned Value Management (EVM) data is recorded.

Question 4

Part a.. Using relevant examples, explain what is project delay [(2 marks)]
Project delay is the inability of a project team to complete activities within the estimated duration. It manifests as a breach of the time constraint. Example: A contractor failing to complete a hospital due to material supply disruptions.
Part b.. Examine the types of project delays using relevant examples [(8 marks)]
  • Critical vs Non-Critical: Critical delays extend the overall duration (e.g., foundation work delay). Non-critical delays do not (e.g., landscaping delay).
  • Excusable vs Non-Excusable: Excusable are outside control (e.g., floods). Non-excusable are due to internal failure (e.g., poor procurement).
  • Concurrent Delays: Multiple independent delay events occurring at once (e.g., equipment failure + material shortage).
  • Compensable vs Non-Compensable: Compensable entitles the team to time/cost extension (e.g., client design changes). Non-compensable does not.
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 6 — Types of Delays, pp.23–24
Part c.. Analyse five causes of project delays with relevant examples. [(10 marks)]
  • Design Errors: Structural errors requiring demolition and rework.
  • Scope Change: Client requesting major additions (e.g., extra floors) mid-construction.
  • Inappropriate Procurement: Vague contracts leading to legal disputes and work stoppages.
  • Complexity: Large-scale projects affected by international material price spikes.
  • Subcontractor Delays: Specialist partners failing to meet deadlines (e.g., MEP installation delays).
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 6 — Causes of Project Delays, pp.25–27
