2024 Auditing Exam Solutions

Question 1

a) Understanding the Entity and Its Environment

ISA 315 Identifying and Assessing the Risks of Material Misstatement Through Understanding the Entity and Its Environment requires auditors to obtain an understanding of the entity and its environment, including its internal control.

Required:
Explain why obtaining an understanding of the entity and its environment is important for the auditor.
(5 marks)

Solution to Q1A

Obtaining an understanding of the entity and its environment, including its internal control, is a fundamental part of the audit process under ISA 315. This understanding is critical for the auditor for several reasons:

  • Risk Assessment and Problem Identification: It assists the auditor in identifying and assessing the risks of material misstatement of the financial statements, whether due to fraud or error. This understanding provides a frame of reference within which the auditor plans the audit and exercises professional judgment throughout the process.
  • Planning and Performance: A thorough understanding allows the auditor to plan and perform the audit effectively and efficiently. It helps the auditor decide which items to examine and whether to use specific audit procedures, such as sampling or substantive analytical procedures.
  • Establishing Materiality: Knowledge of the business helps the auditor establish an appropriate materiality level and evaluate whether that judgment remains suitable as the audit progresses.
  • Evaluation of Audit Evidence: It provides the necessary context for the auditor to evaluate the appropriateness of audit evidence and the validity of management’s representations. This includes assessing the reasonableness of accounting estimates and identifying related party transactions.
  • Informed Professional Judgment: The auditor uses this knowledge to make informed inquiries and assess the reasonableness of management's answers. It also helps the auditor recognize conflicting information, such as contradictory representations from different sources.
  • Identification of Specialized Areas: Understanding the entity helps the auditor identify areas where special audit considerations or specific skills (such as the work of an expert) may be necessary.
b) Grace Plc — Audit Risks

Your firm has recently been appointed as the auditor to Grace Plc, a private company that runs seven supermarkets situated in the Greater Accra Region. You are currently planning your firm's first audit of Grace Plc and are now aware of the following information after a preliminary visit to Grace's head office.

The company installed a new till system in all supermarkets four months before the year end. The new till system is linked to the accounting system at head office and automatically posts transactions to the accounting system. Previously journals were made manually based on totals on till rolls.

After a number of people living close to one of Grace's stores became seriously ill, the source of the illness was traced back to meat the customers had purchased from Grace. Legal proceedings were commenced against Grace by a number of customers during the financial year. However the outcome of the legal proceedings is uncertain, as it is unclear whether the fault lies with Grace for incorrectly storing the meat or with the local farmer who may have contaminated the meat prior to it being supplied to Grace.

Required:
Using the information provided, describe three audit risks and explain the auditor's response to each risk in planning the audit of Grace.
(6 marks)

Solution to Q1B

Here are three audit risks and the corresponding auditor responses in planning the audit for Grace Plc:

  1. Integration and Posting Risks from the New Till System
    • Audit Risk: Grace Plc installed a new till system linked to the head office accounting system just four months before the year-end. There is a significant risk of control risk and detection risk if the system was not correctly integrated or if data is not being automatically posted accurately from the supermarkets to the head office. Errors in the automated link could lead to pervasive material misstatements in sales revenue and cash.
    • Auditor’s Response: The auditor should perform tests of control over the new automated system to evaluate the effectiveness of its design and operation. This includes conducting walk-through tests to verify that transactions are properly initiated, recorded, and processed through to the general ledger. The auditor should also use Computer-Assisted Audit Techniques (CAATs) to test the accuracy of the automated interface between the tills and the head office accounting system.
  2. Potential Material Liability from Legal Proceedings
    • Audit Risk: Legal proceedings regarding the contaminated meat involve significant uncertainty as the outcome and liability (Grace Plc vs. the local farmer) are unclear. This creates an inherent risk related to the audit of accounting estimates and contingencies. There is a risk that the company may not have appropriately recognized a provision or disclosed a contingent liability in accordance with financial reporting standards, potentially leading to understated liabilities or inadequate disclosure.
    • Auditor’s Response: The auditor should inquire of the entity’s legal advisors regarding the status of the litigation and management’s assessment of the likely outcome. The auditor must review the progress of the claims up to the date the financial statements are approved and evaluate whether a material contingent loss should be accrued as a provision (if probable and estimable) or disclosed in the notes (if only possible).
  3. Going Concern and Reputational Damage
    • Audit Risk: The serious illness of customers and subsequent legal actions represent business risks that could pervasively affect the financial statements by casting doubt on the entity’s going concern status. Loss of customer trust in a supermarket chain can lead to a drastic decline in sales, liquidity problems, and ultimately the inability to continue operations. Pending legal proceedings that could result in large claims the entity cannot satisfy also signal going concern doubt.
    • Auditor’s Response: The auditor should perform risk assessment procedures specifically focused on going concern, such as analyzing the entity's latest interim financial statements and cash flow forecasts. The auditor should inquire of management about their plans for future action to mitigate the impact of the reputational damage and legal claims, and obtain written representations regarding these plans and the appropriateness of the going concern assumption.
c) ISA 300 — Benefits of Audit Planning

ISA 300 Planning an Audit of Financial Statements provides guidance to assist auditors in planning an audit.

Required:
Outline four benefits of audit planning.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q1C

In accordance with ISA 300 Planning an Audit of Financial Statements, the following are four benefits of audit planning:

  • Attention to Important Areas: Planning helps the auditor devote appropriate attention to the most important and high-risk areas of the audit, ensuring that potential issues are identified early.
  • Identification of Potential Problems: It assists the auditor in identifying and resolving potential problems on a timely basis, such as issues related to complex transactions or significant accounting estimates.
  • Organization and Management of the Audit: Planning ensures that the audit engagement is properly organized and managed so that it is performed in an effective and efficient manner.
  • Selection and Assignment of Staff: It assists in the proper selection of engagement team members with appropriate levels of capabilities and competence to respond to anticipated risks, and the proper assignment of work to them.
d) ISA 520 — Analytical Procedures at the Planning Stage

ISA 520 Analytical procedures provides guidance on the use of analytical procedures during the course of the external audit. Analytical procedures can be used as substantive audit procedures during audit fieldwork, as well as during planning and review.

Required:
Identify five factors to consider when using analytical procedures at the planning stage of the audit.
(5 marks)

Solution to Q1D

According to ISA 520 Analytical Procedures, auditors apply analytical procedures during the planning stage to understand the entity’s business better and identify areas of potential risk. Five factors to consider when using analytical procedures at this stage include:

  1. Availability of Information: The auditor must consider whether the necessary information, such as budgets, forecasts, or non-financial data (e.g., number of units produced), is available to perform the analysis.
  2. Reliability of the Information: It is crucial to assess whether the information is trustworthy, such as whether budgets were prepared with sufficient care rather than as optimistic goals.
  3. Relevance of the Information: The auditor considers whether the information is appropriate for the intended procedure, such as ensuring that budgets reflect expected results.
  4. Source of the Information: Information from sources independent of the entity is generally considered more reliable than internally generated data.
  5. Comparability of the Information: The auditor evaluates whether the available data, such as broad industry averages, is comparable to the specific entity’s information or if it needs to be supplemented.

Question 2

A. Written Representations

Towards the end of an audit, it is common for the auditor to seek written representations from the management of the client company. This is usually in the form of a letter from management to the auditors containing all of the required written representations. The auditors often draft the letter for the client to sign.

Required:
Explain why auditors seek written representations from clients.
(2 marks)

List four matters commonly included in the letter containing management's written representations.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q2A

Why auditors seek written representations from clients:
Auditors seek written representations to obtain evidence that management has fulfilled its responsibility for the preparation of the financial statements and for the completeness of the information provided to the auditor. It serves as a formal acknowledgment of management's responsibilities and provides audit evidence for matters where other sufficient appropriate audit evidence cannot reasonably be expected to exist (e.g., management's future intentions or judgments).

Four matters commonly included in the letter of representation:

  1. Responsibility for Financial Statements: A statement that management has fulfilled its responsibility for the preparation of financial statements in accordance with the applicable financial reporting framework.
  2. Information Provided: Confirmation that all relevant information and access requested (such as records, documentation, and other matters) have been provided to the auditor.
  3. Fraud: Acknowledgment of management's responsibility for internal controls to prevent and detect fraud, and disclosure to the auditor of any known or suspected fraud.
  4. Laws and Regulations: Confirmation that all instances of non-compliance or suspected non-compliance with laws and regulations have been disclosed to the auditor.

(Other common matters include: Completeness of related party disclosures, the reasonableness of significant assumptions used in accounting estimates, and that all subsequent events have been adjusted or disclosed.)

B. ISA 570 — Going Concern

ISA 570 Going Concern provides guidance to auditors in respect of ensuring that an entity can continue as a going concern.

Required:
Explain SIX actions/activities that an auditor should carry out to ascertain whether an entity is a going concern.
(6 marks)

Solution to Q2B

To ascertain whether an entity is a going concern, the auditor should perform the following six actions/activities:

  1. Analyze Cash Flow Forecasts: Review and analyze management’s cash flow, profit, and other relevant forecasts for a period of at least 12 months from the reporting date to evaluate the entity’s future liquidity.
  2. Review Interim Financial Statements: Examine the most recent interim financial statements available after the year-end to identify any significant downward trends in trading performance or liquidity.
  3. Inquire of Legal Counsel: Review minutes of meetings and inquire with the entity's lawyers regarding the existence and potential financial impact of any litigation or claims that could threaten the company's survival.
  4. Review Borrowing Facilities: Inspect loan agreements and communicate with lenders to confirm the availability of credit facilities and ensure that the entity has not breached any loan covenants.
  5. Evaluate Management’s Plans: Discuss with management their plans for future actions, such as plans to liquidate assets, borrow money, restructure debt, or reduce/delay expenditures, and assess whether these plans are feasible.
  6. Review Board Minutes: Examine the minutes of meetings of shareholders, those charged with governance, and relevant committees for reference to financing difficulties or discussions regarding the entity's ability to continue operations.
C. BDE Company Limited — Substantive Procedures

BDE Company Limited operates a hotel providing accommodation, leisure facilities and restaurants. Its year ends on 31 March. You are the senior associate of ABC and Associates and are currently preparing the audit programmes for the year-end audit of BDE Company Limited. You are reviewing the notes of last week's meeting between the audit manager and finance director where two material issues were discussed.

Depreciation
BDE Company Limited incurred significant capital expenditure during the year for refurbishing the restaurant facilities for the hotel. The finance director has proposed that the new leisure equipment should be depreciated over 10 years using the straight-line method.

Food Poisoning
BDE's directors received correspondence in January from a group of customers who attended a wedding at the hotel. They have alleged that they suffered severe food poisoning from food eaten at the hotel and are claiming substantial damages. BDE's lawyers have received the claim and believe that the lawsuit against the company is unlikely to be successful.

Required:
Describe substantive procedures to obtain sufficient and appropriate audit evidence in relation to the above two issues.
(8 marks)

Solution to Q2C

In accordance with ISA 500 and ISA 540, here are substantive procedures for the two issues identified:

1. Depreciation of New Leisure Equipment (4 marks)

  • Review Capital Expenditure: Inspect invoices and purchase agreements for the refurbishment to confirm the cost of the new leisure equipment and ensure it has been correctly capitalized in the non-current asset register.
  • Assess Useful Life: Evaluate the reasonableness of the 10-year useful life by comparing it to the manufacturer’s specifications, industry averages for leisure equipment, and the company's historical replacement cycle for similar assets.
  • Recalculate Depreciation: Perform an independent recalculation of the depreciation charge for the year (Cost × 10% for the period since the asset was ready for use) to ensure mathematical accuracy.
  • Verify Asset Existence: Perform a physical inspection of the refurbished restaurant and new leisure equipment to ensure the assets exist and are currently in use.

2. Food Poisoning Claim (4 marks)

  • Review Legal Correspondence: Inspect the original correspondence from the customers and any formal legal claim documents to understand the nature of the allegation and the amount of damages being sought.
  • Direct Communication with Lawyers: Send a formal letter of inquiry to BDE’s external legal counsel (an "Audit Inquiry Letter") to obtain their professional opinion on the probability of a successful claim and an estimate of potential financial exposure.
  • Review Board Minutes: Examine the minutes of the Board of Directors and the risk committee to see how the company is managing the claim and if any internal investigations (e.g., health and safety reports) have been conducted.
  • Evaluate Disclosure: Review the draft financial statements to ensure that the legal claim is treated as a contingent liability (disclosed in the notes but not provided for), consistent with the lawyers’ view that the lawsuit is "unlikely to be successful" (i.e., not a "probable" outflow under IAS 37).

Question 3

A. Internal Audit Departments — Typical Activities

In recent times, the establishment of Internal Audit Units for medium and large entities (both private and public sector entities) has become the norm. Many are those who argue that now is the time to legislate this practice and make it mandatory for all public interest entities (PIEs) to have Internal Audit Units.

Required:
List four typical activities normally carried out by internal audit departments.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q3A

Internal audit departments typically perform the following activities:

  • Review of Internal Controls: Evaluating the design and operational effectiveness of the entity's internal control systems to identify weaknesses and suggest improvements.
  • Examination of Financial and Operating Information: Reviewing the means used to identify, measure, classify, and report financial and operating information, including the detailed testing of transactions and balances.
  • Review of Compliance: Assessing the entity’s compliance with laws, regulations, and other external requirements, as well as with management’s internal policies and directives.
  • Value for Money (VFM) Audits: Reviewing the economy, efficiency, and effectiveness of operating activities (often referred to as "the three Es").
B. Internal Audit vs External Audit — Differences

Required:
Explain the main differences between INTERNAL AUDIT and EXTERNAL AUDIT in respect of objectives, scope of work and reporting responsibilities.
(6 marks)

Solution to Q3B

The main differences between internal and external audit are as follows:

Feature Internal Audit External Audit
Objectives To evaluate and improve the effectiveness of risk management, control, and governance processes. To provide an independent opinion on whether the financial statements show a true and fair view.
Scope of Work Determined by management (or those charged with governance) and covers any part of the business. Determined by statute and focuses on the financial statements and underlying records.
Reporting Responsibility Reports directly to management or the audit committee. Reports are generally not public. Reports to the shareholders (the owners) of the company. Reports are in the public domain.
C. Internal and External Auditors — Responsibilities for Fraud and Error

There are similarities and differences between the responsibilities of internal auditor and external auditors. Both internal auditors and external auditors have responsibilities relating to the prevention, detection and reporting of fraud, for example, but their responsibilities are not the same.

Required:
Explain the difference between the responsibilities of internal auditors and external auditors for the prevention, detection and reporting of fraud and error.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q3C

The responsibilities differ primarily in their focus and the party to whom they are accountable:

  • Internal Auditors: They are primarily responsible for the prevention and detection of fraud and error through the design and monitoring of robust internal control systems. Their reporting is internal, aimed at assisting management in discharging their stewardship responsibilities.
  • External Auditors: Under ISA 240, the external auditor's responsibility is to obtain reasonable assurance that the financial statements are free from material misstatement, whether caused by fraud or error. They are not responsible for the prevention of fraud but must detect and report material frauds that affect the financial statements to those charged with governance and, in some cases, regulatory authorities.
D. ISA 610 — Using the Work of Internal Auditors

ISA 610 'Using the work of internal auditors' provides guidance to external auditors on the use of internal audit work.

Required:
Identify and explain four criteria that should be considered by external auditors when assessing whether to place reliance on work performed by internal audit.
(6 marks)

Solution to Q3D

According to ISA 610, the external auditor must consider the following four criteria before placing reliance on the work of internal auditors:

  1. Objectivity: The external auditor assesses the status of the internal audit function within the organization and whether the internal auditors are free from any conflict of interest. A high degree of objectivity is achieved when they report to those charged with governance (e.g., the Audit Committee) rather than management.
  2. Technical Competence: The auditor evaluates whether the internal auditors have adequate technical training and proficiency. This includes checking professional qualifications, experience, and the level of supervision and review within the internal audit department.
  3. Due Professional Care: The auditor considers whether the internal audit work is properly planned, supervised, reviewed, and documented. This involves examining audit programs, working papers, and the quality of their reports.
  4. Communication: The auditor assesses whether communication between the external and internal auditors is likely to be effective. This includes evaluating whether the internal auditors are willing to share their reports and discuss any significant matters that arise.

Question 4

Baba Ltd is a manufacturer of electrical equipment. It has factories in Accra and Tamale and its customer base includes retailers as well as individuals, to whom direct sales are made through their website. The company's year end is 30 September 2023. You are an audit supervisor of Boafo & Co Chartered Accountants and are currently reviewing documentation of Baba's internal control in preparation for the interim audit.

Baba's website allows individuals to order goods directly, and full payment is taken in advance. Currently the website is not integrated into the inventory system and inventory levels are not checked at the time when orders are placed.

Goods are dispatched via local couriers; however, they do not always record customer signatures as proof that the customer has received the goods. Over the past 12 months there have been customer complaints about the delay between sales orders and receipt of goods. Baba has investigated these and found that, in each case, the sales order had been entered into the sales system correctly but was not forwarded to the dispatch department for fulfilling.

Baba's retail customers undergo credit checks prior to being accepted and credit limits are set accordingly by sales ledger clerks. These customers place their orders through one of the sales team, who decides on sales discount levels.

Raw materials used in the manufacturing process are purchased from a wide range of suppliers. As a result of staff changes in the purchase ledger department, supplier statement reconciliations are no longer performed. Additionally, changes to supplier details in the purchase ledger master file can be undertaken by purchase ledger clerks as well as supervisors. In the past six months, Baba has changed part of its manufacturing process and as a result some new equipment has been purchased; however, there are considerable levels of plant and equipment which are now surplus to requirement. Purchase requisitions for all new equipment have been authorised by production supervisors and little has been done to reduce the surplus of old equipment.

A. Internal Control of Baba Ltd

Required — In respect of the internal control of Baba Ltd:
Identify and explain four deficiencies.
Recommend a control to address each of these deficiencies.
Describe a test of control Boafo & Co Chartered Accountants would perform to assess if each of these controls is operating effectively.
(12 marks)

Solution to Q4A

Internal Control Report: Baba Ltd

Identified Deficiency Control Recommendation Test of Control
Website not integrated with inventory: The website does not check inventory levels at the time of order. This leads to accepting orders and payments for out-of-stock items, causing delays and dissatisfaction. The website should be integrated with the inventory system to provide real-time stock availability. If an item is out of stock, the system should prevent the order or provide an expected delivery date. Place a "test order" on the website for an item known to be out of stock and verify that the system either prevents the sale or issues an appropriate warning.
Lack of Proof of Delivery: Couriers do not always obtain customer signatures. This makes it difficult for Baba to prove goods were received, leading to potential revenue loss through fraudulent non-delivery claims. Baba should mandate that couriers obtain a digital or physical signature for all deliveries. Despatch staff should periodically review delivery reports to ensure signatures are captured. Select a sample of despatched orders and inspect the corresponding courier delivery notes/records for a valid customer signature or proof of receipt.
Sales Orders not Forwarded: Orders are entered correctly but are not consistently forwarded to the despatch department, leading to significant delays and customer complaints. The system should automatically generate a sequential "Pending Despatch" report. Management should review this daily to ensure all entered orders have been moved to the warehouse for fulfillment. Observe the daily review process of the "Pending Despatch" report and inspect a sample of reports for evidence of management’s signature or review mark.
Lack of Supplier Statement Reconciliations: Due to staff changes, these reconciliations are no longer performed. This increases the risk of errors in the purchase ledger, double payments, or missed liabilities. Supplier statement reconciliations should be performed monthly by a staff member independent of the purchase ledger clerks. Any discrepancies should be investigated and cleared promptly. Request the folder containing monthly supplier reconciliations and inspect a sample to ensure they have been completed, signed, and reviewed by a supervisor.
Unauthorized Access to Master File: Purchase ledger clerks (as well as supervisors) can change supplier details in the master file. This creates a fraud risk where a clerk could change a supplier's bank details to their own. Access to the purchase ledger master file should be restricted to supervisors only. Any changes made should be logged by the system and reviewed by an independent manager. Attempt to access and change a supplier's bank details using a "clerk-level" login to verify that access is restricted. Review the "audit trail" report for unauthorized changes.
Surplus Plant and Equipment: Old equipment is not being disposed of, and production supervisors are authorizing new purchases without higher-level oversight. This leads to inefficient use of capital and potential impairment issues. All capital expenditure requisitions should be authorized by a senior manager (e.g., Production Director). A formal review of surplus assets should be conducted to facilitate disposal or scrap. Inspect a sample of capital purchase requisitions to verify they were signed by the designated senior manager and check for evidence of a recent "surplus asset" review meeting.

Summary for Audit Committee
The current control environment at Baba Ltd shows significant weaknesses in the Information System and Control Activities components of internal control. Addressing the lack of system integration and the lack of segregation of duties in the purchase ledger should be prioritized to prevent fraud and operational inefficiencies.

B. Substantive Procedures — Plant and Equipment

Required:
Describe substantive procedures you should perform at the year end to confirm each of the following for plant and equipment:
Additions.
(4 marks)

Disposals.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q4B

The following are the substantive procedures to be performed at the year-end for plant and equipment:

Additions (4 marks)

  • Vouching to Supporting Documents: Vouch a sample of plant and equipment additions to original supporting documentation, such as purchase invoices, deeds, or architect’s certificates, to confirm the cost and date of acquisition.
  • Verify Authorization: Inspect the minutes of board meetings or capital expenditure authorizations to ensuring that all material additions were properly approved by management or those charged with governance.
  • Physical Inspection: Physically inspect a sample of the newly acquired assets to confirm their existence and ensure they are currently in use by the entity.
  • Check Capitalization Policy: Review the appropriateness of the capitalization policies, especially for assets constructed by the entity's own labor, to ensure that only eligible costs have been capitalized.

Disposals (4 marks)

  • Test Disposal Proceeds: Obtain a schedule of all disposals made during the year and test the proceeds of sale against independent evidence, such as sales agreements or bank statements.
  • Check Authorization for Disposal: Inspect the formal authority for the disposal of assets (e.g., board minutes or disposal forms) and find out about any assets that were scrapped during the year to ensure they were authorized for removal.
  • Verify Removal from Records: Verify that the original cost and the related accumulated depreciation for the disposed assets have been correctly eliminated from the fixed asset register and general ledger.
  • Recalculate Profit or Loss: Independently recalculate the profit or loss arising on the disposals and agree these amounts to the entries made in the income statement.
