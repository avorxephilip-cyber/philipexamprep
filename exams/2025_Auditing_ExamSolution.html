2025 Auditing Exam Solutions

Question 1

a) International Standards on Auditing (ISAs) — IAASB

International Standards on Auditing (ISAs) are produced by the International Auditing and Assurance Standards Board (IAASB), which is a technical committee of the International Federation of Accountants (IFAC). In recent years, there has been a trend for more countries to implement the ISAs rather than produce their own auditing standards.

A friend is considering registering with Institute of Chartered Accountants (Ghana) as a trainee accountant. However, she is concerned about the extent of regulations which auditors have to follow and does not understand why ISAs have to be used in Ghana.

Required:
Describe the role of the IAASB.
(2 marks)

Explain the process involved in issuing an ISA.
(4 marks)

Explain the extent to which an auditor must follow ISAs.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q1A

Role of the IAASB (2 marks)
The International Auditing and Assurance Standards Board (IAASB) is an independent standard-setting body that serves the public interest by setting high-quality international standards for auditing, assurance, and other related standards. Its primary roles include:

  • Developing and issuing International Standards on Auditing (ISAs) to improve the degree of uniformity of auditing practices and related services throughout the world.
  • Facilitating the convergence of international and national auditing standards, thereby enhancing the quality and consistency of practice throughout the world and strengthening public confidence in the global auditing and assurance profession.

Process Involved in Issuing an ISA (4 marks)
The IAASB follows a rigorous "due process" to ensure the transparency and quality of the standards issued. The typical stages include:

  1. Research and Consultation: The IAASB identifies a need for a new standard or an amendment to an existing one. A project task force is established to research the issue and develop a draft.
  2. Exposure Draft: A proposed standard (Exposure Draft) is published on the IAASB website and distributed to member bodies and the public for comment. This period usually lasts at least 120 days.
  3. Consideration of Comments: The IAASB reviews all comments received during the exposure period. The draft is then revised as necessary based on the feedback from stakeholders.
  4. Approval and Publication: The final standard is approved by an affirmative vote of at least two-thirds of the IAASB members. Once approved, it is published and becomes part of the Handbook of International Quality Control, Auditing, Review, Other Assurance, and Related Services Pronouncements.

Extent to Which an Auditor Must Follow ISAs (4 marks)
According to the regulatory framework for auditing:

  • Mandatory Compliance: Auditors are required to comply with all ISAs relevant to the audit. An ISA is relevant when it is in effect and the circumstances addressed by the ISA exist (e.g., if there is no internal audit function, ISA 610 is not relevant).
  • Compliance with Objectives: The auditor must understand the entire text of an ISA to understand its objectives and apply its requirements properly. The auditor must achieve the objectives stated in each relevant ISA.
  • Departure from ISAs: In exceptional circumstances, an auditor may judge it necessary to depart from a relevant requirement in an ISA. If so, the auditor must perform alternative audit procedures to achieve the aim of that requirement and must document the reasons for the departure and how the alternative procedures achieved the objective.
  • Professional Judgment: While ISAs provide a framework, the auditor must still exercise professional judgment and professional skepticism in applying these standards to the specific facts and circumstances of an audit engagement.
b) IESBA Code of Ethics for Professional Accountants

Compliance with the fundamental principles in IESBA's Code of Ethics for Professional Accountants can be threatened in a number of ways.

Required:
List and explain each of the five fundamental principles of IESBA's Code of Ethics for Professional Accountants.
(5 marks)

List the five ethical threats to independence and objectivity and for each threat identify one example of a circumstance that may create the threat.
(5 marks)

Solution to Q1B

The Five Fundamental Principles (5 marks)

  1. Integrity: This principle imposes an obligation on all professional accountants to be straightforward and honest in all professional and business relationships. Integrity also implies fair dealing and truthfulness.
  2. Objectivity: Accountants should not allow bias, conflict of interest, or undue influence of others to override professional or business judgments.
  3. Professional Competence and Due Care: This requires maintaining professional knowledge and skill at the level required to ensure that a client or employer receives competent professional services. It also involves acting diligently and in accordance with applicable technical and professional standards.
  4. Confidentiality: Professional accountants must respect the confidentiality of information acquired as a result of professional and business relationships. They should not disclose any such information to third parties without proper and specific authority unless there is a legal or professional right or duty to disclose.
  5. Professional Behavior: This principle requires compliance with relevant laws and regulations and the avoidance of any conduct that the professional accountant knows or should know might discredit the profession.

The Five Ethical Threats to Independence and Objectivity (5 marks)

  1. Self-Interest Threat: The threat that a financial or other interest will inappropriately influence the professional accountant’s judgment or behavior.
    • Example: An audit firm or a member of the audit team having a direct financial interest (owning shares) in the audit client.
  2. Self-Review Threat: The threat that a professional accountant will not appropriately evaluate the results of a previous judgment made or service performed by the accountant or the accountant's firm.
    • Example: An audit firm auditing financial statements that they themselves prepared (e.g., providing bookkeeping services and then performing the audit).
  3. Advocacy Threat: The threat that a professional accountant will promote a client’s or employer’s position to the point that their objectivity is compromised.
    • Example: An auditor acting as an advocate on behalf of an audit client in litigation or disputes with third parties.
  4. Familiarity Threat: The threat that due to a long or close relationship with a client or employer, a professional accountant will be too sympathetic to their interests or too accepting of their work.
    • Example: A member of the audit team having a close family member (like a spouse or parent) serving as a director or officer of the audit client.
  5. Intimidation Threat: The threat that a professional accountant will be deterred from acting objectively because of actual or perceived pressures, including attempts to exercise undue influence over the accountant.
    • Example: A client threatening to replace the audit firm or terminate the engagement over a disagreement regarding an accounting treatment.

Question 2

a) Objective of the Statutory Audit

Required:
State the objective of the statutory audit and explain how carrying out the audit in accordance with ISAs helps the auditor to achieve that objective.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q2A

Objective:
The primary objective of a statutory audit is to enable the auditor to express an opinion on whether the financial statements are prepared, in all material respects, in accordance with an applicable financial reporting framework. In many jurisdictions, this is referred to as expressing an opinion on whether the financial statements give a "true and fair view".

How ISAs help achieve this:

  • Standardization of Procedures: ISAs provide a rigorous and consistent framework of procedures that the auditor must follow, ensuring that the audit is conducted to a high level of quality regardless of the entity's location.
  • Risk-Based Approach: ISAs (specifically ISA 315) require a risk-based approach, which ensures that audit effort is focused on areas where there is a higher likelihood of material misstatement, thereby increasing the reliability of the audit opinion.
  • Evidence Collection: ISAs provide guidance on obtaining "sufficient appropriate audit evidence," which gives the auditor a reasonable basis for the opinion and minimizes the risk of an incorrect conclusion.
b) ISA 315 — Matters Documented During Audit Planning

ISA 315 Identifying and assessing the risks of material misstatement through understanding the entity and its environment sets out matters that should be documented during the planning stage of an audit.

Required:
List six matters that should be documented during audit planning.
(3 marks)

Solution to Q2B

According to ISA 315, the auditor should document the following matters during the planning stage to provide a record of the risk assessment process:

  1. The Discussion Among the Engagement Team: Documentation of the brainstorming session regarding the susceptibility of the entity’s financial statements to material misstatement (including fraud risks).
  2. Key Elements of the Understanding: Documentation of the entity and its environment, including each of the internal control components.
  3. The Sources of Information: A record of where the understanding was obtained (e.g., meetings with management, observation of processes).
  4. Identified and Assessed Risks: A list of the risks of material misstatement identified at both the financial statement level and the assertion level.
  5. Significant Risks Identified: Specific documentation of risks that, in the auditor’s judgment, require special audit consideration.
  6. Risks Requiring Substantive Procedures Alone: Documentation of risks for which the auditor has determined it is not possible or practicable to obtain sufficient appropriate audit evidence only from tests of controls.
c) ISA 230 — Audit Working Papers

ISA 230 Audit documentation provides guidance to auditors in respect of audit working papers.

Required:
List six factors which affect the form and content of audit working papers.
(3 marks)

Solution to Q2C

Under ISA 230 Audit Documentation, the form and content of audit working papers are not fixed and will vary based on the specific circumstances of the engagement. Six factors that affect the form and content include:

  1. The Size and Complexity of the Entity: Larger or more complex entities require more extensive documentation to explain intricate transactions and structures.
  2. The Nature of the Audit Procedures: Different procedures (e.g., automated data analytics vs. manual physical inspection) result in different types of documentation.
  3. The Identified Risks of Material Misstatement: Areas identified as high risk will require more detailed working papers to demonstrate the depth of the auditor's response.
  4. The Significance of the Evidence Obtained: More persuasive or critical evidence usually requires more formal and detailed recording.
  5. The Nature and Extent of Exceptions Identified: If errors or deviations are found, the documentation must be more extensive to show how the auditor followed up and resolved the issue.
  6. The Audit Methodology and Tools Used: The use of specific audit software or standardized firm templates will dictate the format of the files.
d) Biggy Plc — Audit Strategy and Outsourced Payroll

You are an audit senior working for Sammy & Co Chartered Accountants and are now commencing the planning of the annual audit of a client, Biggy Plc. Biggy Plc owns and runs a chain of restaurants in all the major cities in Ghana. The date is 4 December 2024 and Biggy Plc's year end is 31 December 2024.

Biggy Plc has proved to be very popular in recent years. This year, it saw its revenue from sales of food and drink increase by 15% compared to the previous year. In view of its success, the directors are considering plans to expand the business by acquiring some existing pubs in Accra and Kumasi. 70% of Biggy Plc's takings are paid in cash, with the remainder being paid by credit card.

For the first time this year, Biggy Plc has outsourced its payroll function to a firm of accountants called Ricks & Co. Payroll costs form a substantial cost in Biggy Plc's statement of profit or loss. Ricks & Co prepares the payroll records and updates it for starters and leavers based on information provided by Biggy Plc. A series of payroll reports are securely e-mailed to Biggy Plc each month and reviewed by the appropriate management. Payments are made to employees on the basis of a net pay report provided and journals are put through to reflect the wages costs and related liabilities.

Required:
List and explain the purpose of three of the main sections of an audit strategy document and for each section, provide an example relevant to Biggy Plc.
(6 marks)

Describe the audit team's responsibilities in relation to obtaining an understanding of the services provided by Ricks & Co when planning the audit of Biggy Plc.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q2D

Main Sections of an Audit Strategy Document
As per ISA 300, the audit strategy sets the scope, timing, and direction of the audit. Below are three main sections:

  1. Characteristics of the Engagement (Scope)
    • Purpose: To define the boundaries of the audit, including the financial reporting framework and industry-specific reporting requirements.
    • Biggy Plc Example: The audit must account for the high volume of cash transactions (70% of takings) and the regulatory requirements for the food and beverage industry in Ghana.
  2. Reporting Objectives, Timing of the Audit, and Nature of Communications
    • Purpose: To determine the timetable for reporting and the nature of communications required (e.g., with management or those charged with governance).
    • Biggy Plc Example: The audit team needs to plan the year-end procedures shortly after December 31, 2024, and coordinate communication with management regarding the upcoming expansion into Accra and Kumasi.
  3. Significant Factors, Preliminary Engagement Activities, and Knowledge Gained on Other Engagements
    • Purpose: To identify areas where there may be higher risks of material misstatement so that audit resources can be directed effectively.
    • Biggy Plc Example: The 15% increase in revenue and the new outsourcing of the payroll function to Ricks & Co are significant factors that increase the risk profile and will require specific audit focus.

Understanding Services Provided by Ricks & Co (4 marks)
When a client outsources a significant function like payroll, the auditor must follow ISA 402 Audit Considerations Relating to an Entity Using a Service Organization. The audit team’s responsibilities include:

  • Obtaining an Understanding of the Service: The auditor must understand how Biggy Plc uses the services of Ricks & Co, including the nature of the services provided and how they affect Biggy Plc’s internal controls relevant to the audit.
  • Assessing the Nature and Materiality of Transactions: Since payroll is a substantial cost in the statement of profit or loss, the auditor must evaluate the significance of the transactions processed by Ricks & Co and their impact on the financial statements.
  • Evaluating Internal Controls: The auditor needs to determine how Biggy Plc maintains control over the information sent to and received from Ricks & Co (e.g., how they review the monthly payroll reports and authorize the net pay payments).
  • Determining the Need for Further Evidence: The auditor must decide if sufficient evidence can be obtained from Biggy Plc’s records or if it is necessary to contact Ricks & Co, potentially requesting a Type 1 or Type 2 Service Auditor's Report to understand the operating effectiveness of the controls at the service organization.

Question 3

RST Plc supplies a wide range of garden and agricultural products to foreign and domestic customers. The company has six divisions, with each division specialising in the sale of specific products, for example, seeds, garden furniture, agricultural fertilizers etc. The company has an internal audit department which provides audit reports to the audit committee on each division on a rotational basis.

Products in the seed division are offered for sale to domestic customers via an internet site. Customers review the product list on the internet and place orders for packets of seeds using specific product codes, along with their credit card details, onto RST Plc's secure server. Order quantities are normally between one and three packets for each type of seed. Order details are transferred manually onto the company's internal inventory control and sales system, and a two-part packing list is printed in the seed warehouse.

Each order and packing list is given a random alphabetical code based on the name of the employee inputting the order, the date, and the products being ordered.

In the seed warehouse, the packages of seeds for each order are taken from specific bins and dispatched to the customer with one copy of the packing list. The second copy of the packing list is sent to the accounts department where the inventory and sales computer is updated to show that the order has been dispatched. The customer's credit card is then charged by the inventory control and sales computer. Bad debts in RST are currently 3% of total sales.

Finally, the computer system checks that for each charge made to a customer's credit card account, the order details are on file to prove that the charge was made correctly. The order file is marked as completed confirming that the order has been dispatched and payment obtained.

a) Sales System Deficiencies — Seed Division of RST Plc

Required:
In respect of sales in the seeds division of RST Plc, prepare a report to be sent to the audit committee of RST Plc which:
Identifies and explains five deficiencies in that sales system;
Explains the possible effect of each deficiency; and
Provides a recommendation to alleviate each deficiency.
(17 marks)

Solution to Q3A

To: Audit Committee of RST Plc
From: ABC & Associates (External Auditors)
Date: [Current Date]
Subject: Internal Control Deficiencies in the Seed Division Sales System

Following our preliminary review of the sales system within the Seed Division of RST Plc, we have identified several internal control deficiencies. Below are the identified weaknesses, their potential effects on the business, and our recommendations for improvement.

Report on Sales System Deficiencies

Deficiency Possible Effect Recommendation
1. Manual Transfer of Data: Order details are transferred manually from the secure server to the internal inventory and sales system. Manual entry is highly susceptible to human error, such as incorrect product codes or quantities, leading to wrong shipments and customer dissatisfaction. The system should be integrated so that orders placed on the secure server interface automatically with the inventory and sales system, eliminating manual intervention.
2. Random Alphabetical Coding: Packing lists are assigned random alphabetical codes based on the employee's name rather than a sequential numerical system. It is impossible to identify missing orders or ensure that all orders have been processed. This lacks a "completeness" check and makes the audit trail difficult to follow. The system should automatically generate unique, pre-printed, or system-generated sequential numbers for every order to ensure all transactions are accounted for.
3. Lack of Physical Verification: In the warehouse, seeds are picked and dispatched with a packing list without a second independent check of the items against the order. There is a risk that the wrong type or quantity of seeds may be sent to customers, leading to increased returns, refund costs, and reputational damage. A supervisor or a second warehouse staff member should perform a "blind check" to verify that the physical goods match the packing list before dispatch.
4. Delayed Billing (Post-Dispatch): The customer’s credit card is only charged after the packing list is sent back to the accounts department and the system is manually updated. If the accounts department fails to update the system or if a packing list is lost, the customer may never be charged, leading to a direct loss of revenue. The system should be configured to "block" or "hold" the funds on the customer's credit card at the point of order, with the final charge processed automatically upon the warehouse scanning the items for dispatch.
5. Retrospective "Proof" Check: The system checks that a charge was correct only after it has been made, by looking for order details on file. This is a "detective" rather than a "preventive" control. If the order file is missing or corrupted before this check, the company may struggle to justify the charge if disputed by the customer. The system should perform a "three-way match" (Order vs. Dispatch Note vs. Payment Authorization) before the final transaction is posted to the ledger and the card is permanently charged.

Additional Observation: Bad Debts

Although not a direct system "deficiency" in the workflow, the report notes that bad debts are currently 3% of total sales. For an internet-based system using credit cards, 3% is relatively high, as credit card transactions are generally guaranteed if authorized correctly. This suggests either a failure in the initial authorization process or a high volume of disputed charges (chargebacks) due to the shipping errors mentioned above.

We recommend that the Internal Audit Department prioritize a review of the Seed Division’s integration between the web server and the accounting software to address these risks.

Signed,
Audit Senior Associate

b) Advantages of an Audit Committee for RST Plc

Required:
Outline three advantages to RST Plc of having an audit committee.
(3 marks)

Solution to Q3B

  • Improved Quality of Financial Reporting: The audit committee provides an independent review of the financial statements and the audit process. This oversight helps ensure that the financial information provided to the board and shareholders is accurate, transparent, and compliant with relevant standards (like IFRS), thereby increasing the "true and fair" quality of the reports.
  • Enhanced Internal Control and Risk Management: The audit committee monitors the effectiveness of the company’s internal control systems and the work of the internal audit department. For RST Plc, which has multiple divisions and a complex sales system, the committee can ensure that internal audit reports are acted upon and that deficiencies (such as the manual data transfer in the seed division) are addressed by management.
  • Strengthened Auditor Independence: The audit committee acts as a bridge between the external auditors and the board of directors. By managing the appointment, remuneration, and dismissal of the auditors, the committee protects the external auditors from undue pressure by management, ensuring a more objective and independent audit.

Question 4

ISA 530 Audit sampling states that the objective of the auditor, when using audit sampling, is to provide a reasonable basis for the auditor to draw conclusions about the population from which the sample is selected.

a) Statistical and Non-Statistical Sampling

Required:
Explain the difference between statistical and non-statistical sampling and describe four methods of sample selection.
(6 marks)

Solution to Q4A

Difference between Statistical and Non-Statistical Sampling:

  • Statistical Sampling: An approach to sampling that uses the laws of probability to select and evaluate a sample. It involves random selection of the sample items and the use of probability theory to evaluate sample results, including the measurement of sampling risk.
  • Non-Statistical Sampling: Also known as judgmental sampling, this approach does not use mathematical/statistical techniques. Instead, the auditor relies on their professional judgment to select items they believe are most representative of the population or those that carry higher risk.

Four Methods of Sample Selection:

  1. Random Selection: Applied through random number generators. Each item in the population has an equal chance of selection.
  2. Systematic Selection: The number of sampling units in the population is divided by the sample size to give a sampling interval (e.g., every 50th item).
  3. Haphazard Selection: The auditor selects the sample without following a structured technique but avoids any conscious bias or predictability.
  4. Block Selection: Selecting a group of contiguous items from within the population (e.g., all sales invoices for the month of March).
b) Procedures When Errors Are Identified in a Sample

Required:
Describe the procedures the auditor would perform where errors have been identified in a sample.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q4B

When errors are identified in a sample, the auditor should perform the following procedures:

  1. Investigate the Nature and Cause of Errors: The auditor should analyze each error to determine if it is a "one-off" anomaly or a systemic issue (e.g., a software bug vs. a manual slip).
  2. Project the Errors to the Population: Under ISA 530, the auditor must project the misstatements found in the sample to the entire population to understand the potential scale of the misstatement.
  3. Evaluate Sampling Risk: The auditor must consider if the results of the sample provide a reasonable basis for conclusion or if the risk of material misstatement is higher than initially assessed.
  4. Perform Additional Procedures: If the projected error is close to or exceeds tolerable misstatement, the auditor should request management to investigate the errors or increase the sample size to obtain more evidence.
c) ISA 500 — Sufficiency and Reliability of Audit Evidence

ISA 500 Audit Evidence requires audit evidence to be sufficient and appropriate. Evidence is appropriate if it is both relevant and reliable.

Required:
Explain six factors that influence the sufficiency and reliability of audit evidence.
(6 marks)

Solution to Q4C

The sufficiency (quantity) and reliability (quality) of audit evidence are influenced by the following six factors:

  1. Risk Assessment: The higher the assessed risk of material misstatement, the more evidence (sufficiency) is likely to be required.
  2. Source of Evidence: Evidence obtained from independent sources outside the entity (e.g., bank confirmations) is more reliable than evidence obtained from within.
  3. Directness of Evidence: Evidence obtained directly by the auditor (e.g., physical observation) is more reliable than evidence obtained indirectly or by inference.
  4. Controls Effectiveness: Evidence generated internally is more reliable when the entity’s internal controls over its preparation and maintenance are effective.
  5. Documentary Form: Evidence in the form of documents (paper or electronic) is more reliable than evidence obtained through oral representations alone.
  6. Originality: Evidence provided by original documents is more reliable than evidence provided by photocopies or digitized versions, which can be easily altered.
d) ISA 315 — Assertions Relating to Classes of Transactions and Events

Required:
List and explain four assertions from ISA 315 Identifying and assessing the risks of material misstatement through understanding the entity and its environment that relate to classes of transactions and events.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q4D

According to ISA 315, auditors use assertions to consider the different types of potential misstatements that may occur. For classes of transactions (Profit & Loss items), four key assertions are:

  1. Occurrence: This confirms that transactions and events that have been recorded actually took place and pertain to the entity (e.g., recorded sales actually happened).
  2. Completeness: This ensures that all transactions and events that should have been recorded have been included (e.g., no expenses were left out).
  3. Accuracy: This pertains to whether amounts and other data relating to recorded transactions have been recorded appropriately (e.g., the math on an invoice is correct).
  4. Cut-off: This ensures that transactions and events have been recorded in the correct accounting period (e.g., December sales are not recorded in January).
