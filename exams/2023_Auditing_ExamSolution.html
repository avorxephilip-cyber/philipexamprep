Question 1

1. ISA 210 — Audit Engagement Terms (Preconditions for an Audit)

ISA 210 deals with the Auditor's responsibility in agreeing the terms of the audit engagement with management and where appropriate, those charged with governance. This includes establishing certain preconditions for an audit and responsibilities which rests with management and where appropriate those charged with governance.

Required:
What three preconditions must be established before an audit engagement is accepted?
(3 marks)

Under what two circumstances will an auditor refuse an engagement?
(2 marks)

Solution to Q1.1

Preconditions for an Audit
Before an audit engagement is accepted, the auditor must establish the following three preconditions:

  1. Acceptable Financial Reporting Framework: The auditor must determine whether the financial reporting framework (e.g., IFRS) to be applied in the preparation of the financial statements is acceptable.
  2. Management's Responsibility for Financial Statements: The auditor must obtain the agreement of management that it acknowledges and understands its responsibility for the preparation of the financial statements in accordance with the applicable financial reporting framework.
  3. Agreement on Internal Control and Access: Management must acknowledge its responsibility for:
    • Such internal control as management determines is necessary to enable the preparation of financial statements that are free from material misstatement;
    • Providing the auditor with access to all information relevant to the preparation of the financial statements;
    • Providing unrestricted access to persons within the entity from whom the auditor determines it necessary to obtain audit evidence.

Circumstances for Refusing an Engagement
An auditor shall refuse an engagement (unless required by law or regulation to do otherwise) under the following two circumstances:

  1. Management Imposes a Limitation on Scope: If management or those charged with governance impose a limitation on the scope of the auditor’s work in the terms of a proposed audit engagement such that the auditor believes the limitation will result in the auditor disclaiming an opinion on the financial statements.
  2. Preconditions are Not Present: If the preconditions for an audit (as listed above) are not met—for example, if management refuses to acknowledge its responsibilities or if the financial reporting framework is deemed unacceptable for the nature of the entity.
2. ISA 315 — Understanding the Entity and Its Environment

To prepare an audit strategy and an audit plan, the Auditor will need to gain an understanding of the entity and its environment in accordance with ISA 315 "Identifying and assessing the risk of material misstatement through understanding the entity and its environment".

Required:
State five sources from which the Auditor can obtain knowledge of the industry and the organisation.
(5 marks)

Identify five components that together make up internal controls.
(5 marks)

Define the concept of risk-based auditing.
(3 marks)

Solution to Q1.2

Sources of Knowledge of the Industry and Organization
The auditor can obtain knowledge of the industry and the organization from the following five sources:

  1. Prior Year Audit Files: Reviewing previous working papers to identify long-standing accounting policies, recurring problems, and the history of the client.
  2. Inquiry with Management and Staff: Discussions with those charged with governance and employees regarding current operations, objectives, and strategies.
  3. Industry Reports and Publications: Reviewing trade journals, industry-specific regulations, and economic surveys to understand the external environment and competition.
  4. Observation and Inspection: Visiting the entity’s premises and plant sites to observe operations and inspecting internal documents like board minutes and procedure manuals.
  5. Analytical Procedures: Performing high-level comparisons of current year data against prior years or industry benchmarks to identify areas of potential risk.

Five Components of Internal Control
According to the framework adopted by ISA 315, internal control consists of the following five components:

  1. The Control Environment: Includes the governance and management functions and the attitudes, awareness, and actions of management (e.g., integrity and ethical values).
  2. The Entity’s Risk Assessment Process: The process by which management identifies business risks relevant to financial reporting and decides how to manage them.
  3. The Information System: The procedures and records (both manual and IT) established to initiate, record, process, and report entity transactions.
  4. Control Activities: The policies and procedures that help ensure management directives are carried out (e.g., authorizations, reconciliations, and physical security).
  5. Monitoring of Controls: A process to assess the effectiveness of internal control performance over time, often involving internal audit functions.

Definition of Risk-Based Auditing
Risk-based auditing is an audit approach that focuses the auditor's efforts and resources on the areas of the financial statements that are most likely to contain material misstatements.

Instead of testing all balances and transactions equally, the auditor first assesses the risks (Inherent Risk and Control Risk) associated with the client’s business and systems. The nature, timing, and extent of audit procedures are then tailored to respond to these specific identified risks, ensuring that the audit is both efficient and effective in providing reasonable assurance.

3. Materiality

Materiality is a fundamental concept in both auditing and accounting. Framework for the preparation of financial statements states that information is material if its omission, misstatement or non-disclosure could influence economic decisions of users taken based on the financial statements.

Required:
State two instances which require the Auditor to apply the concept of materiality.
(2 marks)

Solution to Q1.3

Here are two specific instances where the auditor is required to apply the concept of materiality:

1. During the Planning Stage (Risk Assessment)
The auditor applies materiality when determining the nature, timing, and extent of audit procedures. By setting a materiality level at the beginning of the audit, the auditor can identify which account balances, classes of transactions, and disclosures are significant. This ensures that the audit focus is directed toward areas that could reasonably be expected to influence the economic decisions of users.

2. During the Evaluation Stage (Final Review)
The auditor applies materiality when evaluating the effect of identified misstatements on the audit and of uncorrected misstatements, if any, on the financial statements. At this stage, the auditor must decide whether the aggregate of uncorrected misstatements is material enough to require a modification of the audit opinion (i.e., whether the financial statements as a whole are "true and fair").

Question 2

A. Written Representations

Explain three purposes of, and two procedures for, obtaining written representations.
(5 marks)

Solution to Q2A

Three Purposes of Obtaining Written Representations

  1. To Confirm Management's Responsibilities: Written representations serve to obtain acknowledgement from management that it has fulfilled its responsibility for the preparation of the financial statements in accordance with the applicable financial reporting framework.
  2. To Support Other Audit Evidence: They are used to corroborate other audit evidence relevant to the financial statements or specific assertions when other sufficient appropriate audit evidence cannot reasonably be expected to exist (e.g., confirming management’s intention to hold a specific investment for the long term).
  3. To Document Management’s Responses to Inquiries: They provide a formal record of oral representations made by management during the course of the audit, ensuring there is no misunderstanding regarding material matters discussed.

Two Procedures for Obtaining Written Representations

  1. Requesting a Formal Representation Letter: The auditor formally requests that management (typically the CEO and CFO or directors) provide a signed letter addressed to the auditor that contains specific representations required by auditing standards.
  2. Reviewing Minutes of Meetings: The auditor can obtain written representations by reviewing and obtaining copies of relevant minutes of meetings of the board of directors or similar governance bodies where management has formally acknowledged its responsibilities or documented significant decisions.
B. Dinpa Plc — Acceptance of Appointment

Your firm has been approached by the board of directors of Dinpa Plc who would like to engage your firm as the company's auditors for the next financial year, ending on 31 December 2022. The directors feel that their current auditors do not give them a good service and they will be asked to resign.

Required:
What ethical and statutory matters would you consider and what investigations would you undertake before your firm could accept appointment and be appointed as auditors of Dinpa Plc?
(10 marks)

Solution to Q2B

Here are the ethical, statutory, and investigative considerations required before accepting the audit appointment for Dinpa Plc.

1. Ethical and Professional Considerations

  • Independence and Objectivity: The firm must evaluate whether there are any threats to independence (self-interest, self-review, advocacy, familiarity, or intimidation). If a threat exists, the firm must determine if safeguards can reduce it to an acceptable level.
  • Professional Competence and Due Care: The firm must ensure it has the necessary resources, time, and technical expertise to audit a "Plc" (Public Limited Company), which often involves more complex reporting requirements.
  • Integrity of Management: The auditor should consider the reputation of the directors of Dinpa Plc. If management lacks integrity, the risk of fraud and material misstatement is significantly higher.
  • Communication with Outgoing Auditors: In accordance with ethical codes, the firm must request permission from Dinpa Plc to communicate with the current auditors. This is to identify if there are any professional reasons why the appointment should not be accepted (e.g., unpaid fees or disagreements over accounting treatments).

2. Statutory and Legal Matters

  • Legality of Appointment: Ensure the appointment follows the local Companies Act (e.g., Act 179 in Ghana). This includes verifying that the previous auditors have been properly removed or have resigned in accordance with the law.
  • Preconditions for an Audit (ISA 210): The firm must confirm that an acceptable financial reporting framework is being used and that management acknowledges its responsibility for the financial statements and internal controls.
  • Conflicts of Interest: Check for any legal bars to the appointment, such as a partner of the audit firm being an officer or employee of Dinpa Plc.

3. Investigations and Procedures to Undertake

  • Client Identification (Anti-Money Laundering): Conduct "Know Your Client" (KYC) procedures to verify the identity of the directors and the ultimate beneficial owners of Dinpa Plc to comply with anti-money laundering regulations.
  • Review of Financial History: Inspect recent financial statements and any interim management accounts to assess the financial stability of the company and identify potential audit risks.
  • Direct Inquiries: Ask the directors why they feel the current auditors provide "poor service." This helps determine if the "poor service" is actually an auditor's refusal to agree to a questionable accounting treatment.
  • Professional Clearance: Send a formal letter to the existing auditors (after obtaining client consent) to ask if there are any facts that the firm should be aware of before accepting. If the client refuses to grant permission for this communication, the appointment should generally be declined.
  • Background Checks: Search for any news or legal records regarding the company to identify any ongoing litigation, regulatory breaches, or public scandals.

Summary of Action:
If the firm is satisfied that the risks are manageable and independence is maintained, it should issue an Engagement Letter to Dinpa Plc to document the terms of the engagement before work begins.

C. Dinpa Plc — Audit Committee

Dinpa Plc is seeking to get listed on the stock market and is aware that subsequent to the stock exchange listing, it will need to establish an audit committee and has asked for some advice in relation to this.

Required:
Explain five benefits to Dinpa Plc for establishing an audit committee.
(5 marks)

Solution to Q2C

Here are five benefits to Dinpa Plc for establishing an audit committee as they prepare for a stock market listing.

  1. Increased Confidence for Investors and Stakeholders: For a company seeking to get listed, an audit committee provides an objective and independent review of the company's financial reporting and internal controls. This transparency increases the confidence of potential investors, analysts, and the stock exchange in the reliability of the company’s financial statements.
  2. Enhanced Objectivity and Independence of the External Audit: The audit committee acts as a bridge between the external auditors and the board of directors. By overseeing the appointment, compensation, and work of the external auditors, the committee ensures that the auditors remain independent and are not pressured by executive management.
  3. Strengthened Internal Control Environment: The audit committee is responsible for reviewing the effectiveness of the company’s internal control systems and risk management frameworks. This oversight helps identify weaknesses early, reducing the risk of fraud or material errors that could damage the company's reputation post-listing.
  4. Improved Communication and Resolution of Disagreements: The committee provides a formal forum for resolving any disagreements between management and the external auditors regarding financial reporting. This ensures that technical accounting issues are handled professionally and objectively without compromising the integrity of the financial report.
  5. Support for the Internal Audit Function: The audit committee provides a direct reporting line for the internal audit department. This protects internal auditors from management influence, ensures their recommendations are taken seriously, and helps them focus on areas of significant risk to the organization.

Question 3

A. IFAC Code of Ethics — Five Fundamental Principles

The IFAC Code of ethics governs the practice of auditing to ensure that practitioners will act in the public interest. Furthermore, in an audit, many relevant auditing standards must be followed to perform work that will serve the needs of users of the Auditor's report.

Required:
Identify and explain each of the five fundamental principles of the Code of Ethics and conduct for professional audit practitioners.
(10 marks)

Solution to Q3A

The IFAC (International Federation of Accountants) Code of Ethics requires professional practitioners to comply with the following five fundamental principles:

  1. Integrity
    • Explanation: A professional auditor must be straightforward and honest in all professional and business relationships. Integrity implies fair dealing and truthfulness. An auditor should not be associated with reports or information that they believe contains materially false or misleading statements.
  2. Objectivity
    • Explanation: This principle requires auditors to not allow bias, conflict of interest, or undue influence of others to override professional or business judgments. The auditor must remain impartial when gathering and evaluating evidence to form an audit opinion.
  3. Professional Competence and Due Care
    • Explanation: Auditors have a continuing duty to maintain professional knowledge and skill at the level required to ensure that a client receives competent professional service based on current developments in practice, legislation, and techniques. "Due care" implies acting diligently and in accordance with applicable technical and professional standards.
  4. Confidentiality
    • Explanation: Practitioners must respect the confidentiality of information acquired as a result of professional and business relationships. They should not disclose any such information to third parties without proper and specific authority (unless there is a legal or professional right or duty to disclose) nor use the information for personal advantage.
  5. Professional Behavior
    • Explanation: This principle requires auditors to comply with relevant laws and regulations and avoid any conduct that the auditor knows or should know might discredit the profession. This includes avoiding exaggerated claims for the services they are able to offer or making disparaging references to the work of others.
B. ISA 570 — Going Concern

ISA 570: Going Concern guides auditors to ensure that an entity can continue to operate into the foreseeable future.

Required:
State two audit tests necessary to ascertain whether an entity is a going concern.
(4 marks)

Solution to Q3B

To determine if an entity can continue to operate into the foreseeable future, the auditor must perform procedures to confirm or dispel any significant doubt. Two necessary audit tests include:

  1. Analysis of Cash Flow and Profit Forecasts
    • Procedure: The auditor should analyze and discuss with management their cash flow, profit, and other relevant forecasts.
    • Objective: This test helps evaluate the reliability of the entity’s information system for generating such data and determines if there is adequate support for the underlying assumptions of the forecast. The auditor also compares prospective financial information from recent prior periods with historical results to assess accuracy.
  2. Review of Debentures, Loan Agreements, and Financing
    • Procedure: The auditor should review the terms of debentures and loan agreements to determine whether any have been breached.
    • Objective: This ascertains if the entity has the continued support of its lenders. Breaches in these agreements or an inability to re-negotiate expiring financing arrangements are critical indicators of going concern difficulties.

Additional Procedures Mentioned in Materials:

  • Review of Subsequent Events: Analyzing events occurring after the period end that may affect the entity's ability to continue operations.
  • Inquiry of Management regarding Plans for Future Action: Asking management about their plans to liquidate assets, borrow money, restructure debt, or increase capital to mitigate identified risks.
  • Reading Minutes of Meetings: Reviewing minutes from meetings of shareholders and those charged with governance for references to financing difficulties.
C. Auditor Appointment, Dismissal, and Remuneration — Companies Act 2019 (Act 992)

Auditor appointment, dismissal, and remuneration is regulated by Companies Act 2019, (Act, 992).

Required:
How are auditors of Limited Liability companies appointed?
(3 marks)

What may cause auditors to resign from their appointments?
(3 marks)

Solution to Q3C

Here are the relevant provisions of the Companies Act, 2019 (Act 992) regarding the appointment and resignation of auditors.

How are auditors of Limited Liability companies appointed?
Under Act 992, the appointment of auditors generally occurs through the following mechanisms:

  1. First Auditors (By Directors): The first auditors of a company may be appointed by the directors at any time before the first Annual General Meeting (AGM). They hold office until the conclusion of that meeting.
  2. General Appointment (By Shareholders): At each AGM, the company appoints an auditor to hold office from the conclusion of that meeting until the conclusion of the next AGM. This is typically done via an ordinary resolution by the shareholders.
  3. Casual Vacancy (By Directors): If a vacancy arises during the year (e.g., due to death or sudden resignation), the directors may appoint an auditor to fill the vacancy, subject to the approval of the members at the next general meeting.
  4. Automatic Re-appointment: In many jurisdictions under the Act, an existing auditor is often deemed re-appointed without a specific resolution unless they are no longer qualified, have resigned, or a resolution has been passed to appoint someone else.

What may cause auditors to resign from their appointments?
Auditors may choose to resign from their position for several professional, ethical, or practical reasons, including:

  1. Ethical Threats and Independence Issues: If a situation arises that creates an irreconcilable threat to the auditor’s independence (e.g., a conflict of interest or a close personal relationship with a director) and no safeguards can reduce the risk to an acceptable level.
  2. Disagreements with Management: Serious and persistent disagreements regarding accounting treatments, the scope of the audit, or the necessary disclosures in the financial statements.
  3. Integrity of Management: If the auditor discovers evidence of serious fraud or loses trust in the integrity of those charged with governance, they may resign because the audit risk becomes unacceptably high.
  4. Non-payment of Audit Fees: If the client consistently fails to pay professional fees for work already performed, the auditor may resign to avoid a self-interest threat.
  5. Inability to Obtain Sufficient Evidence: If management imposes severe limitations on the scope of the audit such that the auditor cannot form an opinion, they may decide that resignation is more appropriate than issuing a disclaimer.

Question 4

A. Financial Statement Assertions — Year-End Inventory

Identify and explain four financial statement assertions relevant to account balances at the year end; and
For each identified assertion, describe a substantive/verification procedure relevant to the audit of year-end inventory.
(8 marks)

Solution to Q4A

Here are the four financial statement assertions relevant to year-end account balances (specifically inventory) and their corresponding substantive procedures.

Assertion Explanation Substantive/Verification Procedure for Inventory
Existence This assertion means that assets, liabilities, and equity interests exist at the period end. Physical Inspection: Attend the year-end inventory count and physically inspect items to ensure that the inventory recorded in the books is actually present in the warehouse.
Rights and Obligations This means that the entity holds or controls the rights to assets, and liabilities are the obligations of the entity. Documentation Review: Inspect purchase invoices and shipping documents to verify that the inventory is legally owned by the company and is not held on consignment for a third party.
Completeness This ensures that all assets, liabilities, and equity interests that should have been recorded have been recorded. Cut-off Testing: Select a sample of goods received notes (GRNs) just before the year-end and trace them to the inventory records to ensure that all received goods have been included in the final inventory balance.
Valuation and Allocation This means that assets, liabilities, and equity interests are included in the financial statements at appropriate amounts and any resulting valuation adjustments are properly recorded. Cost vs. NRV Test: Compare the cost of inventory items (from purchase invoices) to their subsequent selling prices (from post-year-end sales invoices) to ensure inventory is valued at the lower of cost and net realizable value (NRV).

Summary of Audit Approach
When auditing year-end balances like inventory, the auditor focuses on these "balance sheet" assertions to ensure that the position stated in the Statement of Financial Position is accurate. While Existence is often the primary concern for assets to prevent overstatement, Completeness is equally vital to ensure the financial statements are not understated.

B. Quality International Plc (QIL) — Internal Controls

Quality International Plc (QIL) is a manufacturer of electrical equipment. It has factories across the country and its customer base includes retailers as well as individuals, to whom direct sales are made through their website. The company's year end is 30 September 2013. You are an audit supervisor of Tackie & Co (the external auditors of QIL) and are currently reviewing documentation of QIL's internal control in preparation for the interim audit. QIL's website allows individuals to order goods directly, and full payment is taken in advance. Currently the website is not integrated into the inventory system and inventory levels are not checked at the time when orders are placed.

Goods are despatched via local couriers; however, they do not always record customer signatures as proof that the customer has received the goods. Over the past 12 months there have been customer complaints about the delay between sales orders and receipt of goods. QIL has investigated these and found that, in each case, the sales order had been entered into the sales system correctly but was not forwarded to the despatch department for fulfilling.

QIL's retail customers undergo credit checks prior to being accepted and credit limits are set accordingly by sales ledger clerks. These customers place their orders through one of the sales team, who decides on sales discount levels. Raw materials used in the manufacturing process are purchased from a wide range of suppliers. As a result of staff changes in the purchase ledger department, supplier statement reconciliations are no longer performed. Additionally, changes to supplier details in the purchase ledger master file can be undertaken by purchase ledger clerks as well as supervisors.

In the past six months QIL has changed part of its manufacturing process and as a result some new equipment has been purchased; however, there are considerable levels of plant and equipment which are now surplus to requirement. Purchase requisitions for all new equipment have been authorised by production supervisors and little has been done to reduce the surplus of old equipment.

Required — In respect of the internal control of QIL:
Identify and explain four deficiencies;
Recommend a control procedure to address each of these deficiencies; and
Describe a test of control Tackie & Co would perform to assess if each of these controls is operating effectively.
(12 marks)

Solution to Q4B

Internal Control Report: Quality International Plc (QIL)

Identified Deficiency Control Recommendation Test of Control
Website not integrated with inventory: The website does not check inventory levels at the time of order. This leads to accepting orders and payments for out-of-stock items, causing delays and dissatisfaction. The website should be integrated with the inventory system to provide real-time stock availability. If an item is out of stock, the system should prevent the order or provide an expected delivery date. Place a "test order" on the website for an item known to be out of stock and verify that the system either prevents the sale or issues an appropriate warning.
Lack of Proof of Delivery: Couriers do not always obtain customer signatures. This makes it difficult for QIL to prove goods were received, leading to potential revenue loss through fraudulent non-delivery claims. QIL should mandate that couriers obtain a digital or physical signature for all deliveries. Despatch staff should periodically review delivery reports to ensure signatures are captured. Select a sample of despatched orders and inspect the corresponding courier delivery notes/records for a valid customer signature or proof of receipt.
Sales Orders not Forwarded: Orders are entered correctly but are not consistently forwarded to the despatch department, leading to significant delays and customer complaints. The system should automatically generate a sequential "Pending Despatch" report. Management should review this daily to ensure all entered orders have been moved to the warehouse for fulfillment. Observe the daily review process of the "Pending Despatch" report and inspect a sample of reports for evidence of management’s signature or review mark.
Lack of Supplier Statement Reconciliations: Due to staff changes, these reconciliations are no longer performed. This increases the risk of errors in the purchase ledger, double payments, or missed liabilities. Supplier statement reconciliations should be performed monthly by a staff member independent of the purchase ledger clerks. Any discrepancies should be investigated and cleared promptly. Request the folder containing monthly supplier reconciliations and inspect a sample to ensure they have been completed, signed, and reviewed by a supervisor.
Unauthorized Access to Master File: Purchase ledger clerks (as well as supervisors) can change supplier details in the master file. This creates a fraud risk where a clerk could change a supplier's bank details to their own. Access to the purchase ledger master file should be restricted to supervisors only. Any changes made should be logged by the system and reviewed by an independent manager. Attempt to access and change a supplier's bank details using a "clerk-level" login to verify that access is restricted. Review the "audit trail" report for unauthorized changes.
Surplus Plant and Equipment: Old equipment is not being disposed of, and production supervisors are authorizing new purchases without higher-level oversight. This leads to inefficient use of capital and potential impairment issues. All capital expenditure requisitions should be authorized by a senior manager (e.g., Production Director). A formal review of surplus assets should be conducted to facilitate disposal or scrap. Inspect a sample of capital purchase requisitions to verify they were signed by the designated senior manager and check for evidence of a recent "surplus asset" review meeting.

Summary for Audit Committee
The current control environment at QIL shows significant weaknesses in the Information System and Control Activities components of internal control. Addressing the lack of system integration and the lack of segregation of duties in the purchase ledger should be prioritized to prevent fraud and operational inefficiencies.
