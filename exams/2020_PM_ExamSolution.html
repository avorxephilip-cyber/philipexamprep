QUESTION 3

Part a.. All projects are temporary in nature. Discuss [[4 Marks]]
This statement is fundamentally correct. A project is defined as a temporary planned endeavor — all project activities must be commenced and completed within a specific period (specific start and end point).
However, 'temporary' does NOT mean: (i) the project duration is always short — some projects span years; or (ii) the product or outcome created is temporary — a bridge built by a project may stand for a century. What is temporary is the project's engagement and team formation. Projects end when: objectives are fully achieved; objectives cannot or will not be met; the need for the project no longer exists; or sponsors withdraw financial support.
Therefore, the temporary nature of a project distinguishes it from ongoing operations, which are repetitive and continuous. Each project has a definite beginning and a definite end.
(Source: Unit 1, Session 1 — Nature of a Project, p.3)
Part b.. Explain any six (6) characteristics of a project [[6 Marks]]
Objective to Achieve: Every project is an ad hoc endeavor with a specific goal to be achieved.
Schedule to Closure: Every project has a temporal life cycle with a specific time to accomplish each activity in the project life cycle.
Complexity: Every project has its level of complexity, requiring specific existing technology to achieve the project's objectives.
Resource Needs: Every project requires specific labour, personnel, equipment, materials, and budget to ensure successful closure.
Organizational Structure: Every project has a management structure providing decisions on information controls, budget, scheduling, quality standards, and overall direction.
Risk and Safety Controls: Every project must have risk management and safety controls to comply with both international and local standards depending on the location and stakeholder requirements.
(Source: Unit 1, Session 1 — Characteristics of a Project, pp.4–5)
Part c.. With the aid of a diagram, explain the three (3) most important traditional constraints of project management [[6 Marks]]
The three most important traditional constraints are the Triple Constraints — Scope, Cost (Budget), and Schedule (Time):
Scope: Defines all the work that must be performed to deliver the project successfully. Expanding scope without adjusting time or cost will compromise quality and lead to project failure.
Cost (Budget): The approved budget allocated for the project. Cost overruns occur when scope expands or time is extended without corresponding budget increases.
Schedule (Time): The planned timeline for project completion. Time is the most visible indicator of project success — delays are immediately recognizable.
DIAGRAM: [Triangle — Scope (top vertex), Cost/Budget (bottom-left vertex), Schedule/Time (bottom-right vertex), Quality in the centre]
These constraints are interdependent: change in one affects the others. Modern project management also recognizes quality, resources, and risk as additional constraints.
(Source: Unit 1, Session 2 — Constraints to Project Management, p.8)

QUESTION 4

Part a.. State two (2) importance of human resource planning in project management [[4 Marks]]
Identifies Roles and Responsibilities: HR planning identifies the specific roles and responsibilities required to achieve the aim of a project. This gives the project leader clarity on the calibre of people to recruit, making them task and goal-oriented.
Sets Skills Standards: HR planning sets the standard of skills and intellectual capacity needed in prospective project team members. It prevents mismatching of qualifications and guides the recruitment method.
(Source: Unit 4, Session 2 — Importance of Human Resource Planning, p.94)
Part b.. Explain any four (4) functions performed by the project manager in managing and leading project teams [[8 Marks]]
Influencing the Team: The project manager influences human resource factors impacting the project — team environment, geographical locations, cultural issues, and internal/external politics — to maintain project performance.
Building Trust: The project manager builds trust by persuading team members to ask questions and seek guidance from each other, fostering familiarity and comfort essential for high team performance.
Performance Appraisals: The project manager uses appraisals to provide feedback to team members, allowing individual reflection and progressive improvement that benefits the entire team in reaching objectives.
Conflict Management: The project manager resolves inevitable conflicts arising from scarce resources, scheduling priorities, and personal work styles using techniques including: withdraw/avoid, smooth/accommodate, compromise/reconcile, force/direct, and collaborate/problem solve.
(Source: Unit 4, Session 3 — Manage Project Team, pp.100–101)
Part c.. State the steps to consider in establishing the project risk context [[8 Marks]]
Step 1: Establish the organizational and project environment in which the risk assessment is taking place.
Step 2: Specify the main objectives and outcomes required for the project.
Step 3: Identify a set of success criteria against which the consequences of identified risks can be measured.
Step 4: Define a set of key elements for structuring the risk identification and assessment process.
Context inputs include: project execution strategy, project charter, cost and schedule assumptions, scope definitions, engineering designs, economic analyses, and other relevant project documentation.
The output is: a concise statement of project objectives, specific success criteria, objectives and scope for the risk assessment, and key elements for structuring the risk identification process.
(Source: Unit 6, Session 2 — Establish the Risk Context, p.153)
