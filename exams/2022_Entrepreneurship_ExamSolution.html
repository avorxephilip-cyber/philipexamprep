Question 1(a): With the Aid of a Diagram, Explain the Entrepreneurial Process [8 marks]

Model Answer:

Every entrepreneurial venture is different in its particulars, but there is a common process that underlies each. The basic process involves four contingencies coming together. The diagram below illustrates the entrepreneurial process (Wickham, 2007):

(Source: Wickham, 2007)

The four central components are:

The Entrepreneur

The individual or team who leads and develops the venture. The entrepreneur is responsible for identifying, evaluating, and analysing new opportunities; attracting and managing resources; and leading and directing the organisation.

Opportunity

A gap in the market that presents the possibility of doing something new, different, and better from the perspective of buyers, thereby creating new value.

Organisation

A system devised to co-ordinate the activities of people who make up the venture and the use of its resources. The organisation gives structure to how the venture operates.

Resources

The entrepreneur brings resources together to build the venture and pursue the opportunity. These include financial, physical, human, technological, and organisational resources.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 3 — The Entrepreneurial Process, pp.13–14)
Question 1(b): Mention and Explain Four Components of Sociological Approaches Used to Examine the Entrepreneurial Process [8 marks]

Model Answer:

The sociological approach tries to explain the social conditions from which entrepreneurs emerge and the social factors that influence the decision to become an entrepreneur. There are four components:

Negative Displacement

Refers to the marginalization of individuals or groups from the core of society. People who find themselves displaced in some negative way — those seen as 'not fitting in' to the main flow of social and economic life — may become entrepreneurs as they seek alternative means of livelihood.

'Between Things'

People who are between things — such as migrants who have moved from one location to another, or politicians who have left office — are more likely to seek entrepreneurial outlets than those who are settled in mainstream life.

Positive Pull

Positive influences that lead to entrepreneurship, coming from a potential partner, a mentor, a parent, a customer, or an investor. For example, a potential partner encourages the individual with the offer of sharing experience, helping with the work, and spreading the risk.

Positive Push

Positive push factors include career paths that offer entrepreneurial opportunities or education that gives the individual appropriate knowledge and opportunity. These factors push the individual toward entrepreneurship from within their own circumstances.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 3 — The Entrepreneurial Process, pp.14–16)
Question 1(c): Differentiate Between Internal and External Locus of Control [4 marks]

Model Answer:

Locus of control is a psychological trait associated with entrepreneurship. In locus-of-control theory, there are two types of people:

External Locus of Control: Externals are people who believe that what happens to them is a result of fate, chance, luck, or forces beyond their control. They do not believe they can significantly influence outcomes through their own effort. Such individuals are less likely to pursue entrepreneurship since they do not believe their actions will determine success.

Internal Locus of Control: Internals are people who believe that for the most part the future is theirs to control through their own effort. Entrepreneurs predominantly have an internal locus of control — they believe that their effort will have something to do with the business's future performance. This belief is essential for undertaking a new business.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 3 — The Entrepreneurial Process, p.14)

Question 2(b): Highlight Two Marketing Mix Activities Likely to Occur at Each Level of the Product Life Cycle [10 marks]

Model Answer:

Introduction Stage: (i) Intensive promotional advertising to create awareness; (ii) Distribution limited to selective outlets while demand builds.

Growth Stage: (i) Expanding distribution channels to reach more customers; (ii) Targeted brand advertising to build loyalty and attract repeat buyers.

Maturity Stage: (i) Price reductions to remain competitive as rivals enter the market; (ii) Product differentiation strategies (e.g., new features, improved packaging) to retain market share.

Decline Stage: (i) Selective or reduced distribution — withdrawing from unprofitable markets; (ii) Cost-cutting in production and marketing while planning product replacement.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 5, Session 5 — Product and Price Strategies, pp.152–153)
Question 3(A): Discuss Five Vital Roles of Business Financing Towards the Growth of Businesses [10 marks]

Model Answer:

Finance is often described as 'the life blood of a business' and plays the following five vital roles in business growth:

Start-Up Finance

Finance is needed to start a business — to purchase assets, raw materials, employ people, and cover initial running costs. Without adequate start-up finance, a business idea cannot be translated into a viable venture.

Business Expansion

As a business grows, it needs higher capacity and new technology to cut unit costs and keep up with competitors. Finance enables expansion projects such as acquiring new premises, additional equipment, or new staff.

Developing and Marketing New Products

Firms need funds for research, development, and marketing of new products. Where competitors constantly update their offerings, adequate finance is necessary for survival and continued growth.

Entering New Markets

When a business seeks to sell its products in new geographical areas or to new customer segments, finance is required for market research, advertising campaigns, and setting up distribution outlets in those markets.

Covering Day-to-Day Operating Expenses

A business needs cash on a day-to-day basis to cover operating expenses, from paying suppliers of raw materials to routine administrative costs. Without this working capital, a growing business may face liquidity crises.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 6, Session 1 — Nature and Significance of Business Finance, pp.167–168)
Question 3(B): State and Explain Five Factors that May Influence the Choice of Finance for Small Businesses [10 marks]

Model Answer:

Amount of Money Required

Large amounts are not available through some sources, while others do not offer the flexibility needed for smaller amounts. The scale of financing needed is a key determinant of which source is appropriate.

How Quickly the Money is Needed

The urgency of the financial need affects which sources are available and at what cost. Spending more time sourcing finance generally results in cheaper rates.

The Cheapest Option Available

The cost of finance is typically measured by the interest charged on borrowed funds. The cheapest form of money comes from the firm's own trading profits, followed by retained earnings, and then external sources.

The Amount of Risk Involved

Higher-risk ventures may not attract external financing without guarantees. Potential lenders assess the probability of the venture generating enough profit to repay the loan, and higher risk generally leads to higher interest rates or rejection.

The Length of Time Finance is Needed

Whether the finance is needed for a long-term project or a short-term need should determine the type of finance used. Long-term projects are better matched with long-term sources (e.g. equity or debentures) while short-term needs are matched with overdrafts or trade credit.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 6, Session 1 — Factors that Influence the Choice of Finance, pp.168–169)
Question 4(A): Identify Two Recent Technological Advances Beneficial for Small Business Growth [2 marks]

Model Answer:

Information and Communications Technology (ICT) / Internet: ICT has surpassed its traditional role (word processing) and now plays a more central role in small businesses. Small firms use ICT for accounting, budgeting, inventory control, spreadsheet analysis, and e-commerce.

GPS and Tracking Technologies: Initially developed for the automotive industry, GPS-like systems are now being applied to healthcare and delivery logistics, enabling small firms to track assets and improve service delivery.

Question 4(C): Explain Five Importance of the Technological Resource [10 marks]

Model Answer:

Enables Firms to Run Their Business More Efficiently

Technological resources serve as tools to help run a business. Small firms now use ICT for accounting, budgeting, word processing, inventory control, and spreadsheet analysis, reducing manual effort and improving accuracy.

Sustains Competitive Advantage

Technological resources, if protected by patents, make it difficult for competitors to imitate. Firms that possess proprietary technological resources are able to maintain their competitive advantage over time.

Encourages Innovation and Adaptability

Firms are now adaptive and apply technological expertise to different industry segments. For example, patents initially developed for the automotive industry are being applied to the medical industry, demonstrating how technological resources enable cross-industry innovation.

Improves Communication and Market Reach

ICT has enabled small firms to communicate more effectively with customers, suppliers, and partners. E-commerce and digital marketing have given small firms access to markets previously only accessible to large corporations.

Reduces Costs Through Sophisticated but Affordable Tools

The general trend away from costly mainframe computing toward cheaper, user-friendly, standard software packages means that sophisticated tools are increasingly available to small firms. This reduces the cost of operations and levels the playing field with larger competitors.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 5 — Technological Resources, pp.126–127)

ADDITIONAL QUESTIONS (2020A — Questions 3 & 4)

Q3(a): Identify and Explain the Stages Involved in the Marketing Research Process [8 marks]

Model Answer:

Define the Problem

The first step is defining the research problem clearly and briefly. A well-defined problem gives direction to the entire research effort and helps in selecting the appropriate data collection methods.

Collect Data

Data is collected from internal sources (sales records, mailing lists, complaint files) and external sources. External data includes secondary data (census reports, magazines, trade publications) and primary data collected through personal interviews, telephone interviews, mail surveys, or focus groups.

Analyse and Interpret the Data

The collected data is evaluated and interpreted in response to the research objectives. The owner summarizes answers and cross-tabulates data to produce focused results. Judgment and common sense are used to determine what the numbers mean.

Draw Conclusions

Based on the understanding of what the facts mean, the owner decides how to use the information in their business. The marketing research process is not complete until the business owner acts on the information collected.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 5, Session 2 — Marketing Research, pp.139–142)
Q3(b): Explain Four Types of Business Informational Resources Used by Entrepreneurs [8 marks]

Model Answer:

Market Information

A huge and complex field — market information is now a major commodity in its own right. Firms depend on it for new product development, market planning, communications, advertising, and export decisions. Information is typically sought on market size, growth, consumer spending power, and consumer behaviour.

Competitor Information

Firms gather information on where competitors are, what they are doing, and their performance. Accurate competitor information is difficult to collect but essential for strategic planning and maintaining competitive position.

Supplier Information

Focuses on cost, reliability, quality, and speed of delivery. Supplier information helps businesses plan production activities and assess whether necessary raw materials can be sourced to meet lead times.

External Financial Information

Companies require a large range of information on issues such as currency rates, share price movements, capital market trends, and the sources and types of finance available. This is especially critical for businesses with complex financial structures or investment portfolios.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 3 — Business Informational Resources, pp.113–115)
Q3(c): Evaluate Two Strategies Firms Can Use in Utilising Resources [4 marks]

Model Answer:

Controlling Rather Than Owning Resources

Entrepreneurs seek to use the minimum possible amount of all types of resources at each stage of the venture's growth. They seek to control resources rather than own them. Advantages include: less capital required, greater flexibility, lower sunk costs, and reduced risk of obsolescence.

Diversifying Sources of Support

Firms will be less vulnerable if they have many sources of support. If one source is discontinued, the firm can fall back on others. Diversification of resource sources also reduces dependency risk and can result in more cost-effective resource acquisition.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 6 — Resource Mobilisation, pp.130–132)
Q4(a): Examine the Possible Disadvantages of Buying an Existing Business [6 marks]

Model Answer:

Potential Ill Will

The previous owner may have created ill will. The business may look great on the surface, but customers, suppliers, creditors, or employees might have extremely negative feelings about it.

Buying Possible Liabilities With Assets

Even if only assets are purchased, liabilities can still be attached to them — for example, employee liabilities which may not be recognized at the time of purchase.

Equipment May Be Obsolete or Inefficient

The equipment may have been suited to the previous business model but not to what the new owner plans to build. Modernizing equipment and facilities is usually expensive.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 5 — Importance of Buying an Existing Business, pp.58–59)
Q4(b): Describe the Different Types of Franchising Available to a Would-Be Entrepreneur [6 marks]

Model Answer:

Trade Name Franchising

Involves a brand name, such as Budget Rent-a-Car. The franchisee buys the right to become identified with the franchisor's trade name without distributing particular products exclusively under the franchisor's name.

Product Distribution Franchising

The franchisee is licensed to sell specific products under the manufacturer's (franchisor's) brand name and trademark via a selective, limited distribution network. Also referred to as dealerships — commonly used in automobiles (Toyota), soft drinks (Coca-Cola), gasoline (Shell), and similar sectors.

Pure (Business Format) Franchising

Provides the franchisee with a complete business format, including a license for a trade name, products or services, physical plant, methods of operation, marketing strategy plan, quality control process, two-way communication systems, and necessary business services. Common among fast-food restaurants and car rental agencies.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 6 — Franchising, pp.61–63)
Q4(c): Discuss Two Advantages and Two Disadvantages of Franchising to the Franchisee [8 marks]

Model Answer:

Advantages:

Product Acceptance: The franchisee enters a business with an established name and proven product/service, removing the need to spend resources building credibility in the marketplace.

Management Training and Support: The franchisor provides comprehensive managerial training programs and follow-up counselling to help the franchisee operate successfully.

Disadvantages:

Franchise Fees and Profit Sharing: Large up-front fees and ongoing royalties can be financially burdensome. The franchisee must share revenues with the franchisor in return for using the name and system.

Lack of Independence: The franchise owner does not have the autonomy of an independent owner and must adhere to the franchisor's operational and structural control standards to protect the public image of the brand.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 6 — Franchising, pp.63–64)
