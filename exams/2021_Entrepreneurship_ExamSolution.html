2021 EXAMINATION — SECTION B

Question 1 — Case Study: Nana Yaw Poku (Rex Athletes Shop context)
Part I: Social Factor that Influenced Mr. Poku into Entrepreneurship [3 marks]
The social factor that influenced Mr. Poku is Positive Pull. This is because Mr. Poku was influenced by a mentor — Mr. Kofi Kyereh — who through mentorship, advice, and inspiration motivated him to pursue entrepreneurship. Positive pull refers to positive influences from a potential partner, a mentor, a parent, or customer that lead an individual toward entrepreneurship. Mr. Kyereh acted as a mentor who pulled Mr. Poku toward starting his own business.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 3 — The Entrepreneurial Process, p.15)
Part II: Option of Small Business Acquisition Between Mr. Poku and Mr. Prah [3 marks]
The option of small business acquisition that transpired is Buy-in / Buy-in Management Buy-out (BIMBO). Mr. Poku combined resources with Mr. Prah (the existing General Manager) and took over the ownership of the Cocoa Tea Company Ltd from the existing owners. This reflects a BIMBO arrangement because it combines an outside investor (Mr. Poku) with an insider (Mr. Prah, the existing management) in the purchase of the firm.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 4 — Opportunities for Buying Existing Business, pp.53–54)
Part III: One Advantage of BIMBO over Other Options [2 marks]
The BIMBO minimizes the risk of buying into a firm from outside because the existing management of the firm (in this case Mr. Prah) are also involved. The insider knowledge of the existing management reduces information asymmetry and the risk of inheriting hidden liabilities or problems not visible to the outside buyer.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 4 — Opportunities for Buying Existing Business, p.54)
Part IV: Type of Entrepreneur Mr. Poku Can Be Classified As [2 marks]
Mr. Poku can be classified as a Serial Entrepreneur. This is because he owns and manages six different businesses, which is the defining characteristic of a serial entrepreneur — someone who continuously starts, grows, and manages multiple businesses over time. Having overcome initial challenges with generating ideas and funding, he now runs multiple ventures.
Question 1(B): Examine Five Logical and Methodological Steps in Buying a New Business [10 marks]

Model Answer:

Buying an existing business should not be approached haphazardly in view of the risk involved. A would-be business owner should follow a logical, methodical approach (Owens, 1990):

Analyse Your Skills, Abilities and Interests

Conduct a self-audit to determine what kind(s) of businesses you should consider. An honest assessment of personal strengths, weaknesses, and interests ensures you pursue a business that fits your capabilities.

Prepare a List of Potential Candidates

Search for businesses for sale through newspapers, trade journals, industry contacts (suppliers, distributors, customers), investment bankers, business brokers, and networking through social and business contacts.

Investigate and Evaluate the Best Candidates

Investigate candidates in terms of the company's strengths and weaknesses, profitability and overall financial condition, cash flow cycle, key competitors, customer base, suitability of current employees, and physical condition of the business.

Explore Financing Options

After placing a value on the business, explore financing the purchase. A deal is typically structured so that the buyer makes a down payment to the seller, who then finances a note for the balance with principal and interest payments over time.

Ensure a Smooth Transition

Once a deal is struck, focus on communicating with employees, being honest with them, listening to their suggestions (as they have intimate knowledge of the business), and considering asking the seller to serve as a consultant during the transition.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 4 — The Right Way to Buy a Business, pp.54–55)

Model Answer:

See detailed answer under 2020 Q1(c) above — the same five procurement steps apply. In summary:

Department initiates a purchase requisition

Purchasing staff verifies specifications and selects potential suppliers

Question 2(B): Discuss Four Technological Resources Available to Small Firms [10 marks]

Model Answer:

People

The most important resource people have is ideas and skills. People do the work, make products and services, and drive technological innovation. In a small firm, the skills and creativity of the entrepreneur and staff are a key technological resource.

Information

Information is a commodity that is bought, sold, given away, and traded. It is essential to technological problem-solving. Sources range from books to the internet, and firms now rely heavily on information technologies for accounting, budgeting, and inventory control.

Tools and Machines

Tools and machines — including computers and software — are critical technological resources. Technical skills encompass both the know-how to use a tool and the know-how to apply it to a useful purpose. Small firms use tools ranging from production machinery to digital software.

Knowledge

Knowledge is built through practical experience and by acquiring and processing information. It encompasses technical know-how and skills that exist only in people's minds. Firms protect knowledge through patents, trademarks, and proprietary processes, making it a strategic technological resource.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 5 — Technological Resources, pp.125–126)
Question 3(A): Discuss the Key Activities Involved in Human Resource Management [14 marks]

Model Answer:

Human Resource Management (HRM) is the strategic and coherent approach to managing an organisation's most valued assets — the people who work in the firm and contribute to the achievement of its objectives. The key activities involved are:

Human Resource Planning

Designed to ensure that personnel needs will be constantly and appropriately met. It involves analysis of internal factors (skill needs, vacancies, departmental changes) and external environmental factors (labour market). Planning covers future needs, future balance of staff, recruiting or laying off, and developing employees.

Recruitment

Concerned with developing a pool of job candidates in line with the HR plan. Candidates are located through newspaper and professional journal advertisements, employment agencies, word of mouth, and visits to campuses. Recruitment begins with job analysis.

Selection

Involves evaluating and screening job candidates using application forms, interviews, employment and skills tests, and reference checks. Selection steps include: completing job application, initial screening interview, testing, background investigation, in-depth interview, physical examination, and job offer.

Socialization

Designed to help selected individuals fit smoothly into the organisation. New employees are introduced to colleagues, acquainted with responsibilities, and informed about goals, policies, and expectations. It conveys information on daily routines, the organisation's history and purpose, and policies and benefits.

Training and Development

Aims to increase employees' ability to contribute to organisational effectiveness. Training is designed to improve skills in the current job. Needs are determined through performance appraisal, job requirement analysis, organisation analysis, and human resource surveys.

Performance Appraisal

Compares an individual's job performance to standards or objectives developed for the position. Low performance may prompt corrective action (additional training, demotion, or separation), while high performance may merit a bonus or promotion.

Promotions, Transfers, Demotions, and Separations

These reflect an employee's value to the organisation. High performers may be promoted or transferred to develop their skills, while low performers may be demoted, transferred to less important positions, or separated from the organisation.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 4 — Human Resource, pp.119–124)
Question 3(B): Discuss Three Sources of Short-Term Business Finance Available to Small Businesses [6 marks]

Model Answer:

Bank Overdraft

Probably the most available and appropriate source of short-term borrowing. The bank allows the borrower to overdraw their account up to a specified limit. Interest is calculated on the daily outstanding balance, so no interest is paid on any unutilized portion. It is repayable on demand and useful for financing working capital.

Factoring

Factoring involves raising funds on the security of the company's debts so that cash is received earlier than waiting for debtors to pay. It is particularly attractive to new businesses facing substantial growth in sales. An additional advantage is possible savings in staff and paperwork associated with maintaining accounts and monitoring debtors.

Trade Credit

An excellent source of low- or no-cost money. Suppliers may extend interest-free credit on purchases of goods or services to well-established customers, meaning the business can order, take delivery, and sell an item before paying for it. This acts as an interest-free loan.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 6, Session 3 — Short Term Business Finance, pp.177–179)
Question 4(A): State and Explain Five Critical Factors to Consider at Pre-Start-Up and Start-Up Phases of New Venture Development [10 marks]

Model Answer:

Is the Business Concept Proprietary?

The product/service should be sufficiently proprietary to permit a long head start against competitors and a period of extraordinary early profits to offset start-up costs. This does not require a formal patent but requires a degree of differentiation.

Are the Initial Production and Marketing Costs Realistic?

Most estimates of initial costs tend to be too low. A careful, detailed analysis should be made so no large unexpected expenses arise. The venture must identify target markets, market channels, and promotional strategy.

Does the Approach Have Potential for High Margins?

Gross margins are a key consideration for investors and funding institutions. Without potential for strong margins, securing funding can be very difficult for a new venture.

Is the Potential Market Large Enough?

In determining market potential, entrepreneurs must look three to five years into the future because some markets take this long to emerge. A viable market size is essential to justify the investment and attract external financing.

Is the Time Required to Reach Market and Break Even Realistic?

In most cases, the faster the better. The entire venture plan is tied to this timeline and an error here can cause serious problems later. The entrepreneur must set a realistic break-even timeline that is consistent with the available resources.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 3 — New Business Assessment, pp.45–47)
Question 4(B): State and Explain Four Methods of Generating Business Ideas [10 marks]

Model Answer:

Observation of the Environment

Entrepreneurs generate ideas by observing daily life, identifying unmet needs, inefficiencies, or problems in existing products and services. A keen observer notices gaps in the market that others overlook and considers whether a business opportunity exists to fill that gap.

Education and Prior Work Experience

Many business ideas emerge from an individual's educational background or prior work experience. Professionals who identify problems in their industry, or who acquire specialized knowledge, are well-positioned to start businesses that address those specific needs.

Brainstorming

A creative group process in which individuals generate a large number of ideas without initially evaluating them. The goal is to produce as many ideas as possible in a short time. It encourages free thinking and builds on others' suggestions, producing innovative combinations.

Mentorship and Social Influence

Exposure to successful entrepreneurs through mentors, role models, family members, or social networks can inspire business ideas. Positive pull from a mentor or partner who shares knowledge and experience often catalyzes the generation and validation of new ideas.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1 & Unit 2 — Entrepreneurship and Starting a Business)
