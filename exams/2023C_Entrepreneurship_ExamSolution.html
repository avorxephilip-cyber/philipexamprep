2023C Entrepreneurship Exam Solutions

2023C EXAMINATION — SECTION B & C

Case Summary: Woarabeba Shito was founded in November 2019 by a female CEO who lost her mother to diabetes/stroke and herself suffered stomach ulcer. She created a nutritious, long-lasting, homemade black pepper sauce for vegans, ulcer patients, meat lovers, sea food lovers, students, workers and households. The business is registered as a Sole Proprietorship (September 2020), employs 10 young people, and uses social media marketing. Vision: to be the leading brand in nutritious, healthy pepper sauce around the world.

SECTION B — QUESTION ONE (Case Study: Woarabeba Shito)
Part A(i): Mission Statement of Woarabeba Shito [1 mark]
The mission statement of Woarabeba Shito is: "We prepare nutritious and delicious long-lasting pepper sauce, under strict hygienic condition for vegans, ulcer patients, students, workers and households in Ghana to reduce malnutrition, diseases, stomach ulcer, and the risk of stroke."
(Source: Directly quoted from the case study, Woarabeba Shito mission statement)
Part A(ii): Vision Statement of Woarabeba Shito [1 mark]
The vision statement is: "To be the leading brand in nutritious and delicious healthy pepper sauce around the world."
(Source: Directly from the Woarabeba Shito case study)
Part A(iii): Four Core Entrepreneurial Attributes/Values of Woarabeba Shito [2 marks]
The four core entrepreneurial attributes/values of Woarabeba Shito are:
Perseverance: To work harder to get the products on the shelves of every supermarket in the world.
Reliability: To be trusted and highly dependable by customers.
Consistency: To consistently maintain product quality.
Integrity: To demonstrate professionalism in every undertaking.
(Source: Woarabeba Shito case study — core values section)
Part B(i): One Economic Impact of Woarabeba Shito [1 mark]
Woarabeba Shito creates employment — it currently employs ten (10) young people in production and distribution of its products, contributing to the reduction of unemployment and increasing household incomes in the economy.
(Source: Woarabeba Shito case study; supported by CoDEUCC Textbook, Unit 1, Session 5 — Advantages of Entrepreneurship: Create Employment, p.22)
Part B(ii): Three Social Impacts of Woarabeba Shito [3 marks]
Educational Support: Part of the proceeds from the business is used to cater for the education of five school dropouts at the primary level and a needy but brilliant student at KNUST. Single parents who face financial difficulties are also being supported.
Support for Female Empowerment: The business looks forward to supporting females to further their education and equip those who opt for technical skills through apprenticeship in interested fields.
Health and Nutrition Improvement: By providing a nutritious alternative to conventional spicy sauces, Woarabeba Shito helps reduce malnutrition, diseases, stomach ulcer, and the risk of stroke among consumers — contributing positively to community health.
(Source: Woarabeba Shito case study; supported by CoDEUCC Textbook, Unit 1, Session 6 — Role of Small Businesses, p.27)
Part C(i): Four Challenges Facing Woarabeba Shito [2 marks]
Financial Planning Difficulties: Frequent increases in prices of raw materials make financial planning very difficult, leading to frequent price increases of the product.
Securing a Production Location: It has been a major challenge to secure a permanent production location in Cape Coast where most of the product is sold.
Lack of Skilled Social Media Personnel: Social media advancement calls for highly competent personnel who can intensify advertisement to increase sales, but the business lacks such personnel.
Inadequate Funds for Equipment and Raw Materials: The business lacks equipment such as sterilizers, industrial blenders, filling machines, and packaging boxes. It also lacks a motor bicycle and personal transport vehicle, causing delays in delivery services.
(Source: Woarabeba Shito case study — challenges section)
Part C(ii): Suggested Solutions to Each Challenge [4 marks]

Solution to Financial Planning Difficulties: Apply to institutional support bodies such as the NBSSI or the Venture Capital Trust Fund (VCTF) for affordable financing. Also, purchasing raw materials in bulk (forward buying) could stabilize input costs.

Solution to Production Location: Explore leasing options for a production facility, which avoids the high capital outlay of purchase. Asset leasing allows businesses to use premises without large upfront costs.

Solution to Lack of Social Media Personnel: Invest in training current staff through a cost-effective cascade training programme — where previously trained individuals provide training to others. This builds internal skills at low cost.

Solution to Inadequate Funds for Equipment: Access government-supported financing through MASLOC, NBSSI RLF, or BAF. These funds were specifically established to help SMEs acquire the operational resources and equipment they need to grow.

(Source: CoDEUCC Textbook — Unit 4, Session 6, Resource Mobilisation p.130; Unit 6, Session 4, Institutional Support pp.181–184)
Part D: Type of Entrepreneur — Owner of Woarabeba Shito [1 mark]
The owner of Woarabeba Shito is best classified as a Novice Entrepreneur — she had no prior business ownership experience but identified a market gap based on personal experience (her mother's illness and her own ulcer) and founded her first independent business.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 1 — Types of Entrepreneurs, p.5)
Part E: Market Coverage Strategy of Woarabeba Shito [2 marks]
Woarabeba Shito is applying a Concentrated Marketing (Niche Marketing) strategy. This involves developing a marketing mix and directing all marketing efforts and resources to appeal to a single market segment — in this case, health-conscious consumers (vegans, ulcer patients, meat-free households, etc.) who are underserved by the existing spicy pepper sauce market. Concentrated marketing is deemed ideal for small new businesses competing against larger competitors.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 5, Session 4 — Target Marketing, p.148)
Part F: Outline Three Strategies Adopted by Woarabeba Shito to Stay Competitive [3 marks]
Concentrated/Niche Marketing: By focusing specifically on health-conscious and medically restricted consumers, Woarabeba Shito carves a niche that mainstream competitors overlook, creating a defensible market position.
Market Penetration: The business uses door-to-door delivery services, social media platforms, and YouTube/TV shows to intensify its reach and increase sales in existing markets.
Product Development: Plans are in place to diversify into closely related products such as dry stew, beef sauce, chicken shito, red pepper sauce, green pepper sauce, and chilli sauce — extending the product line to meet the needs of different target segments.
(Source: CoDEUCC Textbook, Unit 5, Session 3 — Marketing Strategy, pp.143–144; Woarabeba Shito case study)
2023C — SECTION C: ANSWER ONLY ONE QUESTION
SECTION C — QUESTION ONE
Part A: Examine Five Forces in the Business Environment and How They Present Both Threats and Opportunities for Entrepreneurship [10 marks]
The business environment is very instrumental in creating opportunities as well as presenting threats to business survival and growth. The five environmental forces are:
Economic Environment
Covers factors such as population, interest rates, and inflation. Opportunity: A growing population increases the market's capacity for new organizations and creates more demand for products and services. Threat: High interest rates make borrowing expensive, increasing production costs; economic recessions reduce consumer spending, lowering demand for goods and services.
Technological Environment
The rate of technological development affects communication, transportation, and production. Opportunity: New technologies create demand for technologically advanced products (e.g., mobile phones, internet services), opening new markets for entrepreneurs. Threat: Rapid technological change can render existing products or processes obsolete, forcing costly upgrades.
Political Environment
The political climate in a country affects entrepreneurship. Opportunity: Free market economies with democratic governments and freedom of choice ensure efficient allocation of resources and create opportunities for entrepreneurs. Threat: An unstable or corrupt government discourages entrepreneurship as owners cannot be certain they will be allowed to access rewards from their efforts.
Legal Environment
Legal structures govern how people do business and their relationships with stakeholders. Opportunity: A sound legal system protects both business owners and consumers, minimizes distortions to the free market, and encourages entrepreneurs to respond to opportunities. Threat: A poor legal system breeds corruption, distorts the free market, and prevents efficient allocation of resources.
Competitive Environment
Refers to the level of competition in the industry, ease of market entry, availability of substitutes, and the relative power of suppliers and buyers. Opportunity: Monopolistic competition allows entrepreneurs to differentiate their products and enjoy some degree of monopoly power. Threat: Significant entry barriers (large capital requirements, economies of scale, patents) can deter new ventures; close substitutes limit the prices entrepreneurs can charge, eroding profit margins.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 4 — Entrepreneurship and the Environment, pp.17–19)
Part B(i): What is Franchising? [2 marks]
Franchising is a business agreement in which a company (the franchisor) grants others (the franchisees) the right and license (the franchise) to sell a product or service and possibly to use the business system developed by the company. It is a system of distribution in which a series of individually owned businesses operate as if they were part of a large chain. In most instances, the franchisee is granted an exclusive right to distribute the franchisor's goods/services in a specific geographical area.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 6 — Franchising, p.61)
Part B(ii): State and Explain Three Types of Franchising [6 marks]
(See full answer in 2020A Q4b — Trade Name, Product Distribution, and Pure Franchising. Summary:)
Trade Name Franchising: The franchisee buys the right to become identified with the franchisor's trade name (e.g., Budget Rent-a-Car) without distributing particular products exclusively under that name.
Product Distribution Franchising: The franchisee is licensed to sell specific products under the manufacturer's brand name and trademark via a selective distribution network — commonly used for automobiles (Toyota), soft drinks (Coca-Cola), and gasoline (Shell).
Pure (Business Format) Franchising: Provides the franchisee with a complete business format including a license for a trade name, the products/services, physical plant, methods of operation, marketing strategy, quality control process, and communication systems. Common in fast-food restaurants and car rental agencies.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 6 — Franchising, pp.61–62)
Part B(iii): Give Any Two Examples of Franchised Businesses in Ghana [2 marks]
Shell Ghana / Total Ghana: These are product distribution franchises operating filling stations across Ghana under their respective international brand names.
Coca-Cola Ghana / Pepsi: Beverage companies that license their brand names and formulas to bottlers and distributors in Ghana, operating under a product distribution franchising model.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 6 — Franchising, p.62)
SECTION C — QUESTION TWO (Entrepreneurial Process & New Venture Assessment)
Part A: What is a Business Opportunity? [2 marks]
A business opportunity is a gap in the market that presents the possibility of doing something new, different, and better from the perspective of buyers, thereby creating the chance to create new value. It is identified when an entrepreneur recognizes an unmet need or an improvement that can be offered to potential customers. The opportunity is a core component of the entrepreneurial process — it is what the entrepreneur evaluates and pursues in building a new venture.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 3 — The Entrepreneurial Process, p.13)
Part B: With the Aid of a Diagram, Explain the Entrepreneurial Process [8 marks]
(See full detailed answer in 2022 Q1(a), pp.13–14. The four components are the Entrepreneur, Opportunity, Organisation, and Resources — depicted in Wickham's 2007 diagram. Summary:)
[ ENTREPRENEURIAL PROCESS DIAGRAM: Opportunity ↑ | Resources ← ENTREPRENEUR → Organisation ]
Entrepreneur: Identifies, evaluates, and analyses opportunities; attracts and manages resources; leads and directs the organisation.
Opportunity: A gap in the market — something new, different, and better from the buyer's perspective that creates new value.
Organisation: A system devised to coordinate the activities of people and the use of resources in the venture.
Resources: The entrepreneur brings together financial, physical, human, technological, and organisational resources to build the venture and pursue the opportunity.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 3 — The Entrepreneurial Process, pp.13–14)
Part C: Mention Four Feasibility Criteria to Assess the Viability of a New Business Idea [10 marks]
(See also 2021 Q4A for related content. The Feasibility Criteria Approach — Baty, 1984 — provides the following four of the key criteria:)
Is it Proprietary?
The product does not have to be patented, but it should be sufficiently proprietary to permit a long head start against competitors and a period of extraordinary profits early in the venture to offset start-up costs. Differentiation is key.
Does the Approach Have Potential for Very High Margins?
This is almost a necessity for a fledgling company. Gross margins are one thing the financial community understands. Without adequate margins, funding can be very difficult to secure.
Is the Potential Market Large?
In determining potential market, entrepreneurs must look three to five years into the future. A large and growing market justifies the investment and provides room for early mistakes. A shrinking market means less room for error.
Is the Time to Market and Breakeven Realistic?
In most cases, the faster the better. The entire venture plan is tied to this answer and an error here can spell trouble later on. Development costs and calendar times must be realistic and tied to a month-by-month schedule.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 3 — New Business Assessment, pp.46–47)
