2023 EXAMINATION (EXAM CODE 01) — SECTION B

QUESTION 1 (COMPULSORY) — Case Study: KOFI-TECH [20 Marks]

Case Summary: KOFI-TECH is a well-established mid-sized manufacturing company specializing in automotive components. Its founder/CEO Mr. Adutwum contemplates retirement. A group of external investors led by Nana Boa Tene (a seasoned manufacturing executive) proposes to acquire KOFI-TECH — combining his industry expertise with the existing management's in-depth knowledge of the company.

Part (i): What Type of Entrepreneur is Nana Boa Tene? [2 marks]
Nana Boa Tene is a Serial Entrepreneur. He has primarily engaged in founding and managing startups and is now moving into acquiring an existing business. A serial entrepreneur is one who starts a new business after having already started and exited a previous business venture, treating entrepreneurship as a profession rather than a one-time event.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 1 — Types of Entrepreneurs, p.4–5)
Part (ii): How Does Nana Boa Tene's Background Align with KOFI-TECH's Industry and Growth Potential? [4 marks]
Nana Boa Tene's background in operations, supply chain management, and business development aligns with KOFI-TECH's needs in the following ways:
Operational Expertise: His experience in operations and supply chain management directly complements KOFI-TECH's manufacturing operations. This expertise can help streamline production processes, reduce costs, and improve efficiency in the automotive components business.
Business Development: As an entrepreneur, Nana Boa Tene envisions synergy between his industry expertise and the existing management's in-depth knowledge of KOFI-TECH. His entrepreneurial experience in identifying and exploiting opportunities aligns with the company's need to explore potential opportunities for growth.
Entrepreneurial Resources: According to the resource-based perspective, competitive advantage is created when firms possess valuable, rare, imperfectly imitable, and non-substitutable resources. Nana Boa Tene brings strategic human resources — his industry knowledge and network — that could be used to unlock untapped avenues for growth.
Leadership Transition: His wealth of experience in managing businesses positions him well to navigate the complexities of a leadership transition while maintaining KOFI-TECH's two-decade track record of delivering high-quality products.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 1 — Attributes of a Strategic Resource, pp.101–103; Unit 1, Session 1 — Types of Entrepreneurs, pp.4–5)
Part (iii): What Type of Business Acquisition Has Transpired? [2 marks]
The business acquisition option that has transpired is a Buy-in Management Buy-out (BIMBO). This is because it combines an outside investor (Nana Boa Tene and his group of external investors) with the existing inside management team of KOFI-TECH. BIMBO combines outside and inside management in the purchase of a firm — the belief being that the risk of buying in from outside can be minimized if the existing management are also involved.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 4 — Opportunities for Buying Existing Business, p.54)
Part (iv): How Might the Divergence on the Necessity of a Formal Business Plan Impact KOFI-TECH's Strategic Direction? [2 marks]
The divergence — where Nana Boa Tene insists on a formal business plan while the existing management deems it unnecessary given historical success — could impact KOFI-TECH's strategic direction in two significant ways:
Lack of Strategic Focus: Without a formal business plan, KOFI-TECH may lack clear direction on where it is going and how it will get there. The business plan describes the direction the firm is going in, what its goals are, and how it is going to achieve them. Without this, new opportunities may be missed and strategic decisions may be inconsistent.
Financing and Investment Challenges: A formal business plan serves as an important tool in helping to obtain financing. Without it, KOFI-TECH may struggle to attract future investment or financing for expansion, even though the existing management is confident in their historical track record.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 3, Session 1 — The Meaning of the Business Plan, pp.73–74)
Part (v): Advise KOFI-TECH Management on the Two Importance of a Business Plan [4 marks]
The management of KOFI-TECH should be advised that a formal business plan is essential for the following reasons:
Determining and Guiding Viability
The business plan helps determine the viability of the venture in a designated market. For KOFI-TECH, which is now under new leadership and exploring new growth opportunities, a business plan will help assess whether proposed growth strategies are feasible. It provides guidance to the entrepreneur in organizing planning activities, ensuring that resources are allocated effectively toward stated goals.
Providing a Tool for Ongoing Review and Control
A well-written business plan provides broad parameters upon which progress toward goals can be assessed and control decisions made. As Nana Boa Tene recognizes, a business plan is an indispensable tool to guide future operations and capitalize on emerging opportunities. As KOFI-TECH enters a new leadership phase, the plan will serve as a live, strategic and tactical planning focus for responding to changes in the environment.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 3, Session 1 — The Importance of the Business Plan, pp.73–74)
Part (vi): Evaluate Two Potential Benefits and Two Risks of Nana Boa Tene's Proposal to Acquire KOFI-TECH [6 marks]
Potential Benefits:
Buying Market Share and Immediate Turnover: KOFI-TECH has a successful track record spanning over two decades. Acquiring it gives Nana Boa Tene immediate access to an established customer base, market share in automotive components, and an ongoing revenue stream — avoiding the slow build-up of a new venture.
Synergy of Insider Knowledge and External Expertise: The BIMBO structure allows the existing management's in-depth knowledge of KOFI-TECH to complement Nana Boa Tene's industry expertise. This mirrors the advantage of insider knowledge — a benefit unique to acquisitions involving existing management.
Potential Risks:
Risk of Cultural Clash — 'Not All My Own Work': Nana Boa Tene's entrepreneurial experience in founding and managing startups may clash with the established culture of KOFI-TECH's management team. The existing management already resists change (as shown by their reluctance to adopt a business plan). This divergence in approach — between tradition and innovation — could destabilize the management team and undermine the transition.
Risk of Buying Possible Hidden Liabilities: Even if only assets are purchased, liabilities can still be attached. Furthermore, without a formal business plan in place, Nana Boa Tene may not have a complete picture of KOFI-TECH's true financial position. The existing management's confidence based on historical triumphs may mask emerging strategic risks.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 5 — Importance of Buying an Existing Business, pp.57–59; Unit 2, Session 4, p.54)
QUESTION 2
Part A: Explain the Term 'Promotion' in the Marketing Mix [2 marks]
Promotion is one of the four key elements of the marketing mix (the four Ps: Product, Price, Place, Promotion). Promotion aims to inform and persuade consumers about a firm's product or service. An organization's total marketing communications programme is referred to as its promotional mix — the sum of all the methods it uses in pursuit of its advertising and marketing objectives. There are personal, impersonal, and implied methods of communicating with the marketplace.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 5, Session 6 — Place and Promotion Strategies, p.159)
Part B: Briefly Discuss Three Personal and Three Impersonal Methods of Promotion Available to Small Enterprises [12 marks]
Personal Methods of Promotion:
Direct Selling
Direct selling refers to face-to-face interviews with customers, usually on their premises, with the objective of taking an order. It is most common in firms that sell to other businesses (manufacturers, distributors, or services) and can also be used for selling directly to consumers. It is widely used in financial services and home products such as window and security systems.
Telephone Selling
The telephone can be used to make appointments, answer enquiries, or complete the entire sales process by taking an order directly. For a small firm with limited resources, the telephone has become an indispensable aspect of selling because it is cost-effective and can reach many customers quickly.
Exhibitions and Trade Shows
National trade shows and exhibitions cover every significant trade or industry. They provide an opportunity for small firms to display their products directly to large numbers of potential buyers, generate leads, and build brand awareness. Examples include INDUTEC and AITEC in Accra.
Impersonal Methods of Promotion:
Media Advertisements
Includes press, radio, or television advertising. Small firms can also use directories and trade listings — permanent references that outline products or services offered, which can be consulted by potential buyers. Examples include 'Yellow Pages' and the Ghana Business Directory. This form is widely used by owner-managers.
Direct Mail
Direct mail is highly targeted, allowing small firms to direct promotional messages very specifically to narrow or fragmented markets. It can be tested on a limited basis before full rollout, and results can be quantified by tracking response rates. This makes it a cost-efficient option for small businesses.
Leaflets and Brochures
Many small firms produce leaflets or brochures describing their products or services. Using desktop publishing tools, most small firms can now produce more literature about themselves at relatively modest cost, enabling them to communicate their offerings widely without a large marketing budget.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 5, Session 6 — Place and Promotion Strategies, pp.159–162)
Part C: Explain with Relevant Examples an Implied Communication [2 marks]
Implied communications refer to marketing messages conveyed by the overall image and behaviour of a small firm — not through deliberate advertising, but through the pattern of its activities as perceived by customers. As Stokes (1995) points out, an impression of a small firm and its products or services is given by its letterhead, the signage on a shop or delivery van, the answering of the telephone, the style of leaflets and advertisements, the tone of letters, the decor of premises, and the way customers are served.
Example: A well-maintained delivery vehicle with a clean, professional logo communicates reliability and quality to potential customers who may not have seen the firm's advertisements — this is an implied communication.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 5, Session 6 — Place and Promotion Strategies, p.162)
Part D: State and Explain the Two Main Pricing Strategies Available for Small Enterprises [4 marks]
(See also 2020 Q2c and 2023B Q2D for related answers)
Market Pricing
Small businesses seek to price according to what the market will bear — asking what is the maximum price a customer is prepared to pay. While market pricing makes sense in theory, practical problems include fixed prices in some markets, the need for price consistency, and the fact that competitors condition the market's reaction to what is an acceptable price.
Cost-Plus Pricing
Cost-plus pricing involves deciding prices by calculating costs and adding what is judged to be an adequate element of profit. This is perhaps the most popular method used in small firms. In practice, small firm pricing is more likely to be a compromise between what the business needs to cover its costs and what the customer expects and competitors are charging.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 5, Session 5 — Product and Price Strategies, pp.154–155)
QUESTION 3: Explain Five Drawbacks and Five Benefits Associated with Entrepreneurship in Ghana [20 marks]

Model Answer:

Five Benefits (Advantages) of Entrepreneurship in Ghana:

Develop New Markets

Entrepreneurs are resourceful and creative. Under the modern concept of marketing, they can create customers or buyers where none existed before. This makes entrepreneurs different from ordinary businessmen who only perform traditional management functions. In Ghana, entrepreneurs continuously develop new market segments for local and regional goods.

Discover New Sources of Materials

Entrepreneurs are never satisfied with traditional or existing sources of materials. Due to their innovative nature, they persist on discovering new sources of raw materials to improve their enterprises. Those who can develop new sources enjoy a comparative advantage in terms of supply, cost, and quality.

Mobilize Capital Resources

Entrepreneurs are the organizers and coordinators of major factors of production — land, labour, and capital. They have initiative and self-confidence in accumulating and mobilizing capital resources for new business or business expansion, thereby driving economic activity.

Introduce New Technologies, Industries, and Products

Entrepreneurs take advantage of business opportunities and transform them into profits by introducing something new or different. Such entrepreneurial spirit greatly contributes to the modernization of the Ghanaian economy. New technologies and products introduced by entrepreneurs satisfy human needs in more convenient and pleasant ways.

Create Employment

The biggest employer is the private business sector. Millions of jobs are provided by factories, service industries, agricultural enterprises, and entrepreneurial businesses. In Ghana, companies like MELCOM, Woolworths, Ernest Chemist, and many SMEs are major job creators. More jobs generate more incomes, which stimulates demand for goods and services, further driving production and employment.

Five Drawbacks (Disadvantages) of Entrepreneurship in Ghana:

Uncertainty of Income

Opening and running a business provides no guarantee that an entrepreneur will earn enough money to survive. Some start-ups barely earn enough to provide an adequate income. Starting a business means giving up the security of a regular paycheck — a significant deterrent for many Ghanaians who depend on stable employment.

Unpredictable Work Schedule

The work schedule of an entrepreneur is never predictable. An emergency can arise at any moment, requiring late hours and intense effort. This is compounded by the fact that failure means total financial ruin, adding significant stress to an already demanding workload.

Long Hours of Work

Business start-ups often demand long hours and hard work. Because entrepreneurs must do everything themselves, owners experience intense, draining workdays. Many start down the path of entrepreneurship thinking they will own the business, only to discover that the business owns them.

Complete Responsibility

Owning a business requires making decisions on issues about which the entrepreneur may not be fully knowledgeable. When there is no one to consult, pressure quickly builds. Entrepreneurs in Ghana may also face the challenge of working with employees who lack experience.

Discouragement from Obstacles

Launching a business requires dedication, discipline, and tenacity. Entrepreneurs will run into obstacles along the way, and some may appear insurmountable. In Ghana, challenges such as inadequate infrastructure, difficulty accessing finance, and unstable economic conditions can quickly discourage new entrepreneurs.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 5 — Advantages and Disadvantages of Entrepreneurship, pp.21–23)

QUESTION 4

Part A: Identify and Explain Five Factors to Consider When Choosing Sources of Finance for Small Business [10 marks]
(See detailed answer under 2022 Q3B — Five Factors for Choice of Finance, pp.168–169)
A business needs to assess the different types of finance based on the following five criteria:
Amount of Money Required: Large amounts are not available through some sources while others lack flexibility for smaller amounts. The scale of financing needed is a key determinant of which source is appropriate.
How Quickly the Money is Needed: The urgency affects which sources are available and at what cost. Taking more time to source finance generally results in cheaper rates.
The Cheapest Option Available: The cost is measured by the interest charged on borrowed funds. The cheapest form of money for a business comes from its own trading profits, followed by retained earnings, then external sources.
The Amount of Risk Involved: Higher-risk ventures may not attract external financing without guarantees. Lenders assess the probability of the venture generating enough profit to repay the loan.
The Length of Time Finance is Needed: Whether for a long-term or short-term need should determine the type of finance used. Long-term needs are better matched with long-term sources (equity, debentures); short-term needs with overdrafts or trade credit.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 6, Session 1 — Factors that Influence the Choice of Finance, pp.168–169)
Part B: Explain Five Factors Considered in Assessing Creditworthiness [10 marks]
Creditworthiness is very important when it comes to securing short-term sources of finance. The following five factors are considered when assessing it:
Character: The reputation, honesty, and reliability of the borrower must be considered. A borrower with a track record of fulfilling obligations inspires confidence in lenders and increases the likelihood of obtaining credit.
Capacity: The business sense of the borrower, their ability to pay, their level of experience, and their business history. A borrower who demonstrates an ability to generate revenue and manage a business effectively is seen as having the capacity to service and repay the debt.
Circumstances: The general business circumstances in the industry and the broader economy. Lenders assess whether the economic environment is conducive to the borrower's business succeeding — for instance, whether the industry is growing or in decline.
Insurance Cover: The extent of the cover of insurable risks taken out by the borrower. Adequate insurance coverage protects the lender's interest by ensuring that losses from unexpected events (such as fire or theft) do not prevent the borrower from repaying the loan.
Guarantees: The lender may require the borrower to use assets to guarantee the loan. Collateral such as land, buildings, or equipment provides security for the lender in case the borrower defaults on repayment.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 6, Session 3 — Short Term Business Finance, p.177)
