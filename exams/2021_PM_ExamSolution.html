2021 Project Management Exam Solutions

2021 EXAMINATION — SECTION B

Question 1

Part a.. Using relevant examples, explain the four main classifications of project delay types. [(10 marks)]
Critical vs Non-Critical Delays: A critical delay extends the overall project duration, leading to extended overhead, liquidated damages, idle labour, and cost escalation. A non-critical delay does not extend total project duration but causes individual activities to complete later than scheduled. Example: Delay in foundation work on a hospital extends the project (critical); delay in landscaping does not extend completion (non-critical).
Excusable vs Non-Excusable Delays: An excusable delay is one the project team has no control over (force majeure, natural disasters, political unrest, client-caused delays) — the project manager may be entitled to time extension or compensation. A non-excusable delay results from the project manager's own failures (delayed procurement, poor scheduling) — the organization bears all cost consequences. Example: A flood delaying construction (excusable); the contractor failing to mobilize on time (non-excusable).
Concurrent Delays: Occurs when more than one delay event affects multiple activities simultaneously or independently. Not all concurrent delays entitle the manager to extension — it is the causes, not the delays themselves, that must overlap. Example: Equipment failure and material shortage occurring simultaneously on a road project.
Compensable vs Non-Compensable Delays: A compensable delay entitles the project manager to both time extension and cost compensation (all compensable delays fall under excusable). A non-compensable delay means the project manager is solely at fault and receives no compensation. Example: Client-requested design changes mid-project (compensable); poor project manager scheduling (non-compensable).
(Source: Unit 1, Session 6 — Types of Delays in Projects, pp.23–24)
Part b.. Examine five causes of delays in projects. [(10 marks)]
Design Errors: Errors in project design lead to wrong application of techniques, additional work, scope revision, and cost overruns. Example: A structural design error in a bridge requiring demolition and full rework.
Scope Change: Any change to the initial project scope during execution requires a review of budget, schedule, and quality plans. Causes include wrong initial scope definition, inherent risks, and sudden funding changes. Example: A client requesting additional floors on a building mid-construction.
Inappropriate Procurement and Faulty Contractual Management: Ambiguous contracts and poor selection of contractors lead to disputes, arbitration, and long negotiations causing delays. Example: An unclear road construction contract generating work-change-order disputes that stall construction for months.
Complexity: Large, complex projects are prone to delays from inflation, material price changes, exchange rate fluctuations, and omissions in estimation. Example: A major dam project delayed by international material price spikes and complex subcontractor dependencies.
Subcontractor and Consultant Delays: Inability of subcontractors or consultants to complete their contracts on time. These delays are difficult to anticipate or overcome. Example: A specialized MEP subcontractor on a hospital project failing to complete electrical installations by the agreed date.
(Source: Unit 1, Session 6 — Causes of Project Delays, pp.25–27)

Question 2

Part a.. Analyse the processes involved in project procurement management. [(12 marks)]
Procurement Planning: Identifying which project needs are best met externally — considering whether to procure, how, what, how much, and when. Determines contract type (fixed cost, cost reimbursement, or unit price) and develops the procurement strategy.
Solicitation Planning: Implements the procurement management plan and ends with sending solicitation documents — Request for Proposal (RFP), Request for Quotes (RFQ), and invitations to tender — to prospective suppliers.
Solicitation: Obtaining bids and proposals from prospective sellers. Bidder conferences are organized to ensure all sellers have a clear, common understanding of procurement requirements. Proposals describing sellers' ability and willingness to supply are prepared.
Source Selection: Using standard criteria to evaluate all bids/proposals. Proposals are separated into technical and commercial sections. After scoring, ranking, and negotiation, a contract is signed with the highest-ranked supplier.
Contract Administration: Ensures the supplier's performance meets contractual requirements. Covers project plan execution, performance reporting, quality control, change control, and payment systems throughout the contract period.
Contract Closeout: All procurements are formally closed — checking work is done satisfactorily, making final payments, completing cost records, and preparing a final report including lessons learned from all parties.
(Source: Unit 2, Session 5 — Project Procurement Management, pp.45–47)
Part b.. Examine any four main risks that occur in project procurement process. [(8 marks)]
Demand Fluctuation: Demand can be higher or lower than projected due to seasonality, taxation, price, and product availability. This affects project cost, schedule, and scope. Mitigation: use financial products such as options and futures, and continuously update demand information.
Vague Price Information: Prices of materials and services are unstable — purchasing at different times incurs different costs, affecting inventory holding and total project cost. Mitigation: develop flexible buyer-supplier relationships and adopt financial hedging strategies.
Unreliable Yield: Supply yield is uncertain due to limited supplier production capability or inability to meet product requirements. Mitigation: supplier diversification (using multiple suppliers) or collaboration with the supplier to improve reliability.
Uncertain Lead Time: If lead time uncertainty cannot be controlled, it increases total cost and reduces service levels. Mitigation: determine optimal ordering time and use backup supply channels to ensure continuity.
(Source: Unit 2, Session 6 — Procurement Risk Management, pp.49–51)

Question 3

Part a.. Examine two importance and challenges each of project communication management [(8 marks)]
Two Importances:
Relay of Information: Managing communications ensures appropriate information reaches the right people through the right media. It facilitates information flow within and outside the project organization and keeps stakeholders updated with progress reports.
Clarity of Tasks: Good communication skills ensure all project teams have their tasks clearly communicated, giving them a clear understanding of the creative skills expected to undertake the project successfully.
Two Challenges:
Poor Communication During Adversity: When unexpected issues or changes arise, the absence of a dynamic formal communication plan creates gaps. The formal and informal communication plans must complement each other to prevent this.
Language and Cultural Barriers: In global projects, different word meanings, lack of a common language, and varying cultural communication styles create barriers that the project manager must learn to handle.
(Source: Unit 4, Session 4 — Project Communication Management, pp.104–105)
Part b.. Explain the activities involved in the project integration management process [(12 marks)]
Develop Project Charter: Formally authorizes the existence of the project and provides the project manager authority to apply organizational resources. Establishes the partnership between the performing and requesting organizations.
Develop Project Management Plan: Defines, prepares, and coordinates all subsidiary plans, integrating them into a comprehensive PMP that describes how the project will be executed, monitored, controlled, and closed.
Direct and Manage Project Work: Leading and performing the work defined in the PMP and implementing approved changes to achieve project objectives. The project manager manages team, resources, communications, risks, and unplanned activities.
Monitor and Control Project Work: Tracking, reviewing, and reporting project progress against performance objectives. Involves collecting and distributing performance information and determining corrective or preventive actions.
Perform Integrated Change Control: Reviewing all change requests and approving or rejecting changes to deliverables, project documents, and the PMP. This allows documented changes to be reflected in an integrated approach while reducing project risk.
Close Project or Phase: Finalizing all activities to formally complete the project or phase. The project manager confirms all work is complete, goals are met, and then releases resources and transfers deliverables.
(Source: Unit 5, Sessions 1–3 — Project Integration Management Activities, pp.119–132)

Part b.. Explain four importance of time management in projects. [(10 marks)]
Assurance of Project Success: Time management provides assurance of project success — at least in terms of meeting the scheduled date. Time is an indicator of project success; projects going beyond the scheduled time are viewed as failures.
Results in Effectiveness and Efficiency: When time is managed, activities are performed within the stipulated timeframe. Time as a resource is well utilised by the team, enhancing productivity and overall performance.
Reduces Stress: Project-related stress develops when time management is ignored — when work is not progressing with unnecessary delays. Proper task identification, work flow design, and timing allocation reduces this stress significantly.
Results in Quality Output: Project time management ensures work is done based on the time scheduled. When activities follow the schedule, quality is more likely to meet the set standard since nothing is done haphazardly under pressure.
(Source: Unit 3, Session 4 — Importance of Time Management, pp.71–72)
