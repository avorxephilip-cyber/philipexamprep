2024 Project Management Exam Solutions

2024 EXAMINATION — SECTION B

Question 1

A.. Define a project. [(4 marks)] Model Answer: A project is defined as a temporary planned complex, one-time, customer-focused endeavor undertaken to create a unique outcome with a limited budget, schedule, and resources. It can broadly be defined as: (i) a temporary planned endeavor to create a product; (ii) a temporary planned endeavor to create a service or a capability to perform a service; (iii) a temporary planned endeavor to create an improvement in existing product or service lines; or (iv) a result, such as an outcome or document (e.g., a research project that develops new knowledge). 📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 1 — What is a Project?, p.3 B.. Analyse the statement "a project is temporary in nature". [(6 marks)] Model Answer: The statement is fundamentally correct — all projects have a specific start and end point. The temporary nature means project activities must be commenced and completed within a specified period. However, 'temporary' does NOT mean: (i) the duration of every project is short — some projects span many years; or (ii) the product or outcome created by the project is automatically short-lived — a building constructed through a project may stand for decades. What is temporary is the project's engagement and team formation. Projects end when: objectives are fully achieved; when the project is terminated because objectives will not or cannot be met; when the need for the project no longer exists; or when sponsors withdraw financial support. This distinguishes projects from ongoing operations, which are repetitive and continuous. 📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 1 — Nature of a Project, p.3 C.. Examine five characteristics of a project. [(10 marks)] Model Answer: Objective to Achieve: Every project is an ad hoc endeavor with a specific goal to be achieved. Schedule to Closure: Every project has a temporal life cycle with a specific time to accomplish each activity. Complexity: Every project has its level of complexity, hence requiring specific existing technology to achieve project objectives. Resource Needs: Every project requires specific labour, personnel, equipment, materials, and budget to ensure successful closure. Risk and Safety Controls: Every project must have risk management and safety controls to comply with both international and local standards depending on the location of the project and stakeholder requirements. 📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 1 — Characteristics of a Project, pp.4–5

Question 2

A.. Using relevant examples, distinguish between direct and indirect cost related to a project. [(2 marks)] Model Answer: Direct Cost: Costs clearly assigned to the specific project activity that generated them. Examples: all labour costs of the project workers; all material costs directly incorporated into the deliverable. Indirect Cost: Costs linked to overhead and general administration — not directly attributable to any single project activity. Examples (overhead): utilities, taxes, insurance, depreciation on equipment, health and retirement benefits. Examples (administration): advertising, shipping, secretarial support, sales commissions. 📖 Source: Unit 2, Session 2 — Direct and Indirect Costs, p.33 B.. State and explain four sources of project cost. [(6 marks)] Model Answer: Labour Cost: Costs associated with hiring and paying all classifications of workers (skilled, semi-skilled, labourers) involved in developing the project, including salary, hourly/daily rates, and pension or health benefits. Materials Cost: The cost of specific requirements and supplies the project team needs to complete tasks as scheduled. Varies by scope, quality standards, and risk thresholds. Service Cost: The cost of work done by consultants, experts, designers, and sub-contractors to ensure the successful completion of the project. Equipment and Facilities: Costs arising from rental of any equipment or office facilities needed to facilitate the completion of the project. 📖 Source: Unit 2, Session 1 — Sources of Project Cost, pp.31–32 C.. Question 3

A.. Define project stakeholders using relevant examples. [(2 marks)] Model Answer: Project stakeholders are individuals and organizations who may have an interest in and may be actively involved in the project. They are typically project team members, project managers, executives, project sponsors, customers, and users. Examples: Owners, Sponsors, Customers, End Users, Contractors, Suppliers, Project Managers, Project Teams, Society and Citizens, Government agents. 📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 3 — Project Stakeholders, p.12; Unit 5, Session 4, p.135 B.. Examine four importance of stakeholder management in project management. [(8 marks)] Model Answer: Gains Valuable Support: Strong stakeholder support gives the project a valuable sponsor who can help make it a success. Properly managed stakeholders provide moral and organizational support throughout the project lifecycle. Gathers Complete Requirements: Gathering all stakeholder requirements upfront leads to project success — you deliver what the customer really needs. A negatively agitated stakeholder can make it very tough to succeed. Maintains Stakeholder Engagement: Keeping stakeholders well-informed of progress keeps them engaged throughout the entire project lifecycle, and they serve as a check that the project stays aligned with its initial goals. Reduces and Uncovers Risk: The more you engage stakeholders, the more risks are uncovered and reduced. Stakeholders may raise issues and concerns about project needs and constraints that the project team may have overlooked. 📖 Source: Unit 5, Session 4 — Importance of Stakeholder Management, pp.135–136 C.. Analyse four techniques involved in identifying project stakeholders. [(10 marks)] Model Answer: Power and Interest Grid: Groups stakeholders by their level of power (authority) and level of interest in the project or its outcome. High power/high interest stakeholders are managed closely; high power/low interest are kept satisfied; low power/high interest are kept informed; low power/low interest are simply monitored. Power and Influence Grid: Groups stakeholders by their level of power (authority) and level of active influence on the project or its outcome. Useful when power and influence levels are not correlated. Influence and Impact Grid: Groups stakeholders based on their active involvement (influence) in the project and their ability to effect changes to project planning or execution (impact). Salience Model (Power, Urgency, and Legitimacy): Uses three parameters — power, urgency of needs, and legitimacy of involvement — to classify project stakeholders. This identifies the most critical stakeholders requiring immediate management attention. 📖 Source: Unit 5, Session 5 — Techniques for Identifying Project Stakeholders, pp.139–140

Question 4

A.. Examine five internal sources of project risk. [(10 marks)] Model Answer: Top Management Not Recognizing the Activity as a Project: Without proper recognition, the project lacks adequate governance, dedicated resources, and organizational support, leading to mismanagement and failure. Too Many Projects Running Simultaneously: Running multiple projects at once causes resource overcommitment and divided management attention, increasing the probability of delays and budget overruns on all active projects. Impossible Schedule Commitments: Setting unrealistic timelines without adequate feasibility assessment creates a risk of inevitable delay from the outset of the project. No Functional Input into the Planning Phase: Excluding functional departments from the planning phase produces plans that do not reflect operational realities, leading to design omissions and rework during execution. Conflicting Project Priorities: Multiple competing organizational priorities and budget allocations may starve individual projects of timely funding and management attention, causing stagnation. 📖 Source: Unit 6, Session 1 — Internal Sources of Project Risk, p.151 B.. Analyse five risk response strategies used by project managers in responding to risk related to projects they are managing. [(10 marks)] Model Answer: Avoidance/Prevention: Looks for alternative project activities that eliminate potential negative consequences. Involves detailed planning, alternative approaches, training, regular inspections, and procedural changes. Used when risk probability and impact are high. Transfer: Project risks are transferred to firms or individuals with greater capacity to manage them — e.g., client, contractor, subcontractor. Insurance policies are used when the risk is beyond the project manager's control. Risk Reduction/Mitigation: Partially or fully changes exposed areas to reduce the likelihood or impact of risk occurrence. Includes contingency planning, quality assurance, contract terms and conditions, and crisis management plans. Acceptance/Retention: Used when risks cannot be transferred or avoided — particularly for political/natural disaster risks. The project manager conducts a cost-benefit analysis and accepts the risk when other solutions are uneconomical. Risk Sharing: The project's common risks are divided proportionally among two or more entities or project partners. Both profits and potential losses of the project are shared between the parties. Example: Cost of a major infrastructure project shared between the Government of Ghana and the European Union. 📖 Source: Unit 6, Session 4 — Risk Response Strategies, pp.161–162
