2019 Project Management Exam Solutions

2019 EXAMINATION — SECTION B

Question 1

Part a. Explain the key responsibility of the project manager in defining project scope [2 Marks]
The project manager's key responsibility is to ensure that only the required work — and nothing more — will be performed, and that each deliverable can be completed within the allotted time and budget. Scope management means defining and controlling what is included in the project and what is not.
📖 Source: Unit 3, Session 1 — Introduction to Project Scope Management, p.55
Part b. Explain four (4) common problems associated with project scope management [8 Marks]
  • Ambiguity: Leads to unnecessary work and confusion. The scope must be clearly defined and precise to avoid misinterpretations.
  • Incomplete Definition: Leads to schedule slips and cost overruns. The scope must be complete so that all deliverables are identified.
  • Transience (Scope Creep): Unstable scopes lead to late deliveries. Once finalized, the scope should remain unaltered.
  • Uncollaborated Scope: A scope not prepared with stakeholders causes misinterpretations in requirements and design.
📖 Source: Unit 3, Session 1 — Common Problems in Project Scope Management, p.56
Part c. Explain any five (5) importance of project scope management [10 marks]
  • Shared Understanding: Ensures stakeholders and team members understand and agree on how goals will be met.
  • Verification of Completed Work: Sets up procedures for verifying and approving work, ensuring formal acceptance.
  • Controls Scope Bloat: Prevents challenges with unruly requirements lists and unauthorized additions.
  • Defines Exclusions: Clearly states what is NOT part of the project to avoid execution stage errors.
  • Enables Accurate Estimation: Essential for determining accurate project cost and time baselines.
📖 Source: Unit 3, Session 1 — Importance of Project Scope Management, pp.55–56

Question 2

Part a. State the four (4) main types of project termination [4 Marks]
  • Termination by Starvation: Occurs when resources are withdrawn or unavailable; the project may be reactivated later.
  • Termination by Extinction: The project stops because goals are met (success) or objectives cannot be met (failure).
  • Termination by Addition: A successful project is converted into a new permanent operating unit of the organization.
  • Termination by Integration: Project resources and team members are reintegrated into existing functional departments.
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 5 — Project Life Cycle (Project Termination), p.20
Part b. Using relevant examples, explain the three (3) main processes used to ensure effective project costs management [6 marks]
  • Project Cost Estimating: Approximating costs for activities. Example: Separately costing foundation and roofing for a house.
  • Project Cost Budgeting: Aggregating estimates to establish an authorized baseline. Example: Combining civil and MEP estimates into one budget.
  • Project Cost Control: Monitoring status to manage changes to the baseline. Example: Tracking monthly spending on a road project.
📖 Source: Unit 2, Session 3 — Project Cost Management Process, pp.37–39
Part c. Explain any five (5) sources of long-term project funding [10 marks]
  • Sale and Leaseback: Selling assets and leasing them back to release capital for investment.
  • Loan Capital (Debentures): Secured loans where interest is paid before dividends to shareholders.
  • Business Angels: Private investors providing funds in exchange for an equity stake.
  • Venture Capital: Significant funds for high-risk ventures in exchange for equity and strategy roles.
  • Share Capital: Raised through ordinary or preference shareholders who receive dividends.
📖 Source: Unit 2, Session 4 — Sources of Long-Term Project Funding, pp.42–44
