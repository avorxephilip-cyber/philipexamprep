2019 Project Management Exam Solutions

2019 EXAMINATION — SECTION B

Question 1

Part a. Explain the key responsibility of the project manager in defining project scope [2 Marks]
The project manager's key responsibility is to ensure that only the required work — and nothing more — will be performed, and that each deliverable can be completed within the allotted time and budget. Scope management means defining and controlling what is included in the project and what is not.
(Source: Unit 3, Session 1 — Introduction to Project Scope Management, p.55)
Part b. Explain four (4) common problems associated with project scope management [8 Marks]
Ambiguity: Ambiguity in scope leads to unnecessary work and confusion. The scope must be clearly defined and precise to avoid misinterpretations among team members and stakeholders.
Incomplete Definition: Incomplete scopes lead to schedule slips and cost overruns. The scope must be complete and accurate from the outset so that all deliverables are fully identified.
Transience (Scope Creep): Transient or unstable scopes lead to scope creep — the primary cause of late deliveries and never-ending projects. Once finalized, the scope document must remain unaltered for the duration of the project.
Uncollaborated Scope: A scope not collaboratively prepared causes misinterpretations in requirements and design. The scope document must be shared with and agreed upon by all stakeholders at every step.
(Source: Unit 3, Session 1 — Common Problems in Project Scope Management, p.56)
Part c. Explain any five (5) importance of project scope management [10 marks]
Shared Understanding: Scope management ensures that stakeholders and team members alike understand the project scope and agree on how goals will be met.
Verification of Completed Work: Scope management sets up procedures for how work that is completed will be verified and approved, ensuring quality and formal acceptance of deliverables.
Controls Scope Bloat: Scope management helps avoid challenges with bloating scope and unruly requirements lists — it checks what gets added or removed during execution.
Defines Exclusions: Scope management defines what is NOT part of the project, ensuring excluded items are not included during the execution stage.
Enables Accurate Estimation: Without defining project scope, the cost and time that the project will take cannot be estimated. Scope changes directly affect cost and disturb the schedule, causing losses.
(Source: Unit 3, Session 1 — Importance of Project Scope Management, pp.55–56)

Question 2

Part a. State the four (4) main types of project termination [4 Marks]
Termination by Starvation: Occurs when needed resources are lacking, inadequate, or unavailable. The project may be kept on file for reactivation when the economic situation improves.
Termination by Extinction: The project is stopped due to either successful completion (goals met, deliverables handed to users) or unsuccessful completion (objectives cannot be met). Final budget is audited and assets dispersed.
Termination by Addition: A very successful project is not disbanded; instead, the project organization is converted into a new permanent operating unit of the parent organization.
Termination by Integration: Project resources and team members are reintegrated into the organization's existing functional departments upon project conclusion.
(Source: Unit 1, Session 5 — Project Life Cycle (Project Termination), p.20)
Part b. Using relevant examples, explain the three (3) main processes used to ensure effective project costs management [6 marks]
Project Cost Estimating: Approximating the cost to complete project activities by breaking the project into phases or deliverables. Resources considered include labour, materials, equipment, services, and facilities. Example: Estimating a house by separately costing land acquisition, foundation, building, roofing — rather than a single bulk estimate.
Project Cost Budgeting: Aggregating the estimated costs of individual activities or work packages to establish an authorized cost baseline against which expenditures are measured. Example: Combining cost estimates for a hospital project's civil works, MEP systems, and equipment into one approved budget.
Project Cost Control: Monitoring the status of the project to update costs and manage changes to the cost baseline. Any deviation in schedule, quality, or scope will have a cost impact. Example: Tracking monthly spending on a road project against the baseline to catch overruns before they escalate.
(Source: Unit 2, Session 3 — Project Cost Management Process, pp.37–39)
Part c. Explain any five (5) sources of long-term project funding [10 marks]
Sale and Leaseback: Assets are sold to a financial institution and leased back for a term. This releases capital tied in assets for investment, offset by rental payments and loss of capital growth if assets appreciate.
Loan Capital (Debentures): Loans secured by a fixed or floating charge against a company's assets. Debenture holders receive their interest before any dividend is paid to shareholders, and are preferential creditors if the business fails.
Business Angels: Private investors who invest directly in exchange for an equity stake and sometimes a board seat. They invest lesser amounts than venture capitalists and bring useful entrepreneurial knowledge.
Venture Capital: Venture capitalists offer substantial funds to companies considered too risky for standard institutions, in exchange for equity and a strategic non-executive role. Their prime aim is to increase share value for future profit.
Share Capital: Raised through shareholders who receive dividends and potential capital gains. Ordinary shareholders have voting rights but bear risk; preference shareholders receive guaranteed dividends with limited voting rights.
(Source: Unit 2, Session 4 — Sources of Long-Term Project Funding, pp.42–44)
