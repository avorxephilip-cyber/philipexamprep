2025 EXAMINATION (EXAM CODE 01) — SECTION B

QUESTION ONE (COMPULSORY) — Case Study: Don Teeti, Catfish Agribusiness [20 Marks]

Case Summary: Don Teeti grew up near Kpong, Eastern Region, fascinated by fish. After his first degree, he launched a small catfish farm in 2016 on his family's land. His first harvest was poor. In 2018, he attended an agribusiness workshop (Ministry of Food and Agriculture/NGO), gained knowledge in record-keeping, market research, pricing, and customer relationships. He improved operations, secured relationships with hotels, restaurants, and supermarkets. By 2020 he obtained a bank loan, expanded with concrete ponds and recirculation systems. He diversified by selling fingerlings and offering training. During COVID-19, he pivoted to digital marketing (WhatsApp/Facebook) for home deliveries. Today he employs 6 full-time workers. His dream is to expand into fish processing and export across West Africa.

Part a: What is the Sociological Approach that Inspired Don Teeti to Explore Entrepreneurship? Justify Your Answer. [2 marks]
The sociological approach that inspired Don Teeti is Positive Push. Positive push factors include career paths that offer entrepreneurial opportunities or education that gives the individual the appropriate knowledge and opportunity. Don Teeti was inspired by rising opportunities in Ghana's aquaculture sector and shifting consumer preferences from tilapia to catfish — environmental and educational 'push' factors that motivated him to venture into fish farming. His attendance at an agribusiness workshop further exemplifies how education and skill acquisition pushed him further into entrepreneurship.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 3 — The Entrepreneurial Process (Sociological Approach), p.15)
Part b: What Type of Entrepreneur is Teeti? Outline Six Entrepreneurial Attributes/Traits About Teeti. Justify Your Answer. [3+2 marks]
Type: Teeti is best classified as a Novice Entrepreneur who demonstrates characteristics of a Portfolio Entrepreneur. He started with a single catfish farm (novice), but later diversified his income into selling fingerlings and offering training programmes — owning multiple income-generating ventures simultaneously.
Six Entrepreneurial Attributes/Traits Demonstrated by Teeti:
1. Need for Achievement (n Ach): Teeti set goals and pursued them despite early failures and skepticism from family and friends.
2. Risk-Taking Propensity: He started a catfish farm with little capital and no technical experience, demonstrating willingness to accept financial and personal risk.
3. Internal Locus of Control: He believed his effort could change his outcomes — continuing despite poor first harvests rather than attributing failure to bad luck.
4. Resilience and Persistence: He persisted through family skepticism, initial poor harvests, and COVID-19 disruptions without giving up his venture.
5. Adaptability: During COVID-19, he quickly pivoted to digital marketing and home deliveries, demonstrating flexibility in the face of unexpected challenges.
6. Innovativeness: He diversified his farm into a knowledge hub offering training to other farmers, and is exploring sustainable aquaculture practices with research institutions.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 1 — Types of Entrepreneurs, pp.4–5; Session 3 — Entrepreneurial Process (Psychological Approach), pp.14–15)
Part d: Describe Two Main Challenges Teeti Faced During His Initial Catfish Harvest [2 marks]
1. Technical/Operational Challenges: With little business or technical experience, Teeti struggled with water quality, disease control, and feeding practices. These operational gaps led to his first harvest being underwhelming, with many fish underweight and yielding poor market prices.
2. Financial Challenges: Teeti started with minimal capital — combining only personal savings with a small loan from his uncle. The poor first harvest delivered little revenue, leaving him financially constrained and unable to invest adequately in better practices or equipment.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 5 — Disadvantages of Entrepreneurship, pp.22–23)
Part e: Describe Two Instances in the Case Where Teeti Made Good Use of Available Resources [2 marks]
1. Human Resources — Knowledge and Network: In 2018, Teeti attended an agribusiness training workshop where he gained critical knowledge in record-keeping, market research, pricing, and customer relationship management. He also built a network of fellow young farmers with whom he regularly exchanged ideas and solutions. This is a direct application of human resources — the knowledge, training, and experience of the entrepreneur — to improve the business.
2. Physical and Technological Resources: By 2020, Teeti used a small agribusiness loan to expand with additional concrete ponds and install a basic water recirculation system. He also diversified by selling fingerlings to other farmers and offering training, transforming his farm into a local knowledge hub. He leveraged existing family land (physical resource) and digital tools (WhatsApp/Facebook) as technological resources to pivot during COVID-19.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 1 — Types of Resources (Human, Physical, Technological), pp.102–104)
Part f: Explain the Economic Impact of Teeti's Catfish Business to Society as Business Grew [2 marks]
Economic Impact: As Teeti's catfish business grew, it created employment — he now employs six full-time workers and several part-time helpers. This contributes directly to reducing unemployment, increasing household incomes, and generating consumer spending in the local economy. His plan to expand into fish processing and West African exports would further multiply the economic impact through increased foreign exchange earnings and job creation — demonstrating the multiplier effect of small business growth on the economy.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 5 — Advantages of Entrepreneurship (Create Employment), pp.21–22; Unit 1, Session 6 — Role of Small Businesses, pp.26–27)
Part g: Explain the Economic Impact of Teeti Catfish Business to Society [2 marks]
(See Part f above — this question addresses the same area. In summary: Teeti's business contributes to employment creation, income generation, food security, and local economic development in the Eastern Region. It also introduces technical knowledge in aquaculture, contributing to sustainable development and creating economic linkages between farming, processing, and distribution sectors.)
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Session 6 — Role of Small Businesses in Economic Development, pp.26–27)
Part h: Proposed a Vision Statement for Don Teeti's Business [2 marks]

Proposed Vision Statement: "To become the leading sustainable catfish agribusiness in West Africa, delivering premium-quality fish products from farm to table while empowering local farming communities."

This vision is consistent with Teeti's stated dream of expanding into fish processing, exporting across West Africa, and collaborating with research institutions for sustainable aquaculture.

(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 3, Session 1 — The Business Plan, p.73 (direction and goals))
Part i: Describe Three Main Entrepreneurial Lessons You Learnt from Reading This Case [3 marks]
1. Perseverance Overcomes Initial Failure: Despite a poor first harvest, family skepticism, and financial constraints, Teeti continued to improve his operations. The entrepreneurial lesson is that discouragement and early failure are normal parts of the entrepreneurial journey — what matters is the willingness to learn, adapt, and persist. Launching a business requires dedication, discipline, and tenacity.
2. Knowledge and Training are Critical Resources: Teeti's turning point came after attending an agribusiness workshop where he gained practical skills in record-keeping, market research, and customer relationships. The lesson is that entrepreneurs should never stop learning — investing in education and skills development is one of the most valuable forms of resource acquisition for a growing business.
3. Adaptability is Essential for Survival: When COVID-19 disrupted his business, Teeti quickly pivoted to digital marketing and home deliveries — opening a new revenue stream rather than closing down. The lesson is that successful entrepreneurs must be willing to innovate and adapt their business model when external conditions change. Entrepreneurs who control rather than merely own resources, and who diversify their strategies, are better positioned for long-term success.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 1, Sessions 3 & 5 — Entrepreneurial Process & Advantages/Disadvantages of Entrepreneurship, pp.13–23; Unit 4, Session 6 — Resource Mobilisation, pp.130–132)
QUESTION 2 — Case: Ama, Eco-Friendly Fashion Business
Case Summary: Ama is a 25-year-old Ghanaian graduate who has completed National Service and is passionate about sustainable fashion. She wants to start an eco-friendly clothing business from locally sourced fabrics. She is unsure how to generate solid business ideas and is debating whether to start her own brand from scratch or buy into an existing clothing business.
Part a: Identify and Explain Two Methods Ama Can Use to Generate Innovative and Practical Business Ideas [4 marks]
1. Brainstorming: Brainstorming is the method of bringing people together and asking them to suggest alternatives to a problem or to generate new product ideas. Ama could assemble a group of friends, fashion enthusiasts, and sustainability advocates for a brainstorming session on eco-friendly clothing ideas. The basic rules are: no criticism allowed, state any ideas regardless of how extreme, approach ideas as belonging to the group and build on others' ideas, and generate — not evaluate — ideas. This would help Ama rapidly generate diverse product and business model ideas.
2. Focus Groups: Focus groups involve a moderator leading a group of 8 to 14 participants through an open, in-depth discussion about a new product area. The group is stimulated by comments from other members in creatively conceptualizing and developing a new product idea to fulfil a market need. Ama could assemble eco-conscious young Ghanaians, textile workers, and fashion buyers in a focus group to understand what sustainable clothing options they desire — directly feeding into her business concept.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 2 — Finding a Sound Idea for a Business (Brainstorming, Focus Groups), pp.35–37)
Part b: Explain Four Advantages Ama Might Enjoy by Starting Her Own Fashion Business from Scratch Instead of Buying an Existing Business [8 marks]
1. Creation and Control: By starting from scratch, Ama has freedom of choice over what the business does, how it operates, and what its values are. She has maximum control over the affairs of the business, and external influences can be minimized. For a passion-driven entrepreneur building an eco-friendly brand, this creative and operational freedom is essential to building an authentic brand identity.
2. Clean Sheet — No Inherited Problems: A new business starts with no backlog of problems. Although it would create its own challenges, at least they would be new ones and not inherited from the past. Ama avoids buying potential ill will from a previous owner, inheriting unsuitable employees, or acquiring hidden liabilities attached to an existing business.
3. Satisfaction and Personal Achievement: There is inherent satisfaction of success due to the skill and effort of the owners. Starting from scratch means that every milestone is a product of Ama's own talent and hard work. Buying an existing business could diminish the entrepreneur's sense of achievement and motivation to make it succeed.
4. Match Between Founder and Enterprise: New business founders can ensure that their individual strengths are well used and weaknesses minimized by choosing a business well matched to their own qualities and experiences. Ama's passion for sustainable fashion and knowledge of locally sourced fabrics means a new business can be purpose-built around her specific expertise and values — unlike an existing business that may not align with her vision.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 1 — Advantages of Starting Your Own Business, pp.31–32; Unit 2, Session 5 — Disadvantages of Buying Existing Business, pp.58–59)
Part c: Identify and Explain Two Disadvantages Ama Should Be Aware of When Starting from Scratch, and Recommend Specific Strategies to Overcome These Challenges [8 marks]
Disadvantage 1 — Unproven Idea and High Failure Rate: A new business idea might be creative, but would it work in the market? It is generally admitted that the failure rate of business start-ups is high, with less than 50 percent said to survive the first five years. Ama's eco-friendly fashion concept, while innovative, has not been tested in the Ghanaian market. Recommended Strategy: Ama should conduct a thorough feasibility study before launching — specifically a market feasibility analysis to determine whether there is adequate demand for eco-friendly clothing at prices that cover her costs. She should also consider a small product launch or pilot run to test the concept before full-scale investment.
Disadvantage 2 — Difficult to Finance / No Track Record: More often than not, banks and other lenders are keener to lend money to proven ideas than to new concepts. Ama has no business history, no collateral, and no credit track record, making it very difficult to access commercial financing. Recommended Strategy: Ama should explore alternative financing options available to young entrepreneurs in Ghana, such as the VCTF (Venture Capital Trust Fund), NBSSI's Revolving Loan Fund, or MASLOC — all of which provide accessible financing to first-time entrepreneurs. She could also start small using personal savings and reinvest profits before scaling up, reducing reliance on external financing at the outset.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 2, Session 1 — Disadvantages of Starting Your Own Business, pp.32–33; Unit 6, Session 4 — Government and Institutional Support, pp.181–184)
QUESTION 3 — Case: Kwaku, Palm Oil Producer (Marketing Research)
Case Summary: Kwaku is a small palm oil producer in the Central Region who has been selling to local markets. He now wants to expand to supermarkets in Accra and explore possible export markets in West Africa. He has no prior experience with marketing research, customer preferences, pricing, or competition in these new markets.
Part a: Describe the Key Stages Kwaku Should Follow When Conducting Marketing Research to Assess His New Market Opportunities [8 marks]
(See detailed answer under 2024 Q3A above — the same four-stage marketing research process applies to Kwaku's situation, with palm oil-specific examples.)
In brief, the four key stages Kwaku should follow are:
Stage 1 — Define the Problem: Kwaku should clearly define his research objective: 'What are the preferences, pricing expectations, and competitive dynamics of supermarket buyers in Accra and potential West African export markets for palm oil?'
Stage 2 — Collect Data: Use internal records (current sales volumes, customer feedback) and external sources. Primary data via personal interviews with Accra supermarket buyers and West African importers; secondary data from trade associations, GhanaXport, and government agricultural statistics.
Stage 3 — Analyse and Interpret: Kwaku should analyse the collected data to identify pricing benchmarks, customer preferences (e.g., bottled vs. bulk), certification requirements for export, and key competitors in the target markets.
Stage 4 — Draw Conclusions and Act: Based on the research findings, Kwaku decides on his market entry strategy — such as partnering with a distributor in Accra, repackaging his oil for supermarket shelves, and applying for GEPA (Ghana Export Promotion Authority) certification for export.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 5, Session 2 — Marketing Research, pp.139–142)
Part b: Discuss, Using Kwaku's Business Examples, How Marketing Research Can Add Value to His Business Decision-Making and Help Reduce Risks [4 marks]
1. Reduces Risk in Market Entry: Marketing research enables Kwaku to make informed decisions before investing in new markets. For example, by researching Accra supermarket requirements, Kwaku avoids the costly mistake of producing bulk oil when supermarkets require bottled, labelled products. Information reduces risk — the cost of conducting market research is repaid through better decision-making.
2. Supports Competitive Positioning: By gathering competitor information (who supplies palm oil to Accra supermarkets, their prices and quality), Kwaku can position his product competitively. Marketing research enables businesses to overcome barriers to ineffective decision-making and ensures his value proposition — locally sourced, high-quality palm oil — is clearly differentiated from competitors.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 4, Session 3 — Importance of Business Information, p.116; Unit 5, Session 2 — Marketing Research, pp.139–141)
Part c: Identify Two Common Marketing Research Challenges that Ghanaian Entrepreneurs like Kwaku Typically Face, and Explain How Each Challenge Can Be Effectively Overcome [8 marks]
Challenge 1 — Inadequate Funds for Market Research: Small business owners like Kwaku in rural areas often lack the financial resources to commission professional market research. The direct cost of commissioned research can be prohibitive for small enterprises. How to Overcome: Kwaku can focus on collecting primary data through low-cost methods — telephone interviews (cheap and effective), direct visits to supermarket buyers, and personal interviews at market centres. He can also access free secondary data from government agencies (Ghana Statistical Service, Ministry of Food and Agriculture, GEPA) and trade associations, which provide market reports at little or no cost.
Challenge 2 — Lack of Market Research Knowledge and Skills: Like many small entrepreneurs, Kwaku has no prior experience with marketing research, customer preferences, pricing, or competition. Without skills in data collection, analysis, and interpretation, research efforts can yield misleading results. How to Overcome: Kwaku should seek business development support from institutions like the NBSSI, GRATIS, or the Ministry of Food and Agriculture — which offer training in market research and business management to SMEs in Ghana. He could also partner with a business school student or consultant through a low-cost internship or outreach programme.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 5, Session 2 — Marketing Research, pp.139–142; Unit 6, Session 4 — Government and Institutional Support, pp.181–184)
QUESTION 4 — The Business Plan
Part a: Explain the Four Key Sections that Must Be Included in a Business Plan [8 marks]
The business plan is a written outline of the entrepreneur's proposed venture, its operational and financial details, its marketing opportunities and strategy, and its managers' skills and abilities. The four key sections are:
1. Executive Summary: Written after the total plan is prepared, the executive summary should be about two pages in length. It should be concise and summarize all relevant points of the proposed venture — explaining the nature of the venture, financing needed, market potential, and why the venture will succeed. A well-developed executive summary establishes a favourable first impression and succeeds in securing financing.
2. Industry Analysis and Description of Venture: This section puts the new venture in its proper context. It discusses the industry outlook (future trends, past objectives), an analysis of each major competitor, the profile of customers, market segmentation, and the target market. The description of venture includes a statement of general business goals and immediate objectives, the location and size of the business, personnel requirements, the background of the entrepreneur(s), and any patents or trademarks.
3. Marketing Plan: This section covers the firm's pricing strategy, distribution channels, promotion methods, product forecasts, and sales controls. The marketing plan describes how the entrepreneur intends to reach the target market, communicate value, and achieve sales goals. It is a critical component for demonstrating to investors that the entrepreneur understands their market.
4. Financial Plan: This section includes pro forma income statements, cash flow projections, a pro forma balance sheet, break-even analysis, and sources and applications of funds. This section gives potential investors and lenders a clear picture of the venture's financial viability — how much money is needed, how it will be used, and when the investment is expected to be recovered.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 3, Sessions 3–5 — Writing the Business Plan, pp.81–92)
Part b: Discuss Three Important Reasons Why Entrepreneurs Should Write a Business Plan [6 marks]
1. It Helps Determine the Viability of the Venture: The business plan helps determine the viability of the venture in a designated market. For a new or growing business, the discipline of writing a plan forces the entrepreneur to critically assess whether the business concept is realistic, whether the market is large enough, and whether the financial projections support a profitable operation.
2. It Serves as an Important Tool in Obtaining Financing: The business plan serves as an important tool in helping to obtain financing. Lenders and investors — whether banks, venture capitalists, or government institutions like the VCTF — require a comprehensive business plan before committing funds. A well-written plan demonstrates seriousness, professionalism, and the entrepreneur's understanding of the business.
3. It Provides Guidance and a Framework for Operations: The business plan is designed to guide the entrepreneur through the first year of operations. It provides guidance to the entrepreneur in organizing planning activities and developing a schedule to measure progress. Without it, employees will not understand the company's goals or how they are expected to perform.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 3, Session 1 — The Importance of the Business Plan, pp.73–74; Session 6 — Using and Implementing the Business Plan, pp.95–97)
Part c: Provide Three Practical Tips that Entrepreneurs Should Follow to Ensure that Once the Business Plan is Written, It is Effectively Used and Does Not Just Sit on the Shelf [6 marks]
1. Measure Plan Progress Regularly: The business plan should not end up in a drawer once financing has been obtained and the business launched. On a frequent basis (at the beginning of each month), the entrepreneur should check the profit and loss statement, cash flow projections, and information on inventory, production, quality, sales, and collections. This regular feedback allows key members of the organization to correct any major deviations from goals and objectives in time.
2. Update the Plan When Conditions Change: The most effective business plan can become out of date if conditions change. Environmental factors such as the economy, customers, new technology, or competition — and internal factors such as the loss or addition of key employees — can all change the direction of the business plan. The entrepreneur should be sensitive to changes in the company, industry, and market and determine what revisions are needed. A business plan should be a live, strategic and tactical planning focus.
3. Use Control Elements to Monitor Performance: The entrepreneur should implement control procedures — including inventory control (maximize customer service and capital reinvestment), production control (compare estimated costs to day-to-day operation costs), quality control (ensure products perform satisfactorily), sales control (monitor units, revenue, products sold, delivery performance), and disbursement control (review all bills). These controls ensure the plan remains operationally relevant and that the business stays on track toward its stated goals.
(Source: CoDEUCC Entrepreneurship Textbook, Unit 3, Session 6 — Using and Implementing the Business Plan, pp.95–97)
