SECTION B — Answer ANY TWO (2) Questions from this section
  Question 1
a.  Explain the key responsibility of the project manager in defining project scope  [2 Marks]
Model Answer:
The project manager's key responsibility is to ensure that only the required work — and nothing more — will be performed, and that each deliverable can be completed within the allotted time and budget. Scope management means defining and controlling what is included in the project and what is not.
📖 Source: Unit 3, Session 1 — Introduction to Project Scope Management, p.55
b.  Explain four (4) common problems associated with project scope management  [8 Marks]
Model Answer:
Ambiguity: Ambiguity in scope leads to unnecessary work and confusion. The scope must be clearly defined and precise to avoid misinterpretations among team members and stakeholders.
Incomplete Definition: Incomplete scopes lead to schedule slips and cost overruns. The scope must be complete and accurate from the outset so that all deliverables are fully identified.
Transience (Scope Creep): Transient or unstable scopes lead to scope creep — the primary cause of late deliveries and never-ending projects. Once finalized, the scope document must remain unaltered for the duration of the project.
Uncollaborated Scope: A scope not collaboratively prepared causes misinterpretations in requirements and design. The scope document must be shared with and agreed upon by all stakeholders at every step.
📖 Source: Unit 3, Session 1 — Common Problems in Project Scope Management, p.56
c.  Explain any five (5) importance of project scope management  [10 marks]
Model Answer:
Shared Understanding: Scope management ensures that stakeholders and team members alike understand the project scope and agree on how goals will be met.
Verification of Completed Work: Scope management sets up procedures for how work that is completed will be verified and approved, ensuring quality and formal acceptance of deliverables.
Controls Scope Bloat: Scope management helps avoid challenges with bloating scope and unruly requirements lists — it checks what gets added or removed during execution.
Defines Exclusions: Scope management defines what is NOT part of the project, ensuring excluded items are not included during the execution stage.
Enables Accurate Estimation: Without defining project scope, the cost and time that the project will take cannot be estimated. Scope changes directly affect cost and disturb the schedule, causing losses.
📖 Source: Unit 3, Session 1 — Importance of Project Scope Management, pp.55–56

  Question 2
a.  State the four (4) main types of project termination  [4 Marks]
Model Answer:
Termination by Starvation: Occurs when needed resources are lacking, inadequate, or unavailable. The project may be kept on file for reactivation when the economic situation improves.
Termination by Extinction: The project is stopped due to either successful completion (goals met, deliverables handed to users) or unsuccessful completion (objectives cannot be met). Final budget is audited and assets dispersed.
Termination by Addition: A very successful project is not disbanded; instead, the project organization is converted into a new permanent operating unit of the parent organization.
Termination by Integration: Project resources and team members are reintegrated into the organization's existing functional departments upon project conclusion.
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 5 — Project Life Cycle (Project Termination), p.20
b.  Using relevant examples, explain the three (3) main processes used to ensure effective project costs management  [6 marks]
Model Answer:
Project Cost Estimating: Approximating the cost to complete project activities by breaking the project into phases or deliverables. Resources considered include labour, materials, equipment, services, and facilities. Example: Estimating a house by separately costing land acquisition, foundation, building, roofing — rather than a single bulk estimate.
Project Cost Budgeting: Aggregating the estimated costs of individual activities or work packages to establish an authorized cost baseline against which expenditures are measured. Example: Combining cost estimates for a hospital project's civil works, MEP systems, and equipment into one approved budget.
Project Cost Control: Monitoring the status of the project to update costs and manage changes to the cost baseline. Any deviation in schedule, quality, or scope will have a cost impact. Example: Tracking monthly spending on a road project against the baseline to catch overruns before they escalate.
📖 Source: Unit 2, Session 3 — Project Cost Management Process, pp.37–39
c.  Explain any five (5) sources of long-term project funding  [10 marks]
Model Answer:
Sale and Leaseback: Assets are sold to a financial institution and leased back for a term. This releases capital tied in assets for investment, offset by rental payments and loss of capital growth if assets appreciate.
Loan Capital (Debentures): Loans secured by a fixed or floating charge against a company's assets. Debenture holders receive their interest before any dividend is paid to shareholders, and are preferential creditors if the business fails.
Business Angels: Private investors who invest directly in exchange for an equity stake and sometimes a board seat. They invest lesser amounts than venture capitalists and bring useful entrepreneurial knowledge.
Venture Capital: Venture capitalists offer substantial funds to companies considered too risky for standard institutions, in exchange for equity and a strategic non-executive role. Their prime aim is to increase share value for future profit.
Share Capital: Raised through shareholders who receive dividends and potential capital gains. Ordinary shareholders have voting rights but bear risk; preference shareholders receive guaranteed dividends with limited voting rights.
📖 Source: Unit 2, Session 4 — Sources of Long-Term Project Funding, pp.42–44

  QUESTION 3
a..  All projects are temporary in nature. Discuss  [[4 Marks]]
Model Answer:
This statement is fundamentally correct. A project is defined as a temporary planned endeavor — all project activities must be commenced and completed within a specific period (specific start and end point).
However, 'temporary' does NOT mean: (i) the project duration is always short — some projects span years; or (ii) the product or outcome created is temporary — a bridge built by a project may stand for a century. What is temporary is the project's engagement and team formation. Projects end when: objectives are fully achieved; objectives cannot or will not be met; the need for the project no longer exists; or sponsors withdraw financial support.
Therefore, the temporary nature of a project distinguishes it from ongoing operations, which are repetitive and continuous. Each project has a definite beginning and a definite end.
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 1 — Nature of a Project, p.3
b..  Explain any six (6) characteristics of a project  [[6 Marks]]
Model Answer:
Objective to Achieve: Every project is an ad hoc endeavor with a specific goal to be achieved.
Schedule to Closure: Every project has a temporal life cycle with a specific time to accomplish each activity in the project life cycle.
Complexity: Every project has its level of complexity, requiring specific existing technology to achieve the project's objectives.
Resource Needs: Every project requires specific labour, personnel, equipment, materials, and budget to ensure successful closure.
Organizational Structure: Every project has a management structure providing decisions on information controls, budget, scheduling, quality standards, and overall direction.
Risk and Safety Controls: Every project must have risk management and safety controls to comply with both international and local standards depending on the location and stakeholder requirements.
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 1 — Characteristics of a Project, pp.4–5
c..  With the aid of a diagram, explain the three (3) most important traditional constraints of project management  [[6 Marks]]
Model Answer:
The three most important traditional constraints are the Triple Constraints — Scope, Cost (Budget), and Schedule (Time):
Scope: Defines all the work that must be performed to deliver the project successfully. Expanding scope without adjusting time or cost will compromise quality and lead to project failure.
Cost (Budget): The approved budget allocated for the project. Cost overruns occur when scope expands or time is extended without corresponding budget increases.
Schedule (Time): The planned timeline for project completion. Time is the most visible indicator of project success — delays are immediately recognizable.
DIAGRAM: [Triangle — Scope (top vertex), Cost/Budget (bottom-left vertex), Schedule/Time (bottom-right vertex), Quality in the centre]
These constraints are interdependent: change in one affects the others. Modern project management also recognizes quality, resources, and risk as additional constraints.
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 2 — Constraints to Project Management, p.8

  QUESTION 4
a..  State two (2) importance of human resource planning in project management  [[4 Marks]]
Model Answer:
Identifies Roles and Responsibilities: HR planning identifies the specific roles and responsibilities required to achieve the aim of a project. This gives the project leader clarity on the calibre of people to recruit, making them task and goal-oriented.
Sets Skills Standards: HR planning sets the standard of skills and intellectual capacity needed in prospective project team members. It prevents mismatching of qualifications and guides the recruitment method.
📖 Source: Unit 4, Session 2 — Importance of Human Resource Planning, p.94
b..  Explain any four (4) functions performed by the project manager in managing and leading project teams  [[8 Marks]]
Model Answer:
Influencing the Team: The project manager influences human resource factors impacting the project — team environment, geographical locations, cultural issues, and internal/external politics — to maintain project performance.
Building Trust: The project manager builds trust by persuading team members to ask questions and seek guidance from each other, fostering familiarity and comfort essential for high team performance.
Performance Appraisals: The project manager uses appraisals to provide feedback to team members, allowing individual reflection and progressive improvement that benefits the entire team in reaching objectives.
Conflict Management: The project manager resolves inevitable conflicts arising from scarce resources, scheduling priorities, and personal work styles using techniques including: withdraw/avoid, smooth/accommodate, compromise/reconcile, force/direct, and collaborate/problem solve.
📖 Source: Unit 4, Session 3 — Manage Project Team, pp.100–101
c..  State the steps to consider in establishing the project risk context  [[8 Marks]]
Model Answer:
Step 1: Establish the organizational and project environment in which the risk assessment is taking place.
Step 2: Specify the main objectives and outcomes required for the project.
Step 3: Identify a set of success criteria against which the consequences of identified risks can be measured.
Step 4: Define a set of key elements for structuring the risk identification and assessment process.
Context inputs include: project execution strategy, project charter, cost and schedule assumptions, scope definitions, engineering designs, economic analyses, and other relevant project documentation.
The output is: a concise statement of project objectives, specific success criteria, objectives and scope for the risk assessment, and key elements for structuring the risk identification process.
📖 Source: Unit 6, Session 2 — Establish the Risk Context, p.153

  Question 1
a..  Using relevant examples, explain the four main classifications of project delay types.  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Critical vs Non-Critical Delays: A critical delay extends the overall project duration, leading to extended overhead, liquidated damages, idle labour, and cost escalation. A non-critical delay does not extend total project duration but causes individual activities to complete later than scheduled. Example: Delay in foundation work on a hospital extends the project (critical); delay in landscaping does not extend completion (non-critical).
Excusable vs Non-Excusable Delays: An excusable delay is one the project team has no control over (force majeure, natural disasters, political unrest, client-caused delays) — the project manager may be entitled to time extension or compensation. A non-excusable delay results from the project manager's own failures (delayed procurement, poor scheduling) — the organization bears all cost consequences. Example: A flood delaying construction (excusable); the contractor failing to mobilize on time (non-excusable).
Concurrent Delays: Occurs when more than one delay event affects multiple activities simultaneously or independently. Not all concurrent delays entitle the manager to extension — it is the causes, not the delays themselves, that must overlap. Example: Equipment failure and material shortage occurring simultaneously on a road project.
Compensable vs Non-Compensable Delays: A compensable delay entitles the project manager to both time extension and cost compensation (all compensable delays fall under excusable). A non-compensable delay means the project manager is solely at fault and receives no compensation. Example: Client-requested design changes mid-project (compensable); poor project manager scheduling (non-compensable).
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 6 — Types of Delays in Projects, pp.23–24
b..  Examine five causes of delays in projects.  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Design Errors: Errors in project design lead to wrong application of techniques, additional work, scope revision, and cost overruns. Example: A structural design error in a bridge requiring demolition and full rework.
Scope Change: Any change to the initial project scope during execution requires a review of budget, schedule, and quality plans. Causes include wrong initial scope definition, inherent risks, and sudden funding changes. Example: A client requesting additional floors on a building mid-construction.
Inappropriate Procurement and Faulty Contractual Management: Ambiguous contracts and poor selection of contractors lead to disputes, arbitration, and long negotiations causing delays. Example: An unclear road construction contract generating work-change-order disputes that stall construction for months.
Complexity: Large, complex projects are prone to delays from inflation, material price changes, exchange rate fluctuations, and omissions in estimation. Example: A major dam project delayed by international material price spikes and complex subcontractor dependencies.
Subcontractor and Consultant Delays: Inability of subcontractors or consultants to complete their contracts on time. These delays are difficult to anticipate or overcome. Example: A specialized MEP subcontractor on a hospital project failing to complete electrical installations by the agreed date.
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 6 — Causes of Project Delays, pp.25–27

  Question 2
a..  Analyse the processes involved in project procurement management.  [(12 marks)]
Model Answer:
Procurement Planning: Identifying which project needs are best met externally — considering whether to procure, how, what, how much, and when. Determines contract type (fixed cost, cost reimbursement, or unit price) and develops the procurement strategy.
Solicitation Planning: Implements the procurement management plan and ends with sending solicitation documents — Request for Proposal (RFP), Request for Quotes (RFQ), and invitations to tender — to prospective suppliers.
Solicitation: Obtaining bids and proposals from prospective sellers. Bidder conferences are organized to ensure all sellers have a clear, common understanding of procurement requirements. Proposals describing sellers' ability and willingness to supply are prepared.
Source Selection: Using standard criteria to evaluate all bids/proposals. Proposals are separated into technical and commercial sections. After scoring, ranking, and negotiation, a contract is signed with the highest-ranked supplier.
Contract Administration: Ensures the supplier's performance meets contractual requirements. Covers project plan execution, performance reporting, quality control, change control, and payment systems throughout the contract period.
Contract Closeout: All procurements are formally closed — checking work is done satisfactorily, making final payments, completing cost records, and preparing a final report including lessons learned from all parties.
📖 Source: Unit 2, Session 5 — Project Procurement Management, pp.45–47
b..  Examine any four main risks that occur in project procurement process.  [(8 marks)]
Model Answer:
Demand Fluctuation: Demand can be higher or lower than projected due to seasonality, taxation, price, and product availability. This affects project cost, schedule, and scope. Mitigation: use financial products such as options and futures, and continuously update demand information.
Vague Price Information: Prices of materials and services are unstable — purchasing at different times incurs different costs, affecting inventory holding and total project cost. Mitigation: develop flexible buyer-supplier relationships and adopt financial hedging strategies.
Unreliable Yield: Supply yield is uncertain due to limited supplier production capability or inability to meet product requirements. Mitigation: supplier diversification (using multiple suppliers) or collaboration with the supplier to improve reliability.
Uncertain Lead Time: If lead time uncertainty cannot be controlled, it increases total cost and reduces service levels. Mitigation: determine optimal ordering time and use backup supply channels to ensure continuity.
📖 Source: Unit 2, Session 6 — Procurement Risk Management, pp.49–51

  Question 3
a..  Examine two importance and challenges each of project communication management  [(8 marks)]
Model Answer:
Two Importances:
Relay of Information: Managing communications ensures appropriate information reaches the right people through the right media. It facilitates information flow within and outside the project organization and keeps stakeholders updated with progress reports.
Clarity of Tasks: Good communication skills ensure all project teams have their tasks clearly communicated, giving them a clear understanding of the creative skills expected to undertake the project successfully.
Two Challenges:
Poor Communication During Adversity: When unexpected issues or changes arise, the absence of a dynamic formal communication plan creates gaps. The formal and informal communication plans must complement each other to prevent this.
Language and Cultural Barriers: In global projects, different word meanings, lack of a common language, and varying cultural communication styles create barriers that the project manager must learn to handle.
📖 Source: Unit 4, Session 4 — Project Communication Management, pp.104–105
b..  Explain the activities involved in the project integration management process  [(12 marks)]
Model Answer:
Develop Project Charter: Formally authorizes the existence of the project and provides the project manager authority to apply organizational resources. Establishes the partnership between the performing and requesting organizations.
Develop Project Management Plan: Defines, prepares, and coordinates all subsidiary plans, integrating them into a comprehensive PMP that describes how the project will be executed, monitored, controlled, and closed.
Direct and Manage Project Work: Leading and performing the work defined in the PMP and implementing approved changes to achieve project objectives. The project manager manages team, resources, communications, risks, and unplanned activities.
Monitor and Control Project Work: Tracking, reviewing, and reporting project progress against performance objectives. Involves collecting and distributing performance information and determining corrective or preventive actions.
Perform Integrated Change Control: Reviewing all change requests and approving or rejecting changes to deliverables, project documents, and the PMP. This allows documented changes to be reflected in an integrated approach while reducing project risk.
Close Project or Phase: Finalizing all activities to formally complete the project or phase. The project manager confirms all work is complete, goals are met, and then releases resources and transfers deliverables.
📖 Source: Unit 5, Sessions 1–3 — Project Integration Management Activities, pp.119–132

b..  Explain four importance of time management in projects.  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Assurance of Project Success: Time management provides assurance of project success — at least in terms of meeting the scheduled date. Time is an indicator of project success; projects going beyond the scheduled time are viewed as failures.
Results in Effectiveness and Efficiency: When time is managed, activities are performed within the stipulated timeframe. Time as a resource is well utilised by the team, enhancing productivity and overall performance.
Reduces Stress: Project-related stress develops when time management is ignored — when work is not progressing with unnecessary delays. Proper task identification, work flow design, and timing allocation reduces this stress significantly.
Results in Quality Output: Project time management ensures work is done based on the time scheduled. When activities follow the schedule, quality is more likely to meet the set standard since nothing is done haphazardly under pressure.
📖 Source: Unit 3, Session 4 — Importance of Time Management, pp.71–72

  Question 1
You have been appointed as the project manager for the next graduation ceremony of the University of Cape Coast.
Required:
A..  Explain the steps you would follow in managing this project.  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Identify Requirements/Needs: Define what the graduation ceremony requires — venue, logistics, catering, audiovisuals, printing of certificates, security, and guest management.
Address Stakeholder Needs: Identify and engage all stakeholders (UCC management, graduating students, parents, academic staff, caterers, security, media) and address their needs, concerns, and expectations in planning.
Set Up Communications: Establish and maintain effective communications among all active stakeholders, ensuring timely, accurate, and collaborative information sharing throughout.
Develop the Project Management Plan: Create detailed plans covering scope, schedule, budget, quality, risk, procurement, and HR — coordinated into one comprehensive PMP.
Execute the Plan: Implement the plans: procure services, deploy the project team, manage resources, monitor activities, and handle changes or issues as they arise.
Monitor and Control: Track progress against the plan — check that logistics are on schedule, costs are within budget, and quality targets are met. Initiate change requests where needed.
Close the Project: After the ceremony, release resources, settle contracts with suppliers, document lessons learned, and prepare a post-event review.
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 2 — Steps to Manage a Project, p.7
B..  Examine three constraints you are likely to encounter with the aid of a diagram and indicate how these constraints will affect the successful execution of the project.  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Scope: Defines all activities — venue setup, rehearsals, ceremony programme, certificates, reception, and media coverage. If additional elements are requested without adjusting time or budget, quality will be compromised and the ceremony may not be delivered as planned.
Cost (Budget): The ceremony must be delivered within UCC's approved budget. Uncontrolled spending or scope expansion without a budget increase could exhaust funds before the ceremony is complete.
Schedule (Time): The graduation date is fixed and cannot be moved. All procurement, certificate printing, rehearsals, and setup must be completed before that fixed date — any delay in one activity compresses the remaining tasks.
DIAGRAM: [Triangle — Scope (top), Cost/Budget (bottom-left), Schedule/Time (bottom-right), Quality at centre]
Impact: These constraints are interdependent — extending time increases cost; cutting cost reduces scope; expanding scope requires more time and budget. The project manager must continuously balance all three.
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 2 — Constraints to Project Management, p.8

  Question 2
A company has consulted to manage the launch of a new product. As the project manager for the event, you are required to address the following:
A..  Using relevant examples, examine how the following cost may be applicable to the project:  i. Direct cost  ii. Indirect cost  iii. Fixed cost  iv. Variable cost  v. Expedited cost  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Direct Cost: Costs clearly assigned to the specific project activity that generated them. Example: All labour costs of the launch event team and all materials (banners, brochures, demo units) used exclusively for the product launch.
Indirect Cost: Costs linked to overhead and general administration not exclusively attributable to the launch project. Example: Utilities, insurance, depreciation on shared equipment, secretarial support, and general advertising.
Fixed Cost: Costs that do not vary with usage throughout the project lifecycle. Example: Lease of the event venue at a fixed fee regardless of actual attendance numbers.
Variable Cost: Costs that increase or decrease based on actual activity levels. Example: Catering costs that vary with the number of attendees, or printed materials that vary with the guest list size.
Expedited Cost: Unplanned costs incurred to speed up project completion. Example: Paying overtime to event staff or hiring additional temporary workers to meet the launch deadline after falling behind schedule.
📖 Source: Unit 2, Session 2 — Nature of Project Cost, pp.33–34

  Question 3
The government of Ghana is putting up a three-storey building as part of its Agenda 111 projects. This project is expected to be completed within three years. You have been appointed as the project consultant.
Required:
A..  Assess four reasons why this project must be completed within the stipulated time framework provided.  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Time as an Indicator of Project Success: Project time management provides assurance of project success — missing the deadline is viewed as project failure. For a government project (Agenda 111), a missed deadline signals poor governance and budget inefficiency.
Financial Cost of Delays: A critical delay extends overhead costs, including idle labour, equipment costs, and material cost escalation due to inflation. Prolonging the project increases expenditure beyond the approved budget, requiring supplementary funding.
Service Delivery Obligation: The project aims to build hospitals to improve healthcare in each district. Every day of delay denies citizens access to critical healthcare infrastructure — a social obligation that makes timely delivery imperative.
Contractual and Legal Obligations: Construction contracts include liquidated damages clauses imposing financial penalties for every day of delay beyond the agreed completion date. Exceeding the timeline creates legal and financial liability for the government.
📖 Source: Unit 3, Session 4 — Importance of Time Management, pp.71–72; Unit 1, Session 6 — Project Delays, pp.23–24
B..  Explain the steps to be followed in developing a human resource management plan for this project.  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Step 1 — Identify and Document Roles and Responsibilities: Define all roles required to achieve the project goals (e.g., civil engineers, quantity surveyors, project coordinators, site supervisors, health facility designers). Document the responsibilities attached to each role.
Step 2 — Specify Skills Requirements: After determining roles, specify the conceptual, human, and technical skills and qualifications expected of each team member to effectively handle their responsibilities.
Step 3 — Define Reporting Relationships: Draft a project organizational structure showing the chain of command and communication flow between all teams. This enables clear reporting lines and prevents confusion on authority.
Step 4 — Create a Staffing Management Plan: Describe when and how project team members will be acquired, how long they will be needed, and plans for staff acquisition, release, training needs, and recognition/rewards systems.
📖 Source: Unit 4, Session 2 — Plan and Acquire Human Resource for Projects, pp.93–94

  Question 4 (Exam Code 01)
B..  Using relevant examples, distinguish between the following types of cost:  i. direct and indirect project costs  ii. Fixed and variable costs  iii. normal and expedited costs  iv. recurring and nonrecurring costs  [(2.5 marks each)]
Model Answer:
i. Direct vs Indirect Project Costs: Direct costs are clearly assigned to the specific project activity that generated them — e.g., direct labour wages of workers on the project, direct materials incorporated into the deliverable. Indirect costs are linked to overhead and general administration, not traceable to any single project activity — e.g., utilities, insurance, depreciation, advertising, and secretarial support.
ii. Fixed vs Variable Costs: Fixed costs do not vary with usage throughout the project lifecycle — e.g., leasing capital equipment or project hardware at a fixed monthly rate. Variable costs increase or decrease throughout the project lifecycle, subject to significant fluctuations — e.g., equipment usage costs that vary with the number of hours used.
iii. Normal vs Expedited Costs: Normal costs are incurred in the routine process of completing the project according to the original planned schedule agreed upon by all stakeholders. Expedited costs are unplanned costs incurred when steps are taken to speed up the project's completion — e.g., crashing activities through overtime, hiring additional temporary workers, or contracting external resources to regain lost time.
iv. Recurring vs Nonrecurring Costs: Recurring costs are continuous in nature throughout the project lifecycle — e.g., labour costs, material costs, logistics, and sales costs. Nonrecurring costs are charged only once at the beginning or closure of the project — e.g., preliminary marketing analysis, personnel training, and outplacement services.
📖 Source: Unit 2, Session 2 — Nature of Project Cost, pp.33–34

  Question 4
A..  Define project integration management.  [(2 marks)]
Model Answer:
Project Integration Management involves the processes and activities to identify, define, combine, unify, and coordinate the various processes and activities within the Project Management Process Groups. It includes making choices about resource allocation and managing the interdependencies among all project management knowledge areas.
📖 Source: Unit 5, Session 1 — Project Integration Management, p.119
B..  Explain the processes involved in project integration management.  [(12 marks)]
Model Answer:
Develop Project Charter: Formally authorizes the existence of the project and provides the project manager with authority to apply organizational resources to project activities.
Develop Project Management Plan: Defines, prepares, and coordinates all subsidiary plans and integrates them into a comprehensive PMP that describes how the project will be executed, monitored, controlled, and closed.
Direct and Manage Project Work: Leading and performing the work defined in the PMP and implementing approved changes to achieve project objectives. Manages team, resources, communications, and risks.
Monitor and Control Project Work: Tracking, reviewing, and reporting project progress against performance objectives defined in the PMP. Determines corrective or preventive actions when deviations occur.
Perform Integrated Change Control: Reviewing all change requests and approving or rejecting changes to deliverables, project documents, and the PMP. Reduces project risk by integrating documented changes.
Close Project or Phase: Finalizing all activities to formally complete the project or phase, releasing resources, and transferring deliverables to the customer.
📖 Source: Unit 5, Session 1 — Project Integration Management Process, pp.119–120
C..  State two importance of project integration management.  [(3 marks)]
Model Answer:
Initiates Project by Allocating Resources: Managing integration helps initiate the project by allocating resources to commence work. The project charter directs teams to execute the project plan and creates a formal record with senior management commitment.
Prevents Isolated Project Management: Integration management ensures projects are not managed in isolation — it accounts for how aspects of the project relate to each other and to other parts of the organization, ensuring holistic execution.

  Question 1
A..  Define a project.  [(4 marks)]
Model Answer:
A project is defined as a temporary planned complex, one-time, customer-focused endeavor undertaken to create a unique outcome with a limited budget, schedule, and resources. It can broadly be defined as: (i) a temporary planned endeavor to create a product; (ii) a temporary planned endeavor to create a service or a capability to perform a service; (iii) a temporary planned endeavor to create an improvement in existing product or service lines; or (iv) a result, such as an outcome or document (e.g., a research project that develops new knowledge).
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 1 — What is a Project?, p.3
B..  Analyse the statement "a project is temporary in nature".  [(6 marks)]
Model Answer:
The statement is fundamentally correct — all projects have a specific start and end point. The temporary nature means project activities must be commenced and completed within a specified period.
However, 'temporary' does NOT mean: (i) the duration of every project is short — some projects span many years; or (ii) the product or outcome created by the project is automatically short-lived — a building constructed through a project may stand for decades.
What is temporary is the project's engagement and team formation. Projects end when: objectives are fully achieved; when the project is terminated because objectives will not or cannot be met; when the need for the project no longer exists; or when sponsors withdraw financial support. This distinguishes projects from ongoing operations, which are repetitive and continuous.
📖 Source: Unit 1, Session 1 — Nature of a Project, p.3

Model Answer:

Schedule to Closure: Every project has a temporal life cycle with a specific time to accomplish each activity.
Complexity: Every project has its level of complexity, hence requiring specific existing technology to achieve project objectives.
Resource Needs: Every project requires specific labour, personnel, equipment, materials, and budget to ensure successful closure.
Risk and Safety Controls: Every project must have risk management and safety controls to comply with both international and local standards depending on the location of the project and stakeholder requirements.

C..  Analyse four techniques involved in identifying project stakeholders.  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Power and Interest Grid: Groups stakeholders by their level of power (authority) and level of interest in the project or its outcome.   Question 4
A..  Examine five internal sources of project risk.  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Top Management Not Recognizing the Activity as a Project: Without proper recognition, the project lacks adequate governance, dedicated resources, and organizational support, leading to mismanagement and failure.
Too Many Projects Running Simultaneously: Running multiple projects at once causes resource overcommitment and divided management attention, increasing the probability of delays and budget overruns on all active projects.
Impossible Schedule Commitments: Setting unrealistic timelines without adequate feasibility assessment creates a risk of inevitable delay from the outset of the project.
No Functional Input into the Planning Phase: Excluding functional departments from the planning phase produces plans that do not reflect operational realities, leading to design omissions and rework during execution.
Conflicting Project Priorities: Multiple competing organizational priorities and budget allocations may starve individual projects of timely funding and management attention, causing stagnation.
📖 Source: Unit 6, Session 1 — Internal Sources of Project Risk, p.151
B..  Analyse five risk response strategies used by project managers in responding to risk related to projects they are managing.  [(10 marks)]
Model Answer:
Avoidance/Prevention: Looks for alternative project activities that eliminate potential negative consequences. Involves detailed planning, alternative approaches, training, regular inspections, and procedural changes. Used when risk probability and impact are high.
Transfer: Project risks are transferred to firms or individuals with greater capacity to manage them — e.g., client, contractor, subcontractor. Insurance policies are used when the risk is beyond the project manager's control.
Risk Reduction/Mitigation: Partially or fully changes exposed areas to reduce the likelihood or impact of risk occurrence. Includes contingency planning, quality assurance, contract terms and conditions, and crisis management plans.
Acceptance/Retention: Used when risks cannot be transferred or avoided — particularly for political/natural disaster risks. The project manager conducts a cost-benefit analysis and accepts the risk when other solutions are uneconomical.
Risk Sharing: The project's common risks are divided proportionally among two or more entities or project partners. Both profits and potential losses of the project are shared between the parties. Example: Cost of a major infrastructure project shared between the Government of Ghana and the European Union.
📖 Source: Unit 6, Session 4 — Risk Response Strategies, pp.161–162
